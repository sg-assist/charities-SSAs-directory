*If you are reading this because someone near you is in crisis right now — you are doing the right thing by looking. This guide tells you exactly what to do, in order. You do not need to have all the answers. You just need the next step.*

---

## THE MOST IMPORTANT THING TO KNOW

**If there is immediate danger to life — call 995 (SCDF ambulance) or 999 (Police) right now.**

If the person is not in immediate physical danger but is in crisis, call the **IMH Crisis Helpline: 6389 2222** (24 hours, 7 days). A trained mental health professional will answer and guide you through what to do next.

---

## 1. IMMEDIATE SAFETY ASSESSMENT — IS THERE DANGER RIGHT NOW?

Before anything else, ask yourself two questions:

**Is the person physically hurt right now?** (Injury from self-harm, an overdose, a fall.) If yes — **call 995 immediately.**

**Do they have a plan and means to hurt themselves?** Ask directly: *"Are you thinking about ending your life? Do you have a plan?"* Asking does not plant the idea. It opens the door.

### Signs that require 995 or immediate A&E:
- Active self-harm with injury requiring medical treatment
- Overdose (known or suspected)
- Psychosis with severe agitation, confusion, or dangerous behaviour
- Person is unconscious or unresponsive
- Explicit statement of intent with a specific plan and means at hand

### Signs that require crisis helpline (6389 2222) but not necessarily 995:
- Suicidal thoughts without immediate plan or means
- Severe emotional distress, but person is calm enough to talk
- Acute anxiety, panic, or dissociation
- Acute psychiatric episode (e.g. psychosis) — person is distressed but not in immediate physical danger

When in doubt, call 6389 2222 first. The crisis counsellor will advise whether to proceed to A&E or ambulance.

## 2. WHO TO CALL RIGHT NOW

| Situation | Number | Hours |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| **Life-threatening emergency / ambulance** | **995** | 24/7 |
| **Police — danger to self or others** | **999** | 24/7 |
| **IMH Crisis Helpline** | **6389 2222** | 24/7 |
| **Samaritans of Singapore (SOS)** | **1767** | 24/7 |
| **mindline.sg** (chat-based support) | mindline.sg | 24/7 |

**IMH 6389 2222** is the primary mental health crisis line in Singapore. Calls are answered by trained Institute of Mental Health staff who can assess severity, advise on next steps, and arrange emergency psychiatric assessment.

**SOS 1767** provides emotional support by trained volunteers. Best for someone who needs to talk through distress and is not in immediate physical danger.

**Both lines are free.** You do not need a referral. You do not need a patient's identity to call on their behalf.

## 3. GOING TO A&E VERSUS CALLING AN AMBULANCE

### Call an ambulance (995) when:
- The person has taken an overdose — even if they seem fine. Many overdoses look benign for hours
- There is a physical injury from self-harm that needs treatment (lacerations, burns, fractures)
- The person is unconscious, unresponsive, or extremely confused
- You cannot safely transport the person without risk to yourself or them
- The person is actively threatening violence

### Go to A&E directly when:
- The person is in acute psychiatric distress (psychosis, mania, severe dissociation) but is physically uninjured
- Suicidal ideation is severe and the person is willing to go voluntarily
- It is outside IMH outpatient hours and the situation cannot wait

### Which A&E to go to:
Any restructured hospital A&E will assess and stabilise. For dedicated psychiatric assessment, **IMH (Buangkok Green Medical Park, 10 Buangkok View, S(539747))** has a 24-hour Accident and Emergency department staffed by psychiatrists.

If you go to a general hospital A&E (SGH, TTSH, NUH, etc.), they will carry out a medical assessment and call the on-call psychiatry team for mental health evaluation. You will not be turned away.

**Bring**: NRIC of the person in crisis, any information about medication they have taken, any known psychiatric history or care team contact details.

## 4. WHAT HAPPENS AT A&E / IMH

Understanding this in advance reduces fear.

**Step 1 — Triage.** Nurse assesses urgency. Tell them: *"This person has suicidal ideation / self-harmed / is in psychotic episode."* This flags the case for psychiatric review.

**Step 2 — Medical clearance.** A doctor rules out or treats physical causes (drug toxicity, head injury, metabolic causes of confusion). Blood tests, ECG, monitoring as needed.

**Step 3 — Psychiatric assessment.** A psychiatrist or psychiatry resident conducts a mental state examination and risk assessment. This takes 30–90 minutes. They will ask about suicidal thoughts, plans, previous episodes, medication, life circumstances.

**Step 4 — Decision.** The psychiatrist decides:
- **Discharge with outpatient follow-up** — for lower-risk presentations
- **Short-term stabilisation / observation** — stay of 23–72 hours
- **Voluntary admission** — person agrees to inpatient stay
- **Involuntary admission** — under the Mental Health (Care and Treatment) Act (see Section 5)

**Waiting times** at IMH A&E are generally shorter than general hospitals for psychiatric emergencies. At general hospitals, psychiatric assessment may take several hours due to the need to wait for the on-call team.

**You, as a family member or accompanying person**, may be asked to wait. Request an update after each stage. You are allowed to provide collateral history to the psychiatric team — this is helpful, not intrusive.

## 5. VOLUNTARY VERSUS INVOLUNTARY ADMISSION

### Voluntary admission
The person agrees to be admitted for assessment or treatment. They can, in principle, request discharge, though the treating team may advise against it. Most admissions in Singapore are voluntary.

### Involuntary admission under the Mental Health (Care and Treatment) Act (MHCTA)

The MHCTA allows a Designated Medical Practitioner to order admission to an approved psychiatric institution (IMH) if:

- The person is suffering from a mental disorder **and**
- Is unwilling to seek treatment voluntarily **and**
- There is risk of harm to themselves or others, or the person is unable to make a decision about their own care

**Who can initiate:** A medical officer at A&E or any registered medical practitioner can complete a Committal Order (Form 1) for up to 72 hours of assessment. Further detention requires a formal psychiatric review.

**What family members should know:** You cannot directly admit someone involuntarily — a medical officer must make the order. You can ask the A&E team to assess the person. If the person refuses to go to A&E and you believe they are at imminent risk, call 999. The Police have powers under the Criminal Procedure Code to convey a person to hospital for psychiatric evaluation.

**Rights of the person:** Involuntary patients have the right to be informed of their status, to legal representation, and to a review by the Mental Health Review Tribunal.

## 6. AFTER HOSPITALISATION — FOLLOW-UP CARE

Discharge is not the end. It is the start of the recovery phase.

### What to expect at discharge

The hospital will provide:
- A **follow-up outpatient appointment** at IMH or a restructured hospital's psychiatric outpatient clinic — usually within 1–4 weeks
- A **discharge memo** with diagnosis, medications, and crisis plan
- Contact details for the inpatient team if acute concerns arise before the next appointment

**Do not skip the first outpatient appointment.** The risk of a further crisis is highest in the weeks after discharge.

### Subsidised outpatient psychiatry

Public sector psychiatric outpatient care is means-tested and subsidised.

| Provider | Notes |
|----------|-------|
| **IMH Outpatient Clinics** | Main campus (Buangkok) and outreach clinics. Book via IMH or referral from GP |
| **Restructured hospital psychiatry departments** | SGH, TTSH, NUH, CGH, KTPH all have psychiatric outpatient services |
| **Polyclinic mental health services** | GPs at polyclinics can manage mild-to-moderate depression/anxiety; can refer to IMH or hospital outpatient |
| **CHAS (Community Health Assist Scheme)** | Subsidises GP visits including mental health consultations for qualifying households |

### CREST (Community Resource, Engagement and Support Team)

CREST teams provide **community-based mental health support** — they visit clients at home or in the community, support medication compliance, provide psychosocial rehabilitation, and work with families.

CREST is **free** for Singapore Citizens and PRs. Teams operate under various VWOs and are coordinated by IMH. Referral can be made by IMH outpatient, a GP, an FSC, or by calling **6389 2200** (IMH REACH).

## 7. SUPPORT FOR FAMILY MEMBERS AND CAREGIVERS

A mental health crisis is traumatic for everyone in the household. You need support too.

### IMH REACH (Re-integration and Empowerment through Active Community for the mentally ill at Home)

- Community-based mental health programme
- Supports individuals with mental health conditions living in the community
- Includes family psychoeducation and support
- Contact IMH at **6389 2200**

### SAMH Caregiver Support

**Singapore Association for Mental Health (SAMH)** runs caregiver support groups and counselling for families of persons with mental health conditions.

- **SAMH Helpline: 1800-283-7019** (Monday–Friday, 9am–6pm)
- **Website:** samhealth.org.sg
- Services: counselling, psychoeducation workshops, caregiver support group meetings

### Family Service Centres (FSCs)

Your local FSC can provide counselling to family members regardless of whether the person in crisis is their client.

- **50+ FSCs** across Singapore. Find yours at: go.gov.sg/fsc
- No referral needed. Walk in or call.

### What caregivers often find hardest

- Not knowing what to say. (Saying *"I'm here"* is enough. You don't need to fix it.)
- Feeling responsible for preventing another crisis.
- Burnout from hypervigilance.

These are normal responses. You are allowed to ask for your own support.

## QUICK-START: FIRST THREE ACTIONS

1. **Is there immediate physical danger?** → Call **995** (ambulance) or **999** (police)
2. **Crisis but not immediate physical danger?** → Call **IMH Crisis Helpline 6389 2222** — they will tell you the next step
3. **After the crisis is stabilised** → Call **SAMH 1800-283-7019** or IMH REACH **6389 2200** for ongoing support for the person and for yourself

## A NOTE TO THE PERSON IN CRISIS

If you are the one struggling and you found your way to this page: that took courage. You do not have to be in the "right" level of distress to call. SOS (1767) and IMH (6389 2222) will talk to you wherever you are. You are allowed to take up that space.
