## What Is PPH?

PPH stands for **postpartum haemorrhage**. It means a woman is bleeding too much after giving birth.

Some bleeding after birth is normal. PPH is when the bleeding is heavier than normal and does not slow down. It can become life-threatening very quickly.

**This is an emergency. Act fast.**

---

## Signs of PPH — What to Look For

### Signs You Can See

- [ ] Soaking through pads very quickly (more than one pad in 15 minutes)
- [ ] Blood pooling on the bed, mat, or floor
- [ ] Large blood clots (bigger than a golf ball)
- [ ] Bleeding that keeps going and does not slow down

### Signs in the Woman

- [ ] Feeling faint or dizzy — "everything spinning"
- [ ] Pale skin, pale lips, pale inside of eyelids
- [ ] Skin that feels cold and clammy (cool and sweaty)
- [ ] Fast heartbeat — her heart is racing or pounding
- [ ] She feels very weak or cannot sit up
- [ ] She is confused or hard to wake up

**If you see two or more of these signs together — this is a life-threatening emergency. Call for help right now.**

---

## Immediate Actions — What to Do

### 1. Call for Help NOW
- Shout for others nearby.
- Call emergency services or your supervisor immediately.
- Do not leave the woman alone.

### 2. Keep Her Warm
- Cover her with a blanket.
- Keep her lying flat — do not let her sit up.
- Raise her legs slightly if possible (to help blood flow to the heart).

### 3. Reassure Her
- Speak calmly: "I am here. Help is coming."
- Keep her awake and talking if she is conscious.

### 4. Make a Record
While waiting for help, note:
- Time bleeding started
- Estimated amount of blood (how many pads soaked, how large the pool)
- Her pulse if you can check it (count beats for 15 seconds, multiply by 4)

### 5. Prepare for Transport
- Arrange the fastest transport to the nearest facility with an operating room.
- Tell the transport driver this is an emergency.
- Go with her if at all possible.

---

## What NOT to Do

- **Do not** give any medicine or herbal preparation — this is the job of the facility.
- **Do not** massage the belly vigorously — unless you have been trained to do uterine massage and are certain the placenta has been delivered.
- **Do not** leave her to go and get help — send someone else, or take her with you.
- **Do not** give anything by mouth — she may need surgery.
- **Do not** wait to see if the bleeding slows down — act immediately.

---

## What to Tell the Health Facility

When you arrive or call ahead, say:

> "I have a woman who just gave birth. She is bleeding heavily and [describe her signs — pale, faint, soaking pads]. She needs help now."

---

## Danger Signs — Act Now If You See These

- Heavy bleeding soaking pads fast
- Pale, cold, or clammy skin
- Feeling faint or confused
- Racing heartbeat
- Woman cannot stay awake

**Any of these = emergency referral immediately.**

---

## When in Doubt: Refer

If you are unsure, always refer to the nearest health facility. It is never wrong to ask for help.
