## What This Card Is For

This card helps you remind pregnant women when to attend antenatal care (ANC) visits and what to expect at each visit. It is based on the World Health Organization (WHO) recommendation of **8 contacts during pregnancy**.

"Contact" means any visit with a health worker — at a clinic, health post, or home.

---

## Why ANC Matters — What to Tell the Woman

> "Each visit helps make sure you and your baby are healthy. Problems found early are easier to treat. Missing visits puts you and your baby at greater risk."

---

## ANC Schedule — 8 Contacts

| Contact | When | What Is Usually Checked |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | Before 12 weeks (first 3 months) | First full check-up; blood tests; urine test; weight and blood pressure; review medical history; talk about nutrition, rest, and danger signs |
| **2** | 20 weeks | Check blood pressure and weight; listen to baby's heartbeat; check for anaemia (low blood); talk about birth plan |
| **3** | 26 weeks | Blood pressure; weight; check baby's position; ask about any concerns |
| **4** | 30 weeks | Blood pressure; weight; baby's size and position; check swelling; review danger signs |
| **5** | 34 weeks | Blood pressure; weight; baby's position; start thinking about where to give birth |
| **6** | 36 weeks | Blood pressure; weight; baby's position confirmed; birth plan reviewed; signs of labour explained |
| **7** | 38 weeks | Blood pressure; weight; signs of labour; when to go to the facility |
| **8** | 40 weeks | Final check before due date; plan for if labour does not start |

---

## What CHWs Can Do at Each Contact

- Confirm the woman attended her clinic visit.
- Ask if she has any new concerns or warning signs.
- Remind her of the next scheduled visit.
- Check she has her ANC card or booklet and it is up to date.
- Encourage her to bring a support person to visits.

---

## Warning Signs Between Visits

At every home visit, ask these questions. If the answer is yes to any, refer based on CHW-J-01 (Danger Signs in Pregnancy).

- [ ] Any bleeding from the vagina?
- [ ] Severe headache or vision problems?
- [ ] Swelling of face or hands?
- [ ] Baby not moving as much as usual?
- [ ] Fever?
- [ ] Fits or fainting?
- [ ] Leaking fluid from the vagina (not urine)?
- [ ] Severe belly pain?

---

## How to Encourage ANC Attendance

### If she says "I feel fine — do I need to go?"

> "ANC is not just for when you are sick. It is to keep you and your baby well. Some problems have no signs yet — the health worker can find them early."

### If she says "I am too busy / I cannot afford transport"

> "Let's think about how to make it easier. Is there a visit we can plan together? Can a family member take you?"

> Connect her with local transport support or community funds if available.

### If she says "My family does not want me to go"

> "Going to ANC is important for you and your baby's safety. Would it help if I spoke with your family, or came with you to the clinic?"

### If she has missed a visit

> "It is not too late. You can still go. The health worker will be glad you came. Let's plan your next visit now."

---

## First Visit Is the Most Important

The first contact (before 12 weeks) sets up all future care. If a woman comes to you late in pregnancy without any ANC visits, help her get to the clinic as soon as possible.

---

## Key Messages to Repeat Every Visit

1. Keep your ANC card safe — bring it to every visit.
2. Plan early where you will give birth.
3. Know the danger signs and act quickly if they happen.
4. Eat well, rest, and drink clean water.
5. Do not take any medicine without asking a health worker first.

---

## Danger Signs — Refer Immediately If You See These

- Heavy bleeding at any time in pregnancy
- Fits or convulsions
- Severe headache with vision changes
- Baby not moving (after 28 weeks)
- Fever with severe headache or stiff neck
- Difficulty breathing

See CHW-J-01 for the full danger signs list.

---

## When in Doubt: Refer

If you are unsure, always refer to the nearest health facility. It is never wrong to ask for help.
