## What This Card Is For

This card helps you **talk with women and couples about family planning choices**. You can explain what each method is and who it may suit.

**Your role is to inform and refer — not to prescribe or provide.**

Only a trained health worker or clinician can prescribe, insert, or inject contraceptives.

---

## Key Message for Every Conversation

> "There is no single best method. The best method is the one that works for this person, at this time in their life. A health worker can help them choose."

Always end the conversation by offering a referral to a health facility or family planning clinic.

## Method Overview

### 1. Male Condom

| | |
|---|---|
| **How it works** | A thin cover worn over the penis during sex. It stops sperm from reaching the egg. |
| **Effectiveness** | Works well when used correctly every time. |
| **Who can use it** | Almost anyone. No health check needed. |
| **Extra benefit** | The only method that also protects against sexually transmitted infections (STIs). |
| **Who needs clinician first** | No one — available without a prescription. |
| **CHW action** | Explain correct use. Provide if your programme allows. Refer if the person has questions about STIs. |

---

### 2. Female Condom

| | |
|---|---|
| **How it works** | A soft pouch inserted into the vagina before sex. It stops sperm from entering. |
| **Effectiveness** | Works well when used correctly every time. |
| **Who can use it** | Almost anyone. No health check needed. |
| **Extra benefit** | Also protects against STIs. Woman controls its use. |
| **Who needs clinician first** | No one — available without a prescription. |
| **CHW action** | Explain correct use. Refer if not available in your area. |

---

### 3. Combined Oral Contraceptive Pill ("the pill")

| | |
|---|---|
| **How it works** | A pill taken every day. It stops the ovaries from releasing an egg each month. |
| **Effectiveness** | Very effective when taken correctly every day at the same time. |
| **Who can use it** | Most healthy women. |
| **Who should see a clinician first** | Women who smoke and are over 35; women with high blood pressure; women with a history of blood clots, stroke, or heart disease; women who are breastfeeding a baby under 6 months. |
| **CHW action** | Explain what the pill is. Refer to facility for assessment and prescription. Do not give pills yourself unless your programme specifically authorises this. |

---

### 4. Progestogen-only Pill ("mini-pill")

| | |
|---|---|
| **How it works** | A pill taken every day. It works mainly by thickening cervical mucus to block sperm. |
| **Effectiveness** | Very effective when taken correctly every day at the same time. |
| **Who can use it** | Most women, including breastfeeding mothers and women who cannot use oestrogen. |
| **Who should see a clinician first** | Women taking certain seizure or TB medicines (these may reduce effectiveness). |
| **CHW action** | Explain what the mini-pill is. Refer to facility for assessment and prescription. |

---

### 5. Injectable Contraceptive ("the injection" or "the shot")

| | |
|---|---|
| **How it works** | A hormone injection given by a health worker every 1 to 3 months. It stops egg release. |
| **Effectiveness** | Very effective when given on time. |
| **Who can use it** | Most women. |
| **Who should see a clinician first** | Women with unexplained vaginal bleeding; women with liver disease; women who want to become pregnant soon (fertility may take some months to return). |
| **CHW action** | Explain how injections work and the schedule. Refer to facility — only trained providers give injections. |

---

### 6. Implant ("the rod under the skin")

| | |
|---|---|
| **How it works** | A small flexible rod inserted under the skin of the upper arm by a trained provider. It releases hormones slowly for up to 3 to 5 years. |
| **Effectiveness** | One of the most effective methods available. |
| **Who can use it** | Most women who want long-term protection. |
| **Who should see a clinician first** | Same as for injectables. Also women taking certain medicines that may reduce effectiveness. |
| **CHW action** | Explain how implants work and that a small procedure is needed. Refer to facility — only trained providers insert implants. |

---

### 7. Intrauterine Device (IUD) — copper or hormonal

| | |
|---|---|
| **How it works** | A small device placed inside the uterus by a trained provider. The copper type stops sperm from fertilising an egg. The hormonal type also thins the lining of the uterus. Lasts 3 to 12 years depending on the type. |
| **Effectiveness** | One of the most effective methods available. |
| **Who can use it** | Most women, including those who have never been pregnant. |
| **Who should see a clinician first** | Women with unexplained vaginal bleeding; women with a current STI or pelvic infection (must be treated first); women with some uterine problems. |
| **CHW action** | Explain how IUDs work and that a small procedure is needed. Refer to facility — only trained providers insert IUDs. |

---

### 8. Permanent Methods (sterilisation)

| | |
|---|---|
| **How it works** | **For women (tubal ligation):** The fallopian tubes are cut or blocked so eggs cannot meet sperm. **For men (vasectomy):** The tubes carrying sperm are cut or blocked. Both are done by a surgeon. |
| **Effectiveness** | The most effective methods. Intended to be permanent. |
| **Who can use it** | People who are certain they do not want more children. |
| **Who should see a clinician first** | Everyone — this is a surgical procedure requiring full counselling and informed consent. |
| **CHW action** | Explain that permanent options exist. Strongly emphasise that this is a lifelong decision. Refer to facility for full counselling. Do not pressure or push anyone toward this choice. |

## Method Comparison at a Glance

| Method | How Long It Works | User Does Something Each Time? | Needs Clinician? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Male condom | Per use | Yes | No |
| Female condom | Per use | Yes | No |
| Pill (combined) | Daily pill | Yes | Yes (prescription) |
| Mini-pill | Daily pill | Yes | Yes (prescription) |
| Injection | 1–3 months | No (until next shot) | Yes |
| Implant | 3–5 years | No | Yes (insertion) |
| IUD | 3–12 years | No | Yes (insertion) |
| Permanent | Lifelong | No | Yes (surgery) |

## What to Say at the End of Every Family Planning Talk

> "The best way to find the right method for you is to talk with a health worker. Would you like me to help you get to a clinic?"

Always offer a referral. Let the person decide.

---

## Danger Signs — Refer If a Person Reports These After Starting a Method

- Severe belly or pelvic pain
- Chest pain or difficulty breathing
- Severe headache or vision changes
- Heavy or unusual bleeding
- Signs of infection (fever, unusual discharge, pain)

---

## When in Doubt: Refer

If you are unsure, always refer to the nearest health facility. It is never wrong to ask for help.
