## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The world is ageing rapidly. The number of people aged 60 and over reached approximately 1.1 billion in 2023 and is projected to reach 2.1 billion by 2050. This demographic transition — driven by declining fertility and increasing life expectancy — is transforming societies across all regions, though at different speeds. East Asia (Japan, South Korea, China, Thailand), Europe, and increasingly parts of Latin America face the most immediate ageing challenges; sub-Saharan Africa, with its young population structure, will face ageing pressures later this century.

UNFPA's historical mandate has focused on reproductive health, youth, and population growth. Ageing expands this mandate into unfamiliar territory: post-reproductive health, pension and care economy policy, intergenerational solidarity, elder abuse, and age-friendly service design. UNFPA's engagement with ageing is anchored in the 2002 Madrid International Plan of Action on Ageing (MIPAA), for which UNFPA serves as lead UN entity on follow-up. However, UNFPA's ageing work remains underfunded and programmatically underdeveloped compared to its SRHR core.

The relevance is growing. Population ageing affects UNFPA's mandate in several ways: older women face specific health needs (cervical and breast cancer, menopause, osteoporosis, violence and neglect); ageing populations shift health system demands; the care economy (disproportionately borne by women) intersects with gender equality; and ageing societies face fiscal pressures that can squeeze health and social protection spending.

## KEY FACTS

- **Global population aged 60+**: Approximately 1.1 billion (2023); projected 2.1 billion by 2050 (UN DESA)
- **Fastest ageing regions**: East Asia (Japan 29% aged 65+, South Korea 18%), Europe (20%), and emerging in Southeast Asia (Thailand 14%) and Latin America
- **Japan**: World's oldest society; 29% aged 65+; TFR 1.2; population declining since 2008
- **South Korea**: World's lowest TFR (0.7 in 2023); facing severe ageing trajectory
- **China**: Entered population decline in 2022; elderly population projected to reach 500 million by 2050
- **MIPAA (2002)**: Madrid International Plan of Action on Ageing — the primary intergovernmental framework; UNFPA leads follow-up
- **MIPAA review cycles**: Every 5 years (2007, 2012, 2017, 2022); the fourth review completed in 2023
- **Older women's health**: Post-menopausal women face specific health risks largely excluded from traditional SRHR programming
- **Elder abuse**: WHO estimates 1 in 6 people aged 60+ experience some form of abuse; older women are disproportionately affected
- **Care economy**: Globally, women perform approximately 75% of unpaid care work, including elder care; ageing increases this burden
- **Old-age dependency ratio**: Rising in virtually all countries; OECD average projected to reach 60 elderly per 100 working-age by 2050
- **UNFPA ageing budget**: Small relative to SRHR programming; ageing is not one of the three transformative results

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### The Demographic Transition

Population ageing is the inevitable consequence of the demographic transition — the shift from high fertility/high mortality to low fertility/low mortality. Countries that achieved this transition earlier (Europe, Japan) are already deeply aged. Countries transitioning now (Thailand, China, Brazil) will age faster because their fertility decline was more rapid.

The implications are profound:
- Workforce shrinkage without immigration or productivity gains
- Rising elderly dependency ratios straining pension and health systems
- Increased demand for chronic disease management and long-term care
- Shifting family structures (fewer working-age adults to support elderly relatives)

### UNFPA and MIPAA

UNFPA was designated the lead UN entity for follow-up to the Madrid International Plan of Action on Ageing (MIPAA, 2002). MIPAA has three priority directions: older persons and development; advancing health and well-being into old age; and ensuring enabling and supportive environments. UNFPA's ageing work has focused on:
- Supporting governments in MIPAA implementation and review
- Advocacy for older persons' rights and inclusion
- Research on ageing, particularly in developing countries
- Intergenerational solidarity programming

## DETAIL

### How Ageing Relates to UNFPA's Core Mandate

**Post-reproductive health**: Traditional SRHR programming focuses on reproductive years (15–49). But SRHR-related health needs do not end with menopause: cervical cancer peaks in women 50+, breast cancer risk increases with age, menopause-related health issues affect quality of life, and sexually transmitted infections can affect older adults. UNFPA's mandate implicitly extends to these areas but programming has been limited.

**Gender and ageing**: Women live longer than men in most countries but are more likely to face poverty in old age (due to lifetime earnings gaps, inadequate pension coverage, and dependence on deceased spouse's income). Older women face specific risks: isolation, economic dependence, violence and neglect (elder abuse), and limited access to health services.

**Care economy**: As populations age, demand for care work increases — and this burden falls overwhelmingly on women and girls. In developing countries without formal elderly care systems, daughters and daughters-in-law are expected to provide care, often at the expense of their own education, employment, and reproductive health. This is a gender equality issue directly relevant to UNFPA's mandate.

**Intergenerational solidarity**: UNFPA's R-04 document on community resilience already addresses intergenerational solidarity. Ageing adds urgency: societies need to design systems that are fair across generations, avoid age-based discrimination, and maintain social cohesion as the balance between working-age and elderly shifts.

### Regional Perspectives

**East Asia**: Japan's experience is instructive — a rapidly ageing society with a sophisticated long-term care insurance system but persistent gender inequality in care work. South Korea's fertility crisis (TFR 0.7) is driving emergency pro-natalist policies. China's ageing will create the world's largest elderly population in a context of limited social protection and a "4-2-1" family structure (four grandparents, two parents, one child).

**Southeast Asia**: Thailand (TFR 1.3) is ageing faster than its social protection systems can adapt. Vietnam and Indonesia are earlier in the transition but will face similar pressures within decades. UNFPA's Asia-Pacific Regional Office has increased attention to ageing.

**Sub-Saharan Africa**: Currently the youngest region globally (median age approximately 18), SSA will not face severe population ageing this century. However, the growing number of older persons in Africa — particularly older women — already face neglect, inadequate pensions, and HIV-related caregiving burdens (grandmothers raising AIDS-orphaned children).

---

## CURRENT STATUS

- Population ageing is accelerating globally, with East Asia and Europe most immediately affected
- UNFPA's ageing work remains a secondary priority relative to the three transformative results
- MIPAA fourth review (2023) reaffirmed commitments but with limited new financing
- The care economy is gaining attention as a gender equality and development issue
- Some country offices (China, Thailand, Georgia) have developed substantive ageing programmes; most have not
- The 2026–2029 Strategic Plan may expand ageing as a strategic priority, reflecting demographic realities
