## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The 45 Least Developed Countries (LDCs) — defined by the UN Committee for Development Policy based on per capita income, human assets, and economic vulnerability — are where UNFPA's three transformative results ("three zeros") face the steepest challenge and where the organisation concentrates its largest share of core programme resources. LDCs account for a disproportionate share of global maternal deaths (approximately 40 per cent), have the highest unmet need for family planning, the highest rates of child marriage and FGM, and the weakest health systems. Thirty-three of the 45 LDCs are in sub-Saharan Africa, with the remainder in Asia (Afghanistan, Bangladesh, Cambodia, Myanmar, Nepal, Timor-Leste, Laos, Bhutan, Yemen) and the Pacific (Solomon Islands, Kiribati, Tuvalu) and the Caribbean (Haiti).

UNFPA's resource allocation formula directs the largest share of core programme resources to LDCs, reflecting their acute needs. However, the combination of limited government capacity, weak health infrastructure, fragile governance, and frequent humanitarian crises means that translating funding into sustained outcomes is exceptionally difficult. Many LDCs face compounding vulnerabilities — climate change, conflict, food insecurity, debt distress — that undermine SRHR progress even when specific SRHR investments are effective.

The international community has established successive frameworks for LDC development — the Brussels Programme of Action (2001), the Istanbul Programme of Action (2011), and the Doha Programme of Action (2022) — all of which include health and gender equality commitments. UNFPA supports LDC governments in meeting these commitments while advocating for increased international support.

## KEY FACTS

- **LDC count**: 45 countries as of 2025 (Bangladesh is in transition toward graduation)
- **LDC population**: Approximately 1.1 billion, projected to reach 1.9 billion by 2050
- **Share of global maternal deaths**: LDCs account for approximately 40% of the global total despite representing approximately 14% of global population
- **Average MMR in LDCs**: Approximately 400/100,000 — more than 5 times the global average
- **Modern CPR in LDCs**: Approximately 28% among married women — roughly half the global average
- **Unmet need for FP in LDCs**: Approximately 20% — the highest of any country grouping
- **Adolescent birth rate in LDCs**: Approximately 95/1,000 — more than double the global average
- **Child marriage in LDCs**: Approximately 40% of girls married before 18 in LDCs, compared to 21% globally
- **Skilled birth attendance in LDCs**: Approximately 56% of births — compared to 83% globally
- **Health expenditure**: LDC average government health expenditure approximately USD 15 per capita per year, vs. WHO recommendation of at least USD 86 for essential services
- **Aid dependency**: ODA represents over 10% of GNI in many LDCs; SRHR programming depends heavily on external financing
- **UNFPA core allocation to LDCs**: Approximately 60% of core programme resources directed to LDCs through the resource allocation formula
- **LDC graduation**: Bangladesh, Laos, and Nepal are in transition toward graduation from LDC status; implications for future UNFPA resource allocation
- **Doha Programme of Action (2022)**: Includes commitments on universal health coverage, SRHR, gender equality, and demographic dividend — UNFPA is a key implementing partner

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### What Defines an LDC

The UN Committee for Development Policy reviews the LDC list every three years based on three criteria:
1. **GNI per capita**: Below a threshold (approximately USD 1,222 for inclusion)
2. **Human Assets Index**: Composite of health (nutrition, child mortality, maternal mortality) and education indicators
3. **Economic and Environmental Vulnerability Index**: Exposure to natural shocks, trade concentration, smallness

Countries must meet all three criteria for inclusion and exceed thresholds on two of three for graduation. The process is political as well as technical — some countries resist graduation (losing trade preferences and concessional financing).

### Why LDCs Matter for UNFPA's Mandate

The concentration of SRHR need in LDCs is striking:
- 9 of the 10 countries with the highest maternal mortality are LDCs
- All 10 countries with the highest child marriage rates are LDCs
- The 10 countries with the lowest contraceptive prevalence are overwhelmingly LDCs
- LDCs have the youngest population structures — median ages under 20 in many Sahelian countries

UNFPA's strategic rationale for prioritising LDCs is both ethical (greatest need) and practical (greatest potential for impact — marginal improvements in SRHR services reach populations with the worst baselines).

## DETAIL

### Health System Challenges in LDCs

LDC health systems face cascading weaknesses that affect SRHR:

**Workforce**: Critical shortages of midwives, nurses, and physicians. The SoWMy 2021 found that LDCs have approximately 25% of the midwifery workforce needed for universal coverage. Many health workers in LDCs are concentrated in urban areas, leaving rural populations without access.

**Infrastructure**: Health facilities in LDCs often lack electricity, running water, blood banks, surgical capacity, and cold chain for medicines. Emergency obstetric care requires surgical capability (C-section) that is absent in many rural LDC hospitals.

**Supply chains**: Commodity supply chains are fragile. UNFPA's contraceptive procurement reaches national warehouses, but last-mile delivery to health facilities — particularly in remote areas — depends on government logistics systems that are often underfunded and understaffed.

**Financing**: LDC governments spend an average of approximately USD 15 per capita on health annually — far below the estimated USD 86 per capita needed for essential health services (including SRHR). This means SRHR services in LDCs depend heavily on external financing — from UNFPA, bilateral donors, the Global Fund, and other multilateral sources.

### LDC Graduation and UNFPA Transitions

When countries graduate from LDC status, they lose access to specific trade preferences, concessional financing, and often face reduced ODA. For UNFPA, graduation does not automatically mean withdrawal — the resource allocation formula is based on need indicators, not LDC status per se. However, graduating countries typically see gradual reductions in UNFPA core allocations as their income rises and SRHR indicators improve.

Bangladesh's ongoing graduation process is the most significant case. Despite crossing income and human assets thresholds, Bangladesh retains significant SRHR needs (high adolescent pregnancy, GBV, climate vulnerability). UNFPA's engagement is shifting from service delivery support to policy advice and South-South cooperation facilitation.

### The Demographic Dimension

LDCs collectively have the world's fastest population growth — approximately 2.3% annually compared to 0.8% globally. Their population is projected to roughly double by 2050. This growth is concentrated in countries with the weakest governance and service delivery capacity (Niger, DRC, Mali, Chad, Somalia).

The demographic trajectory creates a dual challenge: meeting the immediate SRHR needs of growing populations, and investing in the education, health, and employment conditions that could convert a youth bulge into a demographic dividend rather than a demographic burden.

## CURRENT STATUS

- LDCs remain the primary focus of UNFPA's core programme resource allocation
- The Doha Programme of Action (2022) provides an updated international framework for LDC support, including SRHR commitments
- Multiple compounding crises — COVID-19 recovery, climate impacts, debt distress, food insecurity, conflict — are straining LDC capacity and diverting resources from SRHR investment
- UNFPA is investing in building sustainable national SRHR systems in LDCs rather than parallel service delivery, though the transition is slow
- Demographic momentum ensures LDCs will face growing population pressure through at least 2050, intensifying the need for SRHR investment
- Bangladesh's LDC graduation is a test case for how UNFPA manages the transition while maintaining SRHR support
