## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This document provides operational guidance on disability-inclusive SRHR programming, complementing the rights and legal framework analysis in UNFPA-H-05. An estimated 1.3 billion people globally live with significant disability, yet SRHR services remain largely inaccessible to them. Persons with disabilities are not a homogenous group — physical, sensory (visual, hearing), intellectual, and psychosocial disabilities each create different barriers requiring different adaptations. Effective disability-inclusive SRHR programming requires: physical accessibility of health facilities, communication in accessible formats, training of health workers to address disability-specific needs, inclusion of disability-led organisations as partners, and data disaggregation by disability status.

UNFPA's practical programming for disability inclusion is most advanced in GBV response (accessible safe spaces in humanitarian settings) and CSE (adapted curricula for learners with disabilities). In family planning and maternal health, disability-inclusive programming remains at an early stage. This document reviews programme approaches, evidence on what works, and practical tools for country offices.

---

## KEY FACTS

- **Global prevalence**: 1.3 billion people (16% of global population) with significant disability
- **Sexual violence risk**: 2–3x higher for persons with disabilities; up to 10x for women with intellectual disabilities
- **Contraceptive access**: Women with disabilities in LMICs are 2–3x less likely to use modern contraception
- **Forced sterilisation**: Persists in many countries; CRPD Committee has issued over 40 country-specific recommendations against it
- **Physical accessibility**: Less than 50% of health facilities in LMICs meet basic accessibility standards
- **Washington Group questions**: Standardised disability identification tool; adopted in DHS-8 and recent MICS rounds
- **UNFPA guidelines**: "Women and Young Persons with Disabilities" guidelines published 2018
- **IASC Disability Guidelines**: 2019 guidelines on inclusion in humanitarian action include SRHR provisions

---

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

Disability inclusion in SRHR has gained momentum since the CRPD (2006) but implementation remains poor. The medical model (disability as individual deficit) is being replaced by the social model (disability as societal barrier), but health systems — including SRHR services — have been slow to adapt. Persons with disabilities continue to encounter: examination tables they cannot access, health information they cannot read or hear, providers who refuse to discuss sexuality, and policies that allow others to make reproductive decisions on their behalf.

## DETAIL

### Physical Accessibility
- Ramps, accessible toilets, adjustable examination tables, visual signage
- Minimum standards: WHO "Standards for Improving Quality of Maternal and Newborn Care in Health Facilities" include accessibility provisions
- Cost of retrofitting is often modest relative to total facility investment

### Communication Accessibility
- Sign language interpretation for deaf patients during SRHR consultations
- Braille and large-print materials on contraceptive options
- Easy-read formats for persons with intellectual disabilities
- Picture-based communication for non-verbal individuals

### Health Worker Training
- Disability awareness and attitude change as part of pre-service and in-service training
- Specific clinical skills: communicating about sexuality with persons with intellectual disabilities; adapting clinical procedures for patients with physical disabilities; understanding the higher GBV risk
- Combating assumptions: persons with disabilities are sexual beings, can be parents, and have the right to make their own reproductive choices

### GBV Response
- UNFPA safe spaces in humanitarian settings should meet IASC disability accessibility standards
- GBV screening tools should be adapted for persons with communication-related disabilities
- Referral pathways must include disability-specific services (legal support, psychosocial support)
- Assistive technology (hearing aids, mobility aids) may be critical for GBV survivors with disabilities to access services

### Comprehensive Sexuality Education
- CSE curricula should be adapted for learners with visual, hearing, intellectual, and physical disabilities
- Peer education models using persons with disabilities as educators
- Special schools and inclusive schools both need CSE programming

### Data and Monitoring
- Washington Group Short Set on Functioning: 6 questions identifying difficulty in seeing, hearing, walking, remembering/concentrating, self-care, and communication
- Inclusion in DHS and MICS enables disaggregated SRHR analysis
- Programme monitoring should track disability-inclusive access indicators

---

## CURRENT STATUS

- Disability inclusion is a stated UNFPA priority but implementation varies significantly across country offices
- The Washington Group questions are being adopted in more national surveys, improving the data foundation
- GBV programming is the most advanced area for disability inclusion in UNFPA's work
- Most country offices lack disability inclusion focal points or dedicated budgets
- Partnership with disability-led organisations is growing but not yet systematic
