## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development and its 17 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), adopted in September 2015, provide the overarching framework within which UNFPA operates. UNFPA's mandate intersects primarily with SDG 3 (Good Health and Well-Being), SDG 5 (Gender Equality), and SDG 10 (Reduced Inequalities), but the organisation's work also contributes to SDGs 1 (No Poverty), 4 (Quality Education), 11 (Sustainable Cities), 13 (Climate Action), 16 (Peace, Justice and Strong Institutions), and 17 (Partnerships).

UNFPA serves as custodian or co-custodian agency for several SDG indicators, meaning it is responsible for compiling global data and reporting on progress. The most significant include: indicator 3.1.1 (maternal mortality ratio), indicator 3.1.2 (proportion of births attended by skilled health personnel), indicator 3.7.1 (proportion of women of reproductive age who have their need for family planning satisfied with modern methods), indicator 5.3.1 (proportion of women married before age 18), and indicator 5.3.2 (proportion of girls and women who have undergone FGM).

The transition from the Millennium Development Goals (2000–2015) to the SDGs was consequential for SRHR. The MDGs included maternal health (MDG 5) but largely excluded family planning, GBV, harmful practices, and comprehensive sexuality education. The SDGs expanded the frame, with targets 3.7 (universal access to SRHR services) and 5.6 (universal access to SRHR) explicitly incorporating a broader SRHR agenda. However, the SDG framework also diluted focus by spreading SRHR across multiple goals rather than consolidating it, and the indicator framework remains contested — particularly regarding abortion, CSE, and sexual orientation.

At the current pace, most SRHR-related SDG targets will not be met by 2030. UNFPA's strategic plans acknowledge this reality and frame the 2026–2029 period as the final acceleration window before the 2030 deadline.

## KEY FACTS

- **SDG adoption**: September 2015, by all 193 UN member states (GA resolution 70/1, "Transforming our world")
- **UNFPA custodian indicators**: UNFPA is custodian or co-custodian for approximately 8 SDG indicators across Goals 3, 5, and 17
- **SDG 3.1.1 (MMR)**: Target to reduce global MMR to below 70 per 100,000 live births by 2030; current global estimate approximately 223/100,000 (2020 WHO/UNFPA/World Bank/UNICEF estimate) — far off target
- **SDG 3.7.1 (FP satisfied)**: Proportion of women with demand for family planning satisfied by modern methods; global average approximately 77% (2023) but below 50% in many SSA countries
- **SDG 5.3.1 (child marriage)**: 1 in 5 girls globally married before 18; rate has declined but not fast enough to meet the 2030 elimination target
- **SDG 5.3.2 (FGM)**: Estimated 200+ million girls and women alive today have undergone FGM; prevalence declining in some countries but population growth means absolute numbers may increase
- **SDG 5.6 (SRHR access)**: Target for universal access to sexual and reproductive health and reproductive rights; contested in negotiations and implementation
- **VNR process**: Voluntary National Reviews at the High-Level Political Forum (HLPF); UNFPA has supported countries in reporting on SRHR SDG indicators; quality and depth of SRHR coverage in VNRs varies enormously
- **2023 SDG Summit**: Midpoint assessment found that of 169 SDG targets globally, only 15% are on track; SRHR targets are among those most off-track in the poorest countries
- **MDG to SDG transition**: MDG 5 (Improve Maternal Health) had two targets: reduce MMR by 75% and achieve universal access to reproductive health. The SDGs expanded this significantly but fragmented SRHR across multiple goals
- **Missing SDG targets**: Abortion, comprehensive sexuality education, and LGBTQ+ rights are not explicitly included as SDG targets — a political compromise that limits the framework's comprehensiveness

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### From MDGs to SDGs: What Changed for SRHR

The MDGs (2000–2015) were a mixed legacy for SRHR:

**Positive**: MDG 5 put maternal health on the global development agenda and mobilised significant investment. Maternal mortality declined by approximately 44% between 1990 and 2015 globally.

**Negative**: The original MDG framework (2000) included only MDG 5a (reduce MMR). Target 5b (universal access to reproductive health) was added only in 2007, after advocacy by UNFPA and allies. Family planning, GBV, harmful practices, adolescent SRHR, and comprehensive sexuality education were largely absent from the MDG indicator framework. This meant that SRHR was narrowly equated with maternal health, and broader reproductive rights were marginalised in development financing and accountability.

The SDG negotiation (2012–2015) was an opportunity to correct this. UNFPA and the SRHR community advocated for:
- A standalone SRHR target (achieved: SDG 5.6)
- Integration of SRHR in the health goal (achieved: SDG 3.7)
- Explicit reference to "reproductive rights" in the SDG framework (achieved, after intense negotiation)
- Inclusion of harmful practices (achieved: SDG 5.3)
- Comprehensive sexuality education (partially achieved through SDG 4 education targets, but not explicitly named)
- Abortion (not achieved — excluded from the framework due to political opposition)

### UNFPA's SDG Custodianship Role

As custodian agency for key SDG indicators, UNFPA has specific responsibilities:

1. **Data compilation**: Collecting, validating, and aggregating national data from surveys (DHS, MICS), administrative systems, and modelling exercises
2. **Methodology**: Developing and refining measurement methodologies (e.g., the inter-agency maternal mortality estimation group)
3. **Reporting**: Submitting data to the UN Statistics Division for inclusion in the annual SDG progress reports
4. **Capacity building**: Supporting national statistical offices in collecting and reporting SDG indicator data
5. **Advocacy**: Using SDG data to advocate for investment and policy action

## DETAIL

### Progress Assessment by Target

**SDG 3.1 — Maternal mortality**: The target is to reduce the global MMR to below 70/100,000 by 2030, with no country above 140/100,000. The 2020 global MMR was estimated at 223/100,000 — essentially unchanged from 2015 (227/100,000), meaning no progress was made in the first five years of the SDG period. Sub-Saharan Africa (545/100,000) and South Asia (remaining above the global average) account for the vast majority of maternal deaths. At current trends, the 2030 target is unachievable.

**SDG 3.7 — Universal SRHR access**: Measured primarily through met need for family planning and adolescent birth rate. Met need for modern contraception has improved modestly (from 75% to approximately 77% globally), but progress in SSA and South Asia is slower. The adolescent birth rate has declined globally but remains above 100/1,000 in several SSA countries.

**SDG 5.2 — Eliminate violence against women**: Based on lifetime and past-12-month prevalence data, approximately 1 in 3 women globally has experienced physical and/or sexual violence. Prevalence data shows modest declines in some regions but the pace of change is insufficient for 2030 elimination.

**SDG 5.3 — Eliminate harmful practices**: Child marriage rates have declined from approximately 25% to 19% globally over the past decade, but population growth means the absolute number of child brides is not declining proportionally in all regions. FGM prevalence has declined in most practising countries, but rapidly growing populations in high-prevalence countries (Mali, Sudan, Guinea, Somalia) mean the absolute number at risk may increase.

**SDG 5.6 — Universal SRHR access and reproductive rights**: This target is measured through indicator 5.6.1 (proportion of women aged 15–49 who make their own informed decisions regarding sexual relations, contraceptive use, and reproductive health care) and 5.6.2 (number of countries with laws and regulations guaranteeing full and equal access to SRHR). Progress is uneven — some countries have strengthened legal frameworks while others have restricted access.

### Structural Challenges

**Fragmentation across goals**: SRHR is split across SDGs 3, 4, 5, 10, and others, making it difficult to present a coherent SRHR investment case or accountability framework. No single SDG "owns" SRHR the way the Global Fund "owns" the three diseases or GAVI "owns" immunisation.

**Indicator limitations**: The SDG indicator framework captures only a fraction of SRHR. There are no global indicators for abortion access, quality of maternity care, satisfaction with SRHR services, or comprehensive sexuality education coverage.

**Voluntary reporting**: The VNR process is voluntary, and countries self-select which indicators to report on. Many VNRs skip sensitive SRHR indicators entirely. UNFPA has provided technical support to improve SRHR coverage in VNRs, with mixed results.

**Financing gap**: The Guttmacher/UNFPA "Adding It Up" analysis estimates that meeting the full SRHR SDG agenda would require approximately USD 68.8 billion annually in developing countries; current spending is far below this level.

## CURRENT STATUS

- The 2023 SDG Summit (midpoint assessment) confirmed that SRHR targets are among the most off-track
- UNFPA is positioning the 2026–2029 Strategic Plan as the "final acceleration" period for SDG SRHR targets
- Discussions on the post-2030 development framework are beginning; UNFPA is advocating for SRHR to maintain or strengthen its position in whatever succeeds the SDGs
- The Secretary-General's "Pact for the Future" (2024 Summit of the Future) called for accelerated SDG implementation, including on health and gender equality
- Data gaps remain significant — many countries lack recent survey data for key SRHR indicators, making accurate progress assessment difficult
- UNFPA continues to invest in SDG indicator custodianship, capacity building for national statistical systems, and advocacy for SDG SRHR targets
