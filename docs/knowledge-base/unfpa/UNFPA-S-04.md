## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

LGBTQ+ populations face specific SRHR needs and barriers that are shaped by criminalisation, stigma, discrimination, and violence in many of the countries where UNFPA operates. Same-sex sexual activity is criminalised in approximately 64 countries (ILGA World 2023), with penalties ranging from imprisonment to death. In these contexts, LGBTQ+ individuals face barriers to accessing STI prevention and treatment, HIV services, mental health support, and protection from violence. Even in countries without criminalisation, stigma and discrimination from health providers can effectively deny SRHR services.

UNFPA's approach to LGBTQ+ inclusion is grounded in human rights principles — the right to health, non-discrimination, and freedom from violence apply to all people regardless of sexual orientation or gender identity (SOGI). In practice, UNFPA navigates this commitment cautiously: the organisation supports SRHR services for all populations, including LGBTQ+ individuals, but the degree of explicit programming and advocacy varies by country context, reflecting political sensitivities and legal environments.

HIV programming has served as the primary entry point for LGBTQ+ health engagement in the UN system. UNAIDS, UNFPA, and UNDP work jointly on HIV prevention among key populations (men who have sex with men, transgender persons, sex workers). UNFPA's Division of Labour within the Joint Programme assigns it responsibility for condom programming, STI prevention, and linking HIV with SRHR — all directly relevant to LGBTQ+ health. Beyond HIV, UNFPA addresses GBV against LGBTQ+ persons, supports inclusive CSE, and advocates for non-discriminatory health services.

## KEY FACTS

- **Criminalisation**: Same-sex sexual activity criminalised in 64 countries (ILGA World 2023); 12 countries impose or allow the death penalty
- **Violence**: LGBTQ+ persons face elevated rates of hate crimes, sexual violence, intimate partner violence, and "corrective" violence
- **Health disparities**: MSM face HIV incidence approximately 28 times higher than general male population globally; transgender women approximately 14 times higher (UNAIDS 2023)
- **Mental health**: LGBTQ+ populations show significantly higher rates of depression, anxiety, suicidality, and substance use — driven by minority stress, stigma, and discrimination
- **Transgender health**: Specific SRHR needs including hormone therapy, gender-affirming care, and reproductive options — largely unavailable in LMIC health systems
- **Intersex health**: Estimated 1.7% of births involve some degree of intersex variation; intersex persons face non-consensual medical interventions ("normalising" surgeries) with lifelong SRHR implications
- **UNAIDS Division of Labour**: UNFPA is responsible for condom programming and linking HIV with SRHR for key populations including MSM, transgender persons, and sex workers
- **Yogyakarta Principles (2006, updated 2017)**: Apply international human rights law to SOGI issues; referenced by UN human rights mechanisms but not binding
- **UN Free & Equal campaign**: OHCHR campaign for LGBTQ+ equality; UNFPA has expressed support
- **Provider stigma**: Studies across LMICs document widespread refusal of care, hostile treatment, and breaches of confidentiality by health workers toward LGBTQ+ patients

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### UNFPA's Position

UNFPA's official position is that all people have the right to access SRHR services without discrimination, consistent with the ICPD Programme of Action and international human rights law. The ICPD did not explicitly address sexual orientation or gender identity — these were too politically sensitive in 1994 — but UNFPA interprets the ICPD's rights-based framework as inclusive of all populations.

In practice, UNFPA's programming for LGBTQ+ populations varies enormously by context:
- In countries with legal protection for LGBTQ+ rights (South Africa, Argentina, Thailand, parts of Europe), UNFPA can explicitly include LGBTQ+ populations in SRHR programming
- In countries with criminalisation, UNFPA typically works through HIV programming frameworks (which provide a politically acceptable entry point for working with key populations) without explicit LGBTQ+ branding
- In some highly restrictive contexts, any engagement with LGBTQ+ issues risks political backlash that could jeopardise UNFPA's broader programme

### Political Tensions

LGBTQ+ inclusion is among the most politically contested areas in UNFPA's mandate. Conservative member states — some African, Middle Eastern, and Caribbean countries, supported by the Holy See and some evangelical Christian movements — have consistently opposed any UN reference to SOGI in intergovernmental documents. This has led to:
- Contested language in General Assembly resolutions and Human Rights Council resolutions
- Pressure on UNFPA to avoid explicit LGBTQ+ programming in certain countries
- Internal organisational tension between mandate commitments to non-discrimination and operational pragmatism

## DETAIL

### HIV as Entry Point

The HIV epidemic created an imperative for engaging with MSM, transgender persons, and sex workers regardless of legal status. UNAIDS, UNFPA, and partners have documented that criminalisation and stigma drive these populations away from health services, increasing HIV transmission. The public health argument — that reaching key populations is essential for epidemic control — has provided a framework for engaging governments that would otherwise resist any LGBTQ+ health programming.

UNFPA's specific roles include:
- **Condom programming**: Ensuring condom availability and distribution for all populations, including MSM
- **STI services**: Supporting STI screening and treatment accessible to key populations
- **SRHR-HIV linkages**: Integrating family planning, reproductive health, and HIV services for key populations
- **Community engagement**: Supporting community-based organisations of key populations

### GBV Against LGBTQ+ Persons

LGBTQ+ individuals face specific forms of GBV:
- "Corrective" rape (particularly against lesbians in some African and Caribbean countries)
- Violence from intimate partners (IPV in same-sex relationships is comparable in prevalence to heterosexual relationships but less reported and fewer services available)
- Family violence (particularly against young LGBTQ+ persons)
- State violence (police harassment, prison sexual violence)
- Hate crimes

UNFPA's GBV programming should, in principle, serve all survivors regardless of SOGI — but in practice, GBV services may not be LGBTQ+-friendly, providers may be hostile, and reporting to police in criminalising countries can result in arrest of the survivor.

### Inclusive CSE

WHO and UNESCO's International Technical Guidance on Sexuality Education includes content on sexual orientation and gender identity as a component of comprehensive CSE. UNFPA supports this framework, though implementation is contested: some countries accept SOGI content in CSE, while others explicitly prohibit it.

### Transgender-Specific SRHR

Transgender persons face specific SRHR needs that most LMIC health systems do not address:
- Gender-affirming hormone therapy (often self-administered with unregulated products due to lack of medical access)
- Reproductive options (fertility preservation before hormone therapy or surgery)
- Cancer screening appropriate to anatomical sex
- Mental health support
- Protection from violence, including in health settings

---

## CURRENT STATUS

- LGBTQ+ inclusion remains politically sensitive for UNFPA, with programming varying significantly by country context
- HIV remains the primary programmatic entry point for engaging with MSM and transgender populations
- The global trend on LGBTQ+ rights is mixed — some countries are decriminalising while others are increasing penalties (Uganda's Anti-Homosexuality Act 2023, Ghana's proposed legislation)
- UNFPA's internal policies prohibit discrimination based on SOGI in employment; the degree to which this extends to programming varies
- Young people globally are increasingly visible in LGBTQ+ identities, creating pressure for SRHR services to be inclusive
