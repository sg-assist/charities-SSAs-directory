## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Lancet — one of the world's most influential medical journals — has published several landmark series and commissions on sexual and reproductive health that have shaped global evidence, policy, and programming. These publications represent the most comprehensive and authoritative evidence syntheses in the SRHR field and have directly influenced UNFPA's strategic direction, the SDG framework, and national health policies worldwide.

The most significant Lancet SRHR publications include: the 2006 Lancet Series on Sexual and Reproductive Health (6 papers establishing the evidence base for SRHR as a field); the 2014 Lancet Commission on Investing in Health (the "Global Health 2035" report, making the economic case for health investment including SRHR); the 2018 Guttmacher-Lancet Commission on Sexual and Reproductive Health and Rights ("Accelerate Progress," defining the essential SRHR package); the 2018 Lancet Series on Maternal Health; the 2022 Lancet Commission on Gender-Based Violence and Maltreatment of Young People; and multiple Lancet papers on specific SRHR topics (midwifery, stillbirth, adolescent health).

These publications matter because: they synthesise evidence from hundreds of primary studies into actionable conclusions; they are peer-reviewed by the world's leading researchers; they carry the Lancet's institutional credibility with policymakers; and they explicitly link evidence to policy recommendations. UNFPA has used Lancet evidence to justify strategic plan priorities, design investment cases, and advocate for SRHR in intergovernmental forums.

## KEY FACTS

- **2006 Lancet Series on SRH**: 6 papers; established the comprehensive evidence case for SRHR including family planning, maternal health, STIs, abortion, and adolescent health
- **2014 Lancet Commission on Investing in Health (Global Health 2035)**: Made the "grand convergence" argument — that with sufficient investment, most LMICs could reduce maternal/child mortality to high-income levels by 2035; included SRHR as essential
- **2014 Lancet Series on Midwifery**: 4 papers and a commentary; the definitive evidence base for midwifery investment; found that midwives could deliver 87% of essential SRHR services
- **2016 Lancet Series on Stillbirths**: 5 papers; highlighted stillbirth as a neglected outcome; 2.6 million annual stillbirths linked to inadequate intrapartum care
- **2018 Guttmacher-Lancet Commission**: "Accelerate Progress — Sexual and Reproductive Health and Rights for All"; defined 9 essential SRHR service categories; costed the full package; recommended integration into UHC
- **2022 Lancet Commission on GBV**: Comprehensive review of violence against women and young people; evidence on prevention, response, and structural determinants
- **Citations**: These series and commissions are among the most-cited publications in global health; the 2006 series papers have over 10,000 combined citations
- **Policy impact**: Lancet SRHR publications have been cited in WHO guidelines, UN General Assembly resolutions, national health plans, and donor strategy documents

## DETAIL

### 2006 Lancet Series on Sexual and Reproductive Health

This series was the first comprehensive, evidence-based survey of the SRHR field published in a major medical journal. Six papers covered:
1. Sexual and reproductive health and the health sector
2. Family planning (defining the evidence for contraception's health and development impacts)
3. Maternal health (mapping causes of maternal death and effective interventions)
4. Sexually transmitted and reproductive tract infections
5. Unsafe abortion (quantifying the burden and evidence for harm reduction)
6. Advancing sexual and reproductive health and rights

The series established that SRHR was not a narrow "women's health" issue but a central component of public health requiring health system-wide investment. It influenced the post-MDG development framework debates that eventually produced the SDGs.

### 2018 Guttmacher-Lancet Commission

"Accelerate Progress — Sexual and Reproductive Health and Rights for All" was the most influential SRHR publication of the past decade. Key contributions:

**Essential SRHR package**: Defined 9 service categories that should be universally available: (1) contraceptive counselling and services; (2) maternal and newborn care; (3) safe abortion care; (4) post-abortion care; (5) STI prevention, detection, and treatment; (6) comprehensive sexuality education; (7) prevention and treatment of HIV; (8) counselling and services for sexual function and satisfaction; and (9) prevention, detection, and management of reproductive cancers and other conditions.

**Costing**: Estimated that providing the full SRHR package in developing countries would cost approximately USD 68.8 billion annually — with family planning alone at USD 12.6 billion. Current spending falls approximately USD 30 billion short.

**Integration into UHC**: Recommended that all 9 SRHR service categories be included in UHC essential benefits packages — the most comprehensive articulation of SRHR-UHC integration.

**Contested elements**: The inclusion of abortion care and comprehensive sexuality education as essential services was politically contested; some governments rejected the commission's recommendation.

### 2014 Midwifery Series

Four papers and a commentary established the evidence base for midwifery as one of the highest-impact investments in health. Key finding: if midwifery care were scaled to universal coverage, it would avert an estimated 83% of maternal deaths, neonatal deaths, and stillbirths. This finding was subsequently incorporated into UNFPA's State of the World's Midwifery reports and has been a cornerstone of UNFPA's midwifery advocacy.

### 2022 GBV Commission

The Lancet Commission on Gender-Based Violence and Maltreatment of Young People provided the most comprehensive evidence review to date on:
- Prevalence and health consequences of GBV across the life course
- Structural determinants (gender inequality, poverty, conflict, institutional failures)
- Evidence on prevention (gender-transformative programmes, legal reform, economic empowerment)
- Health system response (clinical management, MHPSS, referral)
- Calls for integrated, life-course approach to GBV prevention and response
