## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

UNFPA's total revenue has fluctuated between approximately USD 1.1 billion and USD 1.6 billion annually over the past decade, placing it among the mid-sized UN funds and programmes — significantly smaller than UNICEF (over USD 7 billion) or UNDP (over USD 5 billion) but larger than UN Women or UNAIDS. The organisation depends overwhelmingly on voluntary government contributions; it receives no assessed contributions from the UN regular budget. This makes UNFPA financially vulnerable in ways that agencies with assessed funding (WHO, ILO) are not.

The most consequential structural trend is the shift from core (regular) resources to non-core (other) resources. Core funding — which UNFPA can allocate flexibly across its mandate — has declined as a share of total revenue, from roughly 55–60 per cent a decade ago to approximately 35–40 per cent by 2023. Non-core funding, which is earmarked by donors for specific countries, themes, or emergencies, now dominates. This constrains UNFPA's ability to direct resources toward its highest-priority needs and imposes heavy transaction costs through fragmented reporting requirements.

UNFPA's donor base is concentrated among a small group of like-minded European governments — principally the Nordic countries, the Netherlands, Germany, and the United Kingdom — supplemented by significant contributions from the United States (when politically aligned administrations are in power), Japan, and a growing but modest set of middle-income and programme country contributors. The repeated US defunding episodes (under the Reagan, George W. Bush, and Trump administrations) demonstrate the fragility of this model.

This document maps UNFPA's revenue streams, donor landscape, financial trends, and the structural implications for programming and strategy.

## KEY FACTS

- **Total UNFPA revenue (2023)**: Approximately USD 1.42 billion, comprising regular resources (core) and other resources (non-core) including co-financing and trust funds
- **Core (regular) resources (2023)**: Approximately USD 480–520 million, representing roughly 35% of total revenue — a historic low as a share
- **Non-core (other) resources (2023)**: Approximately USD 900 million, predominantly earmarked by donors for specific purposes
- **Top donor (2023)**: Sweden, consistently contributing USD 80–100 million annually (combined core + non-core)
- **Other top-five donors**: Norway, Netherlands, Germany, and Denmark — each contributing USD 40–80 million annually in recent years
- **United States**: Contributed USD 32.5 million annually when funding was active; withheld all funding under the Kemp-Kasten amendment during Trump administrations (2017–2020, 2025–)
- **Number of donor governments**: Approximately 130 countries contribute, but the top 10 donors account for over 75% of total revenue
- **Comparison**: UNICEF total revenue exceeds USD 7 billion; UNDP approximately USD 5 billion; WHO approximately USD 4.5 billion; UNFPA at USD 1.4 billion is substantially smaller
- **Private sector and foundation revenue**: Small — estimated at less than 5% of total revenue
- **Programme country contributions**: Growing modestly; some countries (e.g., China, India, Thailand) contribute to UNFPA while still receiving programme support
- **Emergency/humanitarian funding**: Approximately 20–25% of total expenditure is on humanitarian response, funded primarily through non-core channels (CERF, country-based pooled funds, bilateral emergency allocations)
- **Administrative overhead**: UNFPA's management/institutional budget is approximately 10–12% of total expenditure, in line with UN system averages
- **Funding compact commitment**: Under the 2019 UN Funding Compact, member states committed to increasing core funding to at least 30% of total contributions — many have not met this target
- **Biennial support budget**: Approved by the Executive Board; covers institutional costs separate from programme delivery
- **Multi-year pledging**: Nordic donors and the Netherlands typically provide multi-year framework agreements, giving UNFPA some predictability; most other donors pledge annually

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### How UNFPA Is Funded: The Basics

UNFPA receives no assessed contributions — unlike WHO or the UN regular budget, where member states pay mandatory contributions based on a scale of assessments. UNFPA depends entirely on voluntary contributions, primarily from governments. This structure was established when UNFPA was created as a trust fund in 1967 (then the United Nations Fund for Population Activities) and has remained unchanged since.

Revenue comes in two broad categories:

1. **Regular resources (core funding)**: Un-earmarked contributions that UNFPA can allocate according to its strategic plan and resource allocation system. Core funding enables UNFPA to maintain country presence, staff, and programme flexibility. It is the financial backbone of the organisation.

2. **Other resources (non-core funding)**: Earmarked contributions designated by donors for specific countries, themes, emergencies, or programmes. This includes:
   - **Other resources — regular**: Earmarked for specific programme countries or themes
   - **Other resources — emergency**: Earmarked for humanitarian response
   - **Trust funds and co-financing**: Joint programme contributions, thematic funds, and multi-donor trust funds

### The Historical Trajectory

UNFPA's funding has grown in nominal terms over the past two decades but has been volatile in real terms.

**2000–2010**: Revenue grew from approximately USD 400 million to USD 800 million, driven by the Millennium Development Goals era and increased donor attention to reproductive health.

**2010–2015**: Revenue plateaued at around USD 950 million to USD 1.1 billion. Core contributions stagnated while non-core grew — the beginning of the "earmarking shift" that has since intensified.

**2016–2020**: Revenue fluctuated between USD 1.1 billion and USD 1.4 billion. The US defunding under the Trump administration (2017) removed approximately USD 32.5 million annually in core contributions and signalled political vulnerability. Some European donors partially compensated with increased contributions.

**2021–2024**: Revenue reached approximately USD 1.3–1.5 billion as humanitarian funding expanded (Syria, Ukraine, Afghanistan, Horn of Africa crises). However, core funding continued to erode as a share, falling below 40%.

**2025 onwards**: The second Trump-era defunding, combined with broader OECD DAC austerity pressures (UK ODA cuts, European fiscal pressures), creates a challenging environment. UNFPA has projected potential core funding shortfalls.

## DETAIL

### The Donor Landscape

#### Tier 1: Anchor Donors (Nordic Countries + Netherlands)

Sweden, Norway, Denmark, Finland, and the Netherlands have been UNFPA's most reliable and largest donors for decades. These countries share:
- Strong domestic political consensus on SRHR and population issues
- Multi-year framework agreements that provide funding predictability
- High core-to-non-core ratios (often 50% or more of their contributions are un-earmarked)
- Active engagement in UNFPA governance through the Executive Board

Sweden alone has contributed approximately USD 80–100 million annually in recent years, making it typically UNFPA's single largest donor. Norway follows at approximately USD 60–80 million.

The concentration risk is significant: if any single Nordic donor reduces contributions (as happened when Denmark temporarily cut ODA in 2016), the impact on UNFPA is disproportionate.

#### Tier 2: Major but Variable Donors (Germany, UK, Japan, Canada, US)

**Germany** has significantly increased contributions since 2015, becoming a top-five donor through both core and earmarked funding (particularly for humanitarian SRHR and GBV programming). Germany's Federal Ministry for Economic Cooperation and Development (BMZ) and the Federal Foreign Office channel funds through different mechanisms.

**United Kingdom** was historically a top-three donor through DFID but cut ODA from 0.7% to 0.5% of GNI in 2021 and reduced UNFPA funding. The merger of DFID into the Foreign, Commonwealth and Development Office (FCDO) also changed the institutional relationship.

**Japan** contributes primarily through non-core channels, often earmarked for Asia-Pacific programming. Japan's contributions are moderate in volume but strategically important for UNFPA's regional presence.

**United States**: The most politically volatile donor. When funding is active, the US contributes approximately USD 32.5 million annually — modest by the standards of US aid budgets but significant for UNFPA. Under the Kemp-Kasten amendment, any administration can withhold funding if it determines that UNFPA supports coercive population policies. Republican administrations have consistently invoked this amendment; Democratic administrations have consistently restored funding.

**Canada** increased SRHR funding significantly under the Trudeau government (2015–2025), including through the Feminist International Assistance Policy.

#### Tier 3: Emerging and Programme Country Donors

A growing number of middle-income countries contribute to UNFPA while also receiving programme support. China, India, Thailand, Turkey, and several Gulf states have contributed modest amounts. South-South cooperation funding is growing but remains a small fraction of total revenue.

#### Private Sector and Foundations

UNFPA's private sector revenue is minimal compared to UNICEF (which raises over USD 2 billion privately) or WHO. UNFPA has not built a mass-market fundraising apparatus. Foundation contributions (Gates Foundation, Packard Foundation, Hewlett Foundation) are more significant but heavily earmarked for specific programme areas (family planning, data).

### Core vs Non-Core: The Structural Challenge

The shift from core to non-core funding is the most consequential financial trend for UNFPA. In 2010, core funding represented approximately 55% of total revenue. By 2023, it had fallen to approximately 35%.

This matters because:

1. **Programming flexibility**: Core funding allows UNFPA to allocate resources based on need (using the resource allocation system that weights population, poverty, maternal mortality, and other indicators). Non-core funding goes where donors direct it — which may not align with greatest need.

2. **Country presence**: Country offices in low-population, low-profile countries depend heavily on core funding. When core shrinks, these offices face closure or downsizing, even if the SRHR needs are acute.

3. **Transaction costs**: Each earmarked contribution typically requires separate reporting, financial tracking, and donor relationship management. Managing hundreds of non-core projects imposes administrative burdens disproportionate to the funding received.

4. **Strategic coherence**: When 60–65% of funding comes with donor-specified conditions, UNFPA's strategic plan becomes aspirational rather than operational — the organisation delivers what donors fund rather than what its analysis of need dictates.

### How UNFPA Allocates Resources

UNFPA uses a resource allocation system to distribute core resources across programme countries. The formula considers:
- Population size and structure
- Gross national income per capita (inverse — poorer countries get more)
- Maternal mortality ratio
- Adolescent birth rate
- Unmet need for family planning
- Gender inequality index

This system directs approximately 60% of core programme resources to sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia — the regions with the greatest SRHR needs. However, the system only governs core allocations; non-core resources flow according to donor earmarks, which may favour different geographies (humanitarian funding to the Middle East, for example).

### UNFPA's Financial Position Relative to Its Mandate

A persistent tension in UNFPA's financial model is the mismatch between mandate scope and available resources. UNFPA's mandate — derived from the 1994 ICPD Programme of Action — covers:
- Reproductive health and rights for approximately 4.7 billion people of reproductive age
- Maternal health across 150+ programme countries
- Family planning for an estimated 218 million women with unmet need
- Gender-based violence prevention and response
- Adolescent SRHR
- Population data and census support
- Harmful practices (FGM, child marriage)

At USD 1.4 billion annually, UNFPA's per-capita spending on its target population is vanishingly small. For comparison, the Global Fund disburses approximately USD 4 billion annually for three diseases; GAVI approximately USD 2 billion for immunisation. UNFPA's mandate is broader and its funding thinner.

## CURRENT STATUS

As of early 2026, UNFPA faces a constrained funding environment:

- The second US defunding under the Trump administration (inaugurated January 2025) has again removed American contributions
- Several European donors face domestic fiscal pressures and ODA budget cuts
- Core funding continues its downward trajectory as a share of total revenue
- Humanitarian funding demands remain high due to protracted crises in Ukraine, Sudan, Gaza, Myanmar, and the Sahel
- UNFPA's incoming Strategic Plan (2026–2029) will need to address revenue diversification, including more structured private sector partnerships and innovative financing
- The UN Funding Compact's target of 30% core funding has not been met by most member states
- UNFPA has been exploring thematic pooled funds and multi-year partnership agreements to create more flexible, predictable non-core arrangements
