## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

An estimated 476 million indigenous people live in over 90 countries, representing approximately 6 per cent of the global population but 19 per cent of the extreme poor. Indigenous peoples face persistent and often severe SRHR disparities: higher maternal mortality, higher adolescent birth rates, lower access to skilled birth attendance and modern contraception, and greater exposure to gender-based violence — all compounded by discrimination, geographic isolation, linguistic barriers, and health systems that often disregard indigenous healing practices and cultural values.

The UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP, 2007) affirms indigenous peoples' right to the highest attainable standard of health (Article 24) and their right to access health services without discrimination (Article 21). The principle of free, prior and informed consent (FPIC) — central to UNDRIP — has specific implications for SRHR programming: health interventions affecting indigenous communities should be designed with, not merely for, those communities.

UNFPA's engagement with indigenous peoples is most developed in Latin America and the Caribbean, where indigenous population concentrations are largest and where intercultural health models have been pioneered (Bolivia, Ecuador, Guatemala, Mexico, Peru). In other regions — sub-Saharan Africa, Asia, the Pacific — indigenous/tribal SRHR is addressed less systematically. A consistent challenge is data: many countries do not disaggregate SRHR indicators by indigenous status, making it difficult to identify and track disparities.

The historical relationship between population programmes and indigenous peoples is fraught: the eugenics movement targeted indigenous populations; forced sterilisation campaigns in the US, Canada, Peru, and elsewhere disproportionately affected indigenous women; and family planning programmes have sometimes been imposed on indigenous communities without adequate consent. This history creates justified mistrust that contemporary SRHR programming must address through genuine partnership, cultural safety, and community ownership.

## KEY FACTS

- **Indigenous population**: Approximately 476 million across 90+ countries (ILO, based on self-identification)
- **Poverty**: Indigenous peoples are 19% of the extreme poor globally despite being 6% of population (World Bank 2021)
- **Maternal mortality**: Indigenous women in LAC countries face MMRs 2–5 times higher than non-indigenous women in the same country (PAHO data)
- **Skilled birth attendance**: In Guatemala, skilled birth attendance for indigenous women is approximately 30% vs. approximately 70% for non-indigenous (DHS)
- **Adolescent birth rate**: Indigenous adolescent girls in LAC have rates approximately 1.5–3 times higher than national averages
- **Contraceptive prevalence**: Indigenous women in many countries have modern CPR 15–30 percentage points below national averages
- **UNDRIP adoption**: 13 September 2007 (GA resolution 61/295); 144 in favour, 4 against (Australia, Canada, New Zealand, US — all later endorsed), 11 abstentions
- **FPIC**: Free, prior and informed consent is required before adopting measures that may affect indigenous peoples — including health programmes
- **Forced sterilisation history**: Documented in US (Indian Health Service sterilisation programme, 1960s–70s, affecting an estimated 25–50% of Native American women of childbearing age), Peru (approximately 300,000 women sterilised under Fujimori, 1996–2000, disproportionately indigenous), Canada (Indigenous women, documented through 2018)
- **Intercultural health models**: Bolivia (2006 Intercultural Health Law), Ecuador (constitutional recognition of intercultural health), Guatemala (intercultural birthing centres) — pioneering integration of indigenous healing with biomedical care
- **Traditional birth attendants (TBAs)**: Many indigenous communities rely on TBAs; WHO and UNFPA promote integration of TBAs into referral systems rather than replacement
- **Data gap**: Fewer than 30% of countries with significant indigenous populations disaggregate health indicators by indigenous status

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### Historical Context: Population Programmes and Indigenous Peoples

The relationship between international population programmes and indigenous peoples carries a burden of historical harm:

**Eugenics (early-mid 20th century)**: Eugenics programmes in the US, Canada, Australia, and Europe targeted indigenous peoples, persons with disabilities, and other marginalised groups for forced sterilisation. Indigenous women were deemed "unfit" parents based on racist ideologies.

**US Indian Health Service**: Between 1960 and 1976, an estimated 25–50% of Native American women of reproductive age were sterilised, often without informed consent or under coercive conditions (threat of losing welfare benefits, sterilisation during other procedures without explicit consent). A 1976 Government Accountability Office investigation confirmed the abuses.

**Peru's sterilisation campaign**: Under President Fujimori's National Programme for Reproductive Health and Family Planning (1996–2000), approximately 300,000 women and 25,000 men were sterilised, predominantly indigenous Quechua and Aymara-speaking women in rural areas. Many procedures were performed under coercion (quotas for health workers, misleading information, inadequate consent processes). Investigations have documented deaths and complications.

**Canada**: Reports of forced and coerced sterilisation of Indigenous women continued through 2017–2018, documented by the Senate Committee on Human Rights and civil society organisations.

This history creates legitimate mistrust of external SRHR programmes among indigenous communities — a context that current programming must acknowledge and address.

### UNDRIP and FPIC

UNDRIP (2007) establishes that indigenous peoples have:
- The right to the highest attainable standard of physical and mental health (Article 24)
- The right to their traditional medicines and health practices (Article 24)
- The right to be actively involved in developing health programmes affecting them (Article 23)
- The right to free, prior and informed consent before any measures affecting them (Article 19)

FPIC has specific implications for SRHR programming: family planning programmes, maternal health facility construction, CSE in indigenous schools, and GBV interventions in indigenous communities should all involve meaningful consultation and consent — not merely "community participation" as a box-checking exercise.

## DETAIL

### SRHR Disparities for Indigenous Peoples

**Maternal mortality**: The most dramatic disparity. In Guatemala, indigenous women's MMR is estimated at 3–5 times the non-indigenous rate. In Australia, Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander women have maternal mortality approximately 3 times higher than non-Indigenous women. In Panama, indigenous (Comarcas) MMR is approximately 5 times the national average.

**Contraceptive access**: Indigenous women face multiple barriers: geographic distance to health facilities, language barriers (health workers who do not speak indigenous languages), provider discrimination, cultural preferences for larger families (which must be respected, not overridden), and distrust rooted in sterilisation history. In Bolivia, indigenous women's modern CPR is approximately 20 percentage points below the national average.

**Adolescent pregnancy**: Indigenous adolescent girls in LAC, Australia, and parts of Asia have significantly higher birth rates, often linked to child marriage, limited education access, and sexual violence. In some contexts, early pregnancy is culturally normative; in others, it reflects lack of agency and opportunity.

**GBV**: Evidence on GBV among indigenous populations is limited by data gaps, but available studies (Canada's Missing and Murdered Indigenous Women and Girls inquiry, Australia's Closing the Gap reports) document elevated rates of violence, often compounded by colonialism's legacy, intergenerational trauma, poverty, and substance abuse.

### Intercultural Health Models

Several LAC countries have pioneered intercultural health approaches that attempt to integrate indigenous healing practices with biomedical care:

**Bolivia**: The 2006 Intercultural Community Family Health Model (SAFCI) recognises traditional medicine as complementary to biomedical care. UNFPA has supported intercultural maternity homes (Casas Maternas) where indigenous women can give birth with traditional birth attendants alongside biomedical providers.

**Ecuador**: The 2008 constitution recognises the right to intercultural health. Indigenous birthing practices (vertical delivery position, presence of family members, use of traditional teas) are accommodated in public maternity facilities.

**Guatemala**: Intercultural birthing centres in indigenous areas integrate traditional midwives (comadronas) with facility-based care, improving the cultural acceptability of facility delivery while maintaining emergency referral capacity.

### UNFPA's Engagement

UNFPA's work with indigenous peoples is most developed in LAC through LACRO, which has:
- Supported intercultural maternal health programmes in Guatemala, Bolivia, Ecuador, Peru, and Mexico
- Generated data on indigenous SRHR disparities
- Advocated for disaggregated data collection by indigenous status
- Partnered with indigenous women's organisations
- Developed guidance on culturally appropriate SRHR services

In other regions, UNFPA's engagement with indigenous/tribal populations is less systematic, though relevant work exists in: India (tribal health programmes), Philippines (Indigenous Peoples Rights Act), and Australia (Aboriginal health).

## CURRENT STATUS

- Indigenous SRHR disparities remain acute in LAC, with slower progress than national averages
- Intercultural health models are expanding but face sustainability challenges (funding, trained personnel, political commitment)
- Data disaggregation by indigenous status is improving but remains unavailable in most countries outside LAC
- The forced sterilisation legacy continues to shape indigenous communities' relationship with SRHR programmes — trust-building is essential
- UNFPA's 2022–2025 Strategic Plan includes "leaving no one behind" commitments that apply to indigenous peoples, but implementation is uneven
- Climate change disproportionately affects indigenous communities, adding a new dimension to indigenous SRHR vulnerability
