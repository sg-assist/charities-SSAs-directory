## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Telemedicine — the delivery of health services at a distance through telecommunications technology — expanded dramatically during the COVID-19 pandemic and has become an increasingly important modality for SRHR service delivery in settings where face-to-face consultations are difficult, dangerous, or impossible. Applications relevant to UNFPA's mandate include teleconsultation for antenatal care, remote family planning counselling, self-care interventions for reproductive health (self-injectable contraceptives, self-managed medical abortion, HIV self-testing), and telehealth platforms connecting community health workers with specialist guidance.

WHO published its first consolidated guideline on self-care interventions for SRHR in 2019 (updated 2022), recognising self-care as a complement to facility-based services that can expand access, improve autonomy, and reach populations underserved by health systems. Self-care is distinct from telemedicine but closely related — many self-care interventions are enabled or supported by remote communication (information, follow-up, referral).

The COVID-19 experience demonstrated both the potential and limitations of telemedicine for SRHR. In many countries, ANC visits were conducted by phone, family planning counselling moved to teleconsultation, and GBV hotlines became primary access points. Some of these innovations persisted post-pandemic; others reverted to pre-COVID modalities as restrictions lifted. The fundamental barriers to telemedicine — connectivity, device access, digital literacy, regulatory frameworks, and the irreducible need for physical examination and procedures — constrain its applicability, particularly for the populations UNFPA most serves.

## KEY FACTS

- **COVID-19 acceleration**: Telemedicine use increased by approximately 300–400% during the pandemic period (2020–2021) across many health systems; SRHR telemedicine expanded but less than other specialties
- **WHO self-care guideline (2019, updated 2022)**: Recommends self-injectable contraceptives (DMPA-SC/Sayana Press), HIV self-testing, self-sampling for HPV, home-based ovulation predictor kits, and self-management of medical abortion (with facility access for complications) — all as options within a quality health system
- **DMPA-SC (Sayana Press)**: Subcutaneous injectable contraceptive designed for self-injection; now available in over 40 countries; UNFPA procures and distributes; evidence from PATH/UNFPA trials shows high acceptability and correct self-injection rates above 95%
- **Self-managed medical abortion**: WHO recommends self-management of medical abortion using mifepristone + misoprostol with information/support but without requiring facility-based administration (in the first 12 weeks); legal and regulatory access varies enormously
- **ANC teleconsultation**: Evidence from COVID-era studies shows comparable satisfaction but potential for missed clinical findings (blood pressure, fundal height, proteinuria) that require in-person assessment
- **Regulatory barriers**: Most LMICs lack telemedicine regulatory frameworks; licensing, liability, prescribing at a distance, and data protection are often unaddressed
- **Internet access**: Approximately 2.6 billion people remain offline globally; video-based telemedicine requires bandwidth unavailable in many rural LMIC settings
- **Task-shifting**: Telemedicine enables task-shifting — community health workers conducting consultations with remote specialist guidance — expanding effective workforce capacity
- **Provider acceptance**: Studies find mixed provider acceptance of telemedicine for SRHR; providers express concern about quality, liability, and inability to perform physical examinations
- **Digital divide**: Women in LMICs are less likely than men to have smartphone access, internet connectivity, and digital literacy — limiting telemedicine reach to those who need it most

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### Self-Care as a Paradigm

WHO's self-care approach represents a conceptual shift: rather than requiring every SRHR interaction to occur in a health facility with a provider, some interventions can be safely and effectively self-managed by individuals — with health system backup for complications, follow-up, and referral.

The self-care SRHR interventions with the strongest evidence include:
1. **Self-injectable contraception (DMPA-SC)**: Subcutaneous injection every three months; simple technique; high user acceptability; eliminates need for facility visits for injectable contraception
2. **HIV self-testing**: Oral fluid or blood-based rapid diagnostic tests; WHO-recommended since 2016; widely deployed
3. **HPV self-sampling**: Vaginal self-sampling for HPV screening; comparable sensitivity to provider-collected samples; increases screening coverage
4. **Home pregnancy tests**: Long-established and widely available
5. **Self-management of medical abortion**: Mifepristone + misoprostol taken at home; effective and safe in the first trimester; WHO recommended; legal access varies

### COVID-19 and SRHR Telemedicine

The pandemic forced rapid adaptation:
- **ANC**: Phone and video consultations replaced in-person visits during lockdowns. Some countries (UK, India, Brazil) developed formal guidance on remote ANC. Evidence suggests that hybrid models (some in-person visits for clinical assessment + some remote visits for counselling and follow-up) may improve attendance while maintaining quality.
- **Family planning**: Teleconsultation for FP counselling, prescription, and delivery/collection of contraceptives. Self-injectable DMPA-SC became particularly valuable — women could self-inject without visiting facilities.
- **GBV**: As domestic violence increased during lockdowns, phone and online counselling became critical. UNFPA-supported GBV hotlines saw increased call volumes across multiple countries.
- **Post-pandemic**: Some innovations persisted (DMPA-SC self-injection programmes have continued to scale), while others (routine ANC by phone) largely reverted as health systems reopened.

## DETAIL

### Applications in UNFPA Programming

**Self-injectable contraception**: UNFPA has been a major procurer and distributor of DMPA-SC/Sayana Press, working with PATH, governments, and NGO partners to train women in self-injection. The programme has scaled to over 40 countries with evidence of increased FP access, continuation rates, and user satisfaction. This is one of the most successful self-care innovations in SRHR.

**Telemedicine for ANC**: UNFPA has supported teleconsultation pilots for ANC in several countries, particularly during COVID-19. The evidence supports hybrid models where initial assessment, ultrasound, and third-trimester visits are in-person, while interim consultations on nutrition, birth planning, and danger sign awareness are conducted remotely.

**Pharmacy-based SRHR**: Expanding access through trained pharmacists and drug shop operators — who receive guidance via phone or digital tools — is a form of decentralised telemedicine. UNFPA supports pharmacy-based FP delivery in several countries, with pharmacists providing injectable contraceptives, emergency contraception, and SRHR counselling.

**Community health worker support**: CHWs in remote areas use mobile phones to consult with supervising clinicians about complex cases — effectively extending specialist reach through task-shifting and telemedicine. This model is used in UNFPA-supported midwifery and FP programmes.

### Limitations

**Physical examination**: Many SRHR services require physical assessment — fundal height measurement, cervical examination, fetal heart rate, blood pressure, urine analysis, fistula repair, IUD insertion, implant placement. Telemedicine cannot replace these functions.

**Procedure-based services**: Contraceptive implant insertion/removal, IUD placement, surgical management of obstetric complications, C-section, and clinical management of rape all require in-person provider contact.

**Regulatory gaps**: Most LMICs lack clear legal frameworks for telemedicine, including: Can a provider prescribe via phone? Who is liable for adverse outcomes during teleconsultation? How are data protection and confidentiality ensured? These gaps create uncertainty for both providers and clients.

**Quality concerns**: Without standardised protocols for SRHR telemedicine, quality varies enormously. A phone call to a busy health worker who provides brief, non-evidence-based advice is not equivalent to a structured teleconsultation with decision support tools.

---

## CURRENT STATUS

- Self-injectable contraception (DMPA-SC) is the most successful SRHR self-care innovation, scaling rapidly across SSA and South Asia
- Telemedicine for ANC and FP is being integrated into some health systems (India's eSanjeevani, Bangladesh's telemedicine programme) but remains sporadic in most UNFPA programme countries
- WHO self-care guidelines continue to expand, with new recommendations expected on additional self-care interventions
- Regulatory frameworks for telemedicine are slowly developing in LMICs, often accelerated by COVID-19 emergency provisions that are now being formalised
- UNFPA's positioning on self-care is aligned with WHO: self-care as a complement to (not replacement for) quality facility-based services, expanding choice and autonomy
