## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

UNFPA's mandate intersects most directly with two Sustainable Development Goals: SDG 3 (Ensure healthy lives and promote well-being for all at all ages) and SDG 5 (Achieve gender equality and empower all women and girls). Together, these goals contain the specific targets and indicators that define the international community's SRHR commitments through 2030. UNFPA serves as custodian or co-custodian agency for key indicators under both goals, making it responsible for compiling global data and reporting on progress.

SDG 3's SRHR-relevant targets include 3.1 (reduce maternal mortality to below 70/100,000), 3.7 (ensure universal access to SRHR services by 2030), and the broader target 3.8 (achieve universal health coverage, including SRHR). SDG 5's relevant targets include 5.2 (eliminate all forms of violence against women and girls), 5.3 (eliminate harmful practices including child marriage and FGM), and 5.6 (ensure universal access to sexual and reproductive health and reproductive rights).

The current trajectory shows that most of these targets will not be met by 2030 at the current pace of progress. Maternal mortality reduction has stalled globally since 2015. Unmet need for family planning persists at over 200 million women. Child marriage rates are declining but too slowly. GBV prevalence remains essentially unchanged at approximately one in three women globally. This document analyses the targets, their indicators, progress assessment, and implications for UNFPA's programming and advocacy.

## KEY FACTS

- **SDG 3.1.1 (MMR)**: Target <70/100,000 by 2030; current global estimate approximately 223/100,000 (2020) — no decline since 2015
- **SDG 3.1.2 (skilled birth attendance)**: Global average approximately 83%; approximately 56% in LDCs
- **SDG 3.7.1 (FP demand satisfied)**: Global approximately 77% with modern methods; target is universal access
- **SDG 3.7.2 (adolescent birth rate)**: Global approximately 42/1,000 (ages 15–19); approximately 98/1,000 in SSA
- **SDG 5.2.1 (IPV prevalence)**: Approximately 26% of ever-partnered women aged 15–49 have experienced physical/sexual IPV (WHO 2021)
- **SDG 5.3.1 (child marriage <18)**: Approximately 19% globally; approximately 35% in SSA; approximately 28% in South Asia (declining)
- **SDG 5.3.2 (FGM prevalence)**: Approximately 200+ million girls and women globally; prevalence declining in most countries but absolute numbers may increase due to population growth
- **SDG 5.6.1 (women's decision-making on SRHR)**: Only 57% of women aged 15–49 in 68 surveyed countries can make their own decisions on all three dimensions (sexual relations, contraception, health care)
- **SDG 5.6.2 (legal frameworks for SRHR)**: Measured through national laws guaranteeing SRHR access; no country has full legal protection across all components
- **UNFPA custodianship**: UNFPA is custodian for indicators 3.1.1, 3.1.2 (co-custodian with WHO), 5.3.1, 5.3.2, 5.6.1, and 5.6.2
- **2023 SDG midpoint assessment**: Found only 15% of SDG targets globally are on track; SRHR targets among those most off-track in LDCs

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### The MDG-to-SDG Transition for SRHR

The Millennium Development Goals (2000–2015) included MDG 5 (Improve Maternal Health) with two targets: reduce MMR by 75% (Target 5.A) and achieve universal access to reproductive health (Target 5.B). The inclusion of Target 5.B was itself an achievement — it was added only in 2007 after sustained advocacy, because the original 2000 MDG framework excluded family planning entirely.

The SDG framework (2015–2030) expanded SRHR coverage significantly:
- Maternal health retained under SDG 3.1
- SRHR services given a dedicated target under SDG 3.7
- SRHR and reproductive rights given a dedicated target under SDG 5.6
- Harmful practices (child marriage, FGM) given dedicated target under SDG 5.3
- Violence against women given dedicated target under SDG 5.2

What was NOT included in the SDGs (due to political negotiation):
- Explicit reference to abortion access
- Explicit reference to comprehensive sexuality education
- Explicit reference to sexual orientation and gender identity
- A standalone SRHR goal (SRHR was distributed across two goals)

## DETAIL

### SDG 3: Health Targets

**Target 3.1 — Maternal mortality**: "By 2030, reduce the global maternal mortality ratio to less than 70 per 100,000 live births." The global MMR was approximately 342/100,000 in 2000, declined to approximately 227/100,000 by 2015, and was estimated at 223/100,000 in 2020 — essentially stagnant in the SDG period. At current trends, the 2030 target is unachievable at the global level. However, some individual countries (notably Rwanda, Ethiopia, Cambodia) have achieved dramatic reductions, demonstrating that progress is possible with sustained investment.

**Target 3.7 — SRHR services**: "By 2030, ensure universal access to sexual and reproductive health-care services, including for family planning, information and education, and the integration of reproductive health into national strategies and programmes." This is measured through: (3.7.1) proportion of women with demand for FP satisfied with modern methods, and (3.7.2) adolescent birth rate. Global progress has been modest — met need for modern contraception has improved from approximately 75% to 77% globally, but the gap to "universal access" remains large, particularly in SSA.

**Target 3.8 — UHC**: Universal health coverage is a broader target, but its relevance to SRHR is critical — whether SRHR services are included in essential health packages determines whether UHC advances or undermines reproductive rights.

### SDG 5: Gender Equality Targets

**Target 5.2 — Violence against women**: "Eliminate all forms of violence against all women and girls in the public and private spheres, including trafficking and sexual and other types of exploitation." Measured through IPV prevalence and non-partner sexual violence. WHO's 2021 global estimates found that approximately 26% of ever-partnered women have experienced physical/sexual IPV — essentially unchanged from previous estimates. The 2030 "elimination" target is aspirational rather than achievable.

**Target 5.3 — Harmful practices**: "Eliminate all harmful practices, such as child, early and forced marriage and female genital mutilation." Child marriage (measured as proportion married before 18) has declined from approximately 25% to 19% globally, but population growth means absolute numbers of child brides may not decrease in high-prevalence regions. FGM prevalence has declined in most practising countries but not fast enough for 2030 elimination.

**Target 5.6 — SRHR access and reproductive rights**: "Ensure universal access to sexual and reproductive health and reproductive rights as agreed in accordance with the Programme of Action of the International Conference on Population and Development and the Beijing Platform for Action and the outcome documents of their review conferences." The reference to "as agreed" was a negotiated phrase reflecting political compromise on the scope of reproductive rights.

### Measurement Challenges

**Data frequency**: Key SRHR indicators depend on household surveys (DHS, MICS) conducted every 3–5 years. Many countries lack recent survey data, making accurate progress assessment impossible. The SDG framework assumes annual reporting, but SRHR data is inherently episodic.

**Indicator limitations**: The selected indicators capture only a fraction of SRHR. There is no SDG indicator for quality of maternal care, satisfaction with SRHR services, abortion access, CSE coverage, or SRHR for marginalised populations (persons with disabilities, LGBTQ+, sex workers).

**Disaggregation**: The "leave no one behind" principle requires data disaggregated by income, age, disability, ethnicity, and geography. Most countries cannot provide this level of disaggregation for SRHR indicators.

## CURRENT STATUS

- The 2023 SDG midpoint assessment confirmed that SRHR targets are among the most off-track globally
- UNFPA is positioning the 2026–2029 period as a "final acceleration" window
- Post-2030 framework discussions are beginning — UNFPA advocates for maintaining and strengthening SRHR targets
- The gap between SDG commitments and actual financing remains enormous
- UNFPA continues to invest in data systems, indicator custodianship, and capacity building for national SDG monitoring
