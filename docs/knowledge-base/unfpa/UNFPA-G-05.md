## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Eastern Europe and Central Asia (EECA) presents a distinctive set of challenges for UNFPA's mandate. Unlike sub-Saharan Africa or South Asia, where high fertility and maternal mortality dominate, EECA is characterised by below-replacement fertility, population decline, ageing, and gender-biased sex selection in the Caucasus — alongside persistent GBV, limited youth SRHR access, and the health legacies of post-Soviet health system transitions. The Ukraine conflict (2022–) has added a major humanitarian dimension, displacing millions and disrupting SRHR service delivery.

UNFPA's Eastern Europe and Central Asia Regional Office (EECARO), based in Istanbul, oversees programming in approximately 18 countries — from Turkey and the Caucasus (Armenia, Azerbaijan, Georgia) through Central Asia (Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan, Turkmenistan, Uzbekistan) to Eastern Europe (Ukraine, Moldova, Belarus) and the Western Balkans (Albania, Bosnia and Herzegovina, North Macedonia, Serbia, Kosovo). Several countries in the region have "graduated" from UNFPA programme support as incomes have risen, while others maintain active country programmes.

The region's SRHR challenges are nuanced: contraceptive prevalence appears moderate on paper but relies heavily on traditional methods (withdrawal) and reflects limited method choice. Abortion rates are historically high (the Soviet legacy of abortion as the primary family planning method has diminished but not disappeared). GBV is prevalent but often normalised. Young people face barriers to SRHR information and services. And demographic anxiety — governments' concern about low fertility and population decline — can create tensions with UNFPA's rights-based approach, as some governments pursue pro-natalist policies that may conflict with reproductive autonomy.

## KEY FACTS

- **Regional population**: Approximately 240 million across EECA countries
- **TFR range**: From 1.2 (Ukraine, Moldova, Bosnia) to 3.4 (Tajikistan); many countries below 1.5
- **MMR**: Generally low by global standards — regional average approximately 18/100,000; highest in Kyrgyzstan (50), Turkmenistan (55), Tajikistan (17); lowest in Belarus (1), Czech Republic-adjacent countries
- **Contraceptive methods**: High reliance on traditional methods in some countries — withdrawal accounts for 30–50% of contraceptive use in Turkey, Azerbaijan, and Albania
- **Abortion**: Rates have declined substantially since 1990s but remain above global averages in some countries; legacy of abortion as primary FP method persists
- **Gender-biased sex selection**: Sex ratio at birth in Azerbaijan approximately 115 boys/100 girls, Armenia approximately 110 — among the most skewed globally outside China and India
- **GBV prevalence**: Intimate partner violence lifetime prevalence ranges from approximately 20% (Serbia) to over 35% (Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan, Turkey); honour-based violence in parts of the Caucasus and Central Asia
- **Child marriage**: Elevated in Central Asia (Tajikistan, Kyrgyzstan) and parts of the Caucasus and Turkey
- **Ukraine conflict**: Over 6 million internally displaced, 6+ million refugees; UNFPA humanitarian response providing SRHR and GBV services
- **Roma population**: Approximately 10–12 million in Europe; face severe SRHR disparities (higher MMR, lower CPR, lower SBA, forced sterilisation history in Czech Republic and Slovakia)
- **HIV**: Concentrated epidemic among key populations (people who inject drugs, sex workers, MSM) in Ukraine, Russia, and Central Asia; SRHR-HIV integration is a priority
- **UNFPA country office transitions**: Several countries (Russia, Czech Republic, Romania) have transitioned out of UNFPA programme support

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### The Soviet Health Legacy

Most EECA countries inherited Soviet-era health systems that shaped SRHR in distinctive ways:
- **Abortion as contraception**: The Soviet Union was the first country to legalise abortion (1920), and abortion became the dominant method of fertility regulation. The contraceptive revolution that Western Europe experienced in the 1960s–1970s arrived much later in the Soviet bloc.
- **Medicalised maternity care**: Soviet maternity care was highly medicalised, with near-universal facility delivery but authoritarian provider-patient relationships, limited pain management, separation of mothers and newborns, and exclusion of partners from delivery rooms.
- **Limited modern contraception**: Hormonal contraception was unavailable or mistrusted in most Soviet bloc countries; IUDs were used but with older designs.

Since 1991, EECA countries have transitioned — unevenly — toward modern SRHR service delivery. UNFPA has played a significant role in introducing modern contraceptives, training providers in evidence-based maternity care, and supporting policy reform. Abortion rates have declined dramatically (Russia's abortion rate fell from approximately 100/1,000 women in 1990 to approximately 15/1,000 by 2022), reflecting improved contraceptive access.

### Demographic Anxiety and Pro-Natalism

Several EECA governments — Russia, Hungary, Poland, Georgia, Serbia — have adopted explicit pro-natalist policies motivated by concern about population decline: cash bonuses for births, extended parental leave, restrictions on abortion access, and anti-immigration rhetoric framing population loss as a national security threat.

This creates a tension with UNFPA's rights-based mandate. UNFPA supports reproductive choice — including the choice to have few or no children. Pro-natalist governments may view UNFPA's family planning programming as working against their demographic goals. UNFPA navigates this by emphasising that: rights-based approaches are more effective than coercion; family-friendly policies (childcare, parental leave) can increase fertility when women want more children but face structural barriers; and population policies must respect individual autonomy.

## DETAIL

### Ukraine Humanitarian Response

The Russian invasion of Ukraine (February 2022) created Europe's largest humanitarian crisis since World War II. UNFPA's response includes:
- **SRHR services**: Maternity care for displaced women, reproductive health kits, mobile health units serving conflict-affected areas
- **GBV response**: Safe spaces, hotlines, case management; documentation of conflict-related sexual violence
- **Scale**: UNFPA's Ukraine response is one of its largest humanitarian operations, with annual appeals exceeding USD 60 million
- **Challenges**: Access to front-line areas, destruction of maternity facilities, disrupted supply chains, staff safety

### Gender-Biased Sex Selection in the Caucasus

Azerbaijan and Armenia have among the most skewed sex ratios at birth globally (115 and 110 boys per 100 girls respectively, compared to the natural ratio of approximately 105). This reflects deep-rooted son preference combined with access to prenatal sex determination technology and sex-selective abortion.

UNFPA has been a lead agency on this issue, supporting:
- Research and data to document the problem
- Community awareness campaigns challenging son preference
- Policy advocacy for regulation of prenatal sex disclosure
- Programmes to promote the value of girls

Progress has been modest. Azerbaijan's sex ratio has improved slightly but remains significantly skewed.

### Central Asia

UNFPA's Central Asian programming addresses:
- **Maternal health**: Kyrgyzstan and Turkmenistan have the region's highest MMR; UNFPA supports EmONC, midwifery, and maternal death surveillance
- **Child marriage**: Bride kidnapping (ala kachuu) persists in Kyrgyzstan despite legal prohibition; early marriage is common in Tajikistan
- **GBV**: Domestic violence laws exist but enforcement is weak; UNFPA supports services and advocacy
- **Family planning**: Method mix remains limited in some countries; UNFPA supports access to modern methods

## CURRENT STATUS

- Ukraine remains UNFPA's largest humanitarian operation in the EECA region
- Several EECA countries are transitioning or have transitioned out of UNFPA programme support as incomes rise, though GBV, demographic challenges, and Roma inclusion remain relevant
- Pro-natalist policy trends in some countries create ongoing tension with reproductive rights frameworks
- Gender-biased sex selection in the Caucasus shows slow improvement but remains a challenge
- Central Asian countries face moderate but persistent SRHR challenges, with UNFPA maintaining active country programmes
- The HIV epidemic among key populations in EECA requires continued SRHR-HIV integration
