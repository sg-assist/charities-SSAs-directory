## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

UNFPA employs a results-based management (RBM) system centred on the Integrated Results and Resources Framework (IRRF), which links the organisation's strategic plan outcomes to measurable indicators, resource allocations, and accountability mechanisms. The IRRF is the backbone of UNFPA's performance reporting to the Executive Board and to donors, translating the three transformative results — ending preventable maternal deaths, ending unmet need for family planning, and ending gender-based violence and harmful practices — into a cascade of outcomes, outputs, and indicators.

The quality of UNFPA's RBM has improved significantly over the past decade. MOPAN assessments have noted progress in results architecture, data quality, and use of evidence. However, fundamental challenges persist: attribution is inherently difficult in population-level SRHR programming where UNFPA is one contributor among many; indicator selection tends to favour what is measurable over what matters; country-level results data quality varies enormously; and the pressure to demonstrate "results" can create perverse incentives toward easily counted outputs rather than meaningful systemic change.

This document maps UNFPA's RBM system, its strengths and weaknesses, external assessments, and the ongoing tension between accountability demands and the realities of complex social change programming.

---

## KEY FACTS

- **IRRF structure**: Organised around UNFPA's three transformative results, with outcome-level indicators (population-level changes), output-level indicators (UNFPA's direct contributions), and efficiency indicators
- **Number of indicators**: The 2022–2025 Strategic Plan IRRF contains approximately 30 outcome indicators and 40+ output indicators
- **Reporting frequency**: Annual reporting to the Executive Board through the Executive Director's Annual Report; midterm review at the two-year mark
- **Data sources**: DHS/MICS surveys (for population-level indicators), administrative data from country offices, programme monitoring data, and independent evaluations
- **MOPAN assessment (2021–22)**: Rated UNFPA as "satisfactory" overall, with strengths in strategic direction and partnerships, and weaknesses in cross-cutting issues integration and knowledge management
- **Evaluation coverage**: UNFPA's Evaluation Office conducts approximately 8–12 evaluations annually (country programme, thematic, joint)
- **Attribution challenge**: UNFPA contributes to national SRHR outcomes alongside governments, WHO, UNICEF, bilateral donors, NGOs, and the private sector — isolating UNFPA's specific contribution is methodologically complex
- **Output vs outcome tension**: Donors and the Board want measurable results; the most meaningful changes (maternal mortality reduction, norm change on GBV) take decades and cannot be attributed to a single agency
- **Data quality**: Country offices self-report output data, with quality assurance processes but acknowledged inconsistencies across offices; population-level data depends on national survey cycles (DHS every 3–5 years)

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### The Evolution of RBM in UNFPA

UNFPA adopted results-based management in the early 2000s, following the broader UN system shift from input-based to results-based programming. The 2008–2011 Strategic Plan introduced the first comprehensive results framework. Each subsequent strategic plan has refined the IRRF, improving indicator definitions, baseline data, and target-setting.

The 2014–2017 Strategic Plan was significant for establishing the "three transformative results" as the organising framework — a simplification that improved communication and strategic focus but also narrowed the results architecture around three themes at the expense of other important areas (population data, adolescent development, human rights).

The 2018–2021 Strategic Plan expanded the IRRF to include "acceleration" indicators — measuring the pace of change, not just the level — in recognition that achieving the SDGs by 2030 requires not just progress but accelerated progress.

The 2022–2025 Strategic Plan further refined the framework with attention to "leaving no one behind" disaggregation (by age, disability, geography, wealth quintile) and humanitarian-development nexus indicators.

### How the IRRF Works in Practice

**Outcome indicators** measure population-level changes in UNFPA's areas of work: maternal mortality ratio, contraceptive prevalence rate, adolescent birth rate, proportion of women who have experienced GBV, prevalence of FGM, child marriage rates. These indicators come from national surveys (DHS, MICS) and inter-agency estimation processes (WHO/UNFPA/World Bank/UNICEF maternal mortality estimates). UNFPA reports on these but cannot claim sole credit for changes — they reflect the combined efforts of governments, multiple development partners, and societal trends.

**Output indicators** measure UNFPA's direct contributions: number of women delivering in UNFPA-supported facilities with skilled birth attendants; couple-years of protection from UNFPA-procured contraceptives; number of health workers trained in EmONC; number of GBV survivors receiving services in UNFPA-supported spaces; number of countries with costed family planning plans. These are more directly attributable to UNFPA but carry their own measurement challenges (counting trainees does not guarantee improved practice; procuring contraceptives does not guarantee they reach the intended users).

**Efficiency indicators** track UNFPA's internal performance: programme expenditure rates, management-to-programme cost ratios, audit recommendation implementation rates.

## DETAIL

### Strengths of UNFPA's RBM System

**Clear strategic focus**: The three transformative results provide a simple, communicable framework that aligns the entire organisation. Staff at all levels can articulate UNFPA's goals, and the results chain from country-level outputs to global outcomes is logically coherent.

**Improving data quality**: Successive MOPAN assessments have noted improvements in UNFPA's data systems, indicator definitions, and quality assurance processes. The organisation has invested in its Strategic Information System (SIS) for programme monitoring and reporting.

**Independent evaluation function**: The Evaluation Office's independence from management (it reports to the Executive Board) provides a credible external check on self-reported results. Evaluation findings have informed strategic plan design and programme adjustments.

**Transparency**: UNFPA publishes detailed results data in its annual report, supplementary statistical annexes, and the UNFPA Data Portal. This transparency enables external scrutiny and builds donor confidence.

### Weaknesses and Challenges

**Attribution problem**: The fundamental challenge is that UNFPA's ultimate goals — reducing maternal mortality, ending unmet need for family planning, eliminating GBV and harmful practices — depend on factors far beyond UNFPA's control: government health spending, political will, cultural norms, economic development, conflict and instability. UNFPA can demonstrate plausible contribution but not definitive attribution. This is not unique to UNFPA; it is inherent to development programming. But it creates a persistent gap between what donors want to hear ("your funding saved X lives") and what honest evidence can support ("our funding contributed to a system that saved lives, along with many other factors").

**Measurability bias**: The IRRF tends to privilege indicators that can be counted — women reached, providers trained, commodities distributed, safe spaces established — over outcomes that matter more but are harder to measure: quality of care, changes in social norms, strengthened health systems, enhanced agency and empowerment. This is a recognised limitation; UNFPA has experimented with qualitative and mixed-methods indicators but these remain marginal in the results framework.

**Survey dependency**: Population-level indicators depend on household surveys (DHS, MICS) that are conducted every 3–5 years. This means UNFPA often reports outcome data that is several years old, creating a disconnect between current programming and available results evidence. In countries without recent surveys (many fragile states), outcome data may be a decade old.

**Self-reporting risks**: Output data is primarily reported by country offices, which have both the information and the incentive to present results positively. Quality assurance processes exist (regional office review, headquarters validation, spot checks) but cannot fully eliminate reporting bias. Independent evaluations have occasionally found discrepancies between reported and verified outputs.

**Aggregation challenges**: UNFPA aggregates country-level data into global totals (e.g., "X million women and girls reached"), but these aggregated figures can obscure important variation. Reaching 100,000 women in a well-functioning health system is qualitatively different from reaching 100,000 women in a conflict-affected context, but both count the same in global totals.

### External Assessments

**MOPAN 2021–22**: The most comprehensive external assessment rated UNFPA as "satisfactory" across most dimensions. Key findings:
- Strong strategic vision and mandate clarity
- Improved results framework and reporting
- Good partnership and coordination
- Weaknesses in cross-cutting issues (disability, leave no one behind)
- Knowledge management needs strengthening
- Humanitarian-development nexus programming needs clearer results frameworks

**JIU reviews**: Joint Inspection Unit reports on RBM across the UN system have included UNFPA, generally finding it in the middle of the performance range — better than some agencies but not at the frontier of RBM practice.

**Donor assessments**: Major donors conduct their own assessments of UNFPA performance. The UK's former DFID (now FCDO) multilateral development reviews and Australia's ODE assessments have generally rated UNFPA as adequate but with room for improvement, particularly on value for money evidence and outcome-level results demonstration.

## CURRENT STATUS

- UNFPA's 2022–2025 Strategic Plan midterm review (2024) assessed progress against the IRRF and identified areas requiring acceleration
- The 2026–2029 Strategic Plan development process includes significant consultation on results framework design, with attention to measuring transformative change rather than just service delivery outputs
- Digital data systems (including real-time monitoring dashboards) are being piloted in several country offices
- The Evaluation Office is expanding its use of synthesis evaluations to draw cross-cutting lessons rather than only programme-specific findings
- Pressure from donors for "value for money" evidence continues to increase, requiring UNFPA to invest more in cost-effectiveness analysis alongside traditional results reporting
