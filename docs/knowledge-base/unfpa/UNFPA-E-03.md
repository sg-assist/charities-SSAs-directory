## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Measuring SRHR outcomes is essential for accountability, programme improvement, and resource allocation — but it is also fraught with methodological challenges. The core SRHR indicators (maternal mortality ratio, contraceptive prevalence rate, met need for family planning, adolescent birth rate, skilled birth attendance, GBV prevalence, FGM prevalence, child marriage rates) each carry specific measurement difficulties related to data sources, definitions, frequency, and interpretive complexity. UNFPA, as custodian agency for several SDG indicators and a major user of SRHR data for programme planning, must navigate these challenges while producing credible evidence for the Executive Board, donors, and national partners.

Data sources for SRHR indicators fall into three main categories: household surveys (DHS, MICS), administrative/facility data (HMIS, CRVS), and modelled estimates (inter-agency estimation groups). Each has strengths and limitations. Surveys provide population-level data with methodological rigour but are conducted infrequently (every 3–5 years); administrative data is continuous but often incomplete and subject to reporting bias; and modelled estimates fill gaps but introduce model uncertainty.

This document maps the key SRHR indicators, their data sources, measurement challenges, and implications for UNFPA's monitoring and evaluation work.

## KEY FACTS

- **DHS (Demographic and Health Surveys)**: Conducted in over 90 countries; the gold standard for population-level SRHR data; funded primarily by USAID; sample sizes typically 5,000–30,000 households; conducted every 3–5 years
- **MICS (Multiple Indicator Cluster Surveys)**: UNICEF-supported; conducted in over 100 countries; includes SRHR indicators; increasingly harmonised with DHS
- **HMIS**: Health Management Information Systems (primarily DHIS2) provide continuous facility-based data on service delivery (ANC visits, deliveries, FP clients); limitation: only captures facility users, not population-level coverage
- **CRVS**: Civil Registration and Vital Statistics — the preferred source for birth and death data; incomplete in many LMICs (only approximately 75% of births globally are registered)
- **Inter-agency estimation groups**: WHO/UNFPA/UNICEF/World Bank/UNDESA produce modelled estimates for maternal mortality (MMEIG), contraceptive use (UNPD), and other indicators where country data is insufficient
- **Maternal mortality estimation**: The most methodologically complex SRHR indicator; direct measurement requires complete death registration and cause-of-death attribution; most LMICs rely on survey-based estimates or modelling
- **GBV measurement**: Prevalence data comes primarily from specialised surveys (WHO multi-country study, DHS domestic violence module); underreporting is severe; comparing across countries and time is methodologically challenging
- **Unmet need definition**: Has been revised multiple times (Westoff, Bradley revised definition); current DHS definition includes women who are fecund, sexually active, not using contraception, and not wanting a birth within 2 years — contested conceptually and practically

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### Why Measurement Matters

SRHR indicators serve multiple purposes: global accountability (SDG monitoring), national planning (health sector resource allocation), programme management (identifying where services are working and where they are not), and advocacy (demonstrating need and progress). Poor measurement leads to poor decisions — misallocation of resources, inability to identify populations in need, and incorrect assessment of programme effectiveness.

### The Data Ecosystem

**Household surveys (DHS, MICS)**: The backbone of SRHR measurement in LMICs. These large-scale, nationally representative surveys collect data on fertility, family planning, maternal health, child health, HIV, GBV, and other topics. Their strengths: methodological rigour, standardised questionnaires enabling cross-country comparison, population-level representativeness. Their limitations: conducted only every 3–5 years (meaning data may be several years old at the time of publication); expensive (USD 1–3 million per survey); dependent on external donor funding; and unable to capture rare events (maternal death) with sufficient precision at sub-national levels.

**Administrative data (HMIS/DHIS2)**: Facility-based reporting systems that collect continuous data on service delivery. Strengths: timeliness, granularity (facility-level), low marginal cost. Limitations: captures only those who reach health facilities (excluding home births, traditional healers, informal sector); data quality depends on health worker reporting compliance; and denominators (population served) are often estimated rather than known.

**CRVS**: Birth and death registration provide the foundation for demographic statistics. Where CRVS is complete (most high-income countries, some MICs), maternal mortality can be measured directly from death certificates with cause-of-death coding. Where CRVS is incomplete (most LDCs), maternal mortality must be estimated through surveys or modelling.

## DETAIL

### Key Indicator Measurement Challenges

**Maternal Mortality Ratio (SDG 3.1.1)**: The most difficult SRHR indicator to measure accurately. Maternal death is relatively rare (even in high-MMR countries, it occurs in fewer than 1% of pregnancies), making survey-based estimates imprecise. The inter-agency estimation group (MMEIG) uses a Bayesian model combining CRVS data, survey data, and covariates to produce country and global estimates — but confidence intervals are wide (often ±30% for country estimates). The 2023 MMEIG report estimated global MMR at 223/100,000 for 2020 — but this is a modelled estimate, not a direct count.

**Contraceptive Prevalence Rate (CPR) and Met Need**: CPR measures the proportion of women (or couples) using any contraceptive method. Met need measures the proportion of women who want to avoid pregnancy and are using modern methods. The "unmet need" indicator has undergone multiple definitional revisions. The current definition captures women who are fecund, sexually active, not wanting a pregnancy, and not using contraception — but this misses nuances like women who use traditional methods by choice, women whose partners prevent them from using contraception, and women who face method-related side effects.

**GBV Prevalence (SDG 5.2.1)**: Measuring violence against women is inherently difficult. Underreporting is severe (estimates suggest only 10–40% of GBV incidents are reported). Different surveys use different definitions, reference periods, and methodologies, making comparison across countries and time periods problematic. WHO's standardised methodology (multi-country study, implemented through DHS modules) has improved comparability, but cultural factors in reporting remain a significant confound.

**Adolescent Birth Rate (SDG 3.7.2)**: Measured as births per 1,000 girls aged 15–19. This indicator is relatively straightforward to measure through surveys and CRVS, but it misses important nuances: it does not capture pregnancies that ended in abortion or miscarriage, it does not distinguish wanted from unwanted births, and it does not capture births to girls under 15 (a critical indicator of sexual violence and child marriage).

### UNFPA's Role in Data Quality

UNFPA contributes to SRHR data quality through:
- **Census support**: Supporting national statistical offices in conducting population censuses — the denominator for all population-level indicators
- **Survey support**: Technical and financial support for DHS and MICS rounds in programme countries
- **CRVS strengthening**: Supporting countries in transitioning to digital CRVS with improved death registration and cause-of-death coding
- **HMIS**: Supporting SRHR modules within national DHIS2 platforms
- **Estimation processes**: Participating in MMEIG and other inter-agency estimation groups
- **Capacity building**: Training national statisticians and M&E officers in SRHR data collection, analysis, and use

## CURRENT STATUS

- SRHR data quality is improving but significant gaps persist, particularly in fragile states and LDCs
- The DHS-8 programme (current phase) includes expanded SRHR modules and disability questions
- Digital data collection (tablets replacing paper) is improving data quality and timeliness
- Real-time monitoring systems are being piloted but not yet at scale
- UNFPA continues to invest in national statistical capacity as a core programme area
- Post-2030 framework discussions include debate about which SRHR indicators to retain, modify, or add
