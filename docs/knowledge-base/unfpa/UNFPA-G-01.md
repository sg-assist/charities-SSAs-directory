## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Sub-Saharan Africa is UNFPA's largest operational region by every measure: highest maternal mortality, highest unmet need for family planning, highest rates of adolescent pregnancy, child marriage, and female genital mutilation, and the most acute humanitarian SRHR needs. The region accounts for approximately 70 per cent of global maternal deaths (roughly 200,000 of 287,000 annually), despite representing only about 14 per cent of the world's population. UNFPA maintains country offices across virtually all 49 sub-Saharan African countries and allocates the largest share of its core programme resources to the region through its needs-based resource allocation formula.

The scale of need is matched by the scale of challenge. Many sub-Saharan African countries face compounding obstacles: weak health systems, shortage of skilled health workers (particularly midwives), inadequate commodity supply chains, large youth populations with limited access to education and SRHR services, deeply entrenched gender inequalities, and governance fragility that disrupts service delivery. Protracted conflicts in the Sahel, the Horn of Africa, the Great Lakes region, and parts of West Africa create humanitarian crises that overlay and intensify these development challenges.

Progress has occurred — maternal mortality in SSA declined by approximately 37 per cent between 2000 and 2020, modern contraceptive prevalence has risen from approximately 17 per cent to 29 per cent, and child marriage rates have fallen in several countries. But the pace of improvement is insufficient to meet SDG targets, and population growth means that absolute numbers of maternal deaths, unplanned pregnancies, and girls at risk of FGM and child marriage may not decline even as rates improve.

## KEY FACTS

- **Maternal mortality ratio (SSA, 2020)**: Approximately 545 per 100,000 live births — 50 times higher than in Europe (WHO/UNFPA/UNICEF/World Bank estimates)
- **Share of global maternal deaths**: SSA accounts for roughly 70% of the global total (approximately 200,000 of 287,000 annually)
- **Modern contraceptive prevalence (SSA, 2023)**: Approximately 29% among married/in-union women — the lowest of any world region
- **Unmet need for family planning (SSA)**: Approximately 18% of married women — highest globally
- **Adolescent birth rate (SSA)**: Approximately 98 per 1,000 girls aged 15–19 — more than double the global average of approximately 42/1,000
- **Child marriage (SSA)**: Approximately 35% of girls married before age 18; Niger (76%), Central African Republic (68%), and Chad (67%) have the highest rates globally
- **FGM (SSA)**: Highest prevalence in Somalia (98%), Guinea (95%), Djibouti (94%), and Mali (89%); the UNFPA-UNICEF Joint Programme operates in 17 African countries
- **Obstetric fistula**: An estimated 2 million women in SSA live with untreated fistula; UNFPA's Campaign to End Fistula operates in over 50 countries, predominantly in Africa
- **Skilled birth attendance (SSA)**: Approximately 60% of births — compared to over 99% in developed regions
- **Midwifery workforce gap**: SSA has approximately 25% of the midwifery workforce needed to provide universal coverage (SoWMy 2021)
- **Youth population**: SSA's population under 25 exceeds 650 million — the youngest regional demographic profile globally
- **UNFPA programme expenditure in SSA**: Typically 40–50% of total programme expenditure globally
- **Humanitarian crises**: Active humanitarian SRHR operations in Sudan, South Sudan, DRC, Somalia, Ethiopia (Tigray/Amhara), Burkina Faso, Mali, Niger, Nigeria (northeast), Cameroon, Mozambique, and others
- **HIV intersection**: SSA accounts for approximately 65% of global HIV infections; SRHR-HIV integration is a major programme priority

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### UNFPA's Presence in SSA

UNFPA operates through two regional structures in SSA: the East and Southern Africa Regional Office (ESARO, based in Johannesburg) and the West and Central Africa Regional Office (WCARO, based in Dakar). Together, they oversee country offices in virtually all SSA countries, plus liaison arrangements for smaller states.

The scale of presence reflects both need and history. UNFPA has been active in SSA since the 1970s, initially focused on population and family planning programmes. The 1994 ICPD reoriented programming toward reproductive rights, but family planning — reframed as "voluntary, rights-based family planning" — remains a central pillar.

### Demographic Context

SSA is the world's fastest-growing region, projected to reach approximately 2.1 billion people by 2050 (from approximately 1.2 billion in 2023). Total fertility rates remain high — approximately 4.6 children per woman regionally, compared to a global average of 2.3. In several Sahelian countries (Niger, Mali, Chad, Somalia), TFR exceeds 6.

This demographic trajectory has profound implications for development, governance, and SRHR:
- Rapidly growing youth populations create a potential "demographic dividend" if investments in education, health, and employment are sufficient — or a "demographic burden" if they are not
- High fertility is both a cause and consequence of limited access to family planning, girls' education, and women's economic empowerment
- Population momentum means that even as fertility rates decline, absolute population growth will continue for decades

## DETAIL

### Maternal Health

SSA's maternal mortality crisis is the most severe globally. The regional MMR of 545/100,000 (2020) masks enormous variation: Chad (1,063), South Sudan (1,223), and Sierra Leone (443) are among the worst globally, while Cabo Verde (42) and Mauritius (84) approach middle-income levels.

UNFPA's maternal health programming in SSA focuses on:
- **EmONC (Emergency Obstetric and Newborn Care)**: Supporting the upgrading of health facilities to provide signal functions for emergency obstetric care; strengthening referral systems
- **Midwifery**: Training and deploying midwives, with major programmes in countries like Ethiopia (Health Extension Programme support), Mozambique, and Tanzania
- **Maternal Death Surveillance and Response (MDSR)**: Supporting governments in establishing systems to review and learn from every maternal death
- **Community-based interventions**: Working with community health workers to promote antenatal care, facility delivery, and postnatal care

**Evidence**: Maternal mortality reduction in SSA has been real but insufficient. Rwanda (from 1,270 to 248/100,000 between 2000 and 2020), Ethiopia (from 897 to 267), and Tanzania (from 828 to 238) are cited as success stories. Common factors: political will, community health worker deployment, increased health facility access, and improved EmONC.

### Family Planning

Modern contraceptive prevalence in SSA has risen from approximately 17% (2000) to 29% (2023), but this conceals major disparities. East Africa (Kenya, Rwanda, Ethiopia) has seen significant increases; West and Central Africa (Niger, Chad, DRC, Nigeria) remains extremely low (under 15% in several countries).

UNFPA's role in SSA family planning:
- **Commodity procurement and supply chain**: UNFPA is the primary multilateral supplier of contraceptives to many SSA countries through the UNFPA Supplies Partnership (formerly the Global Programme to Enhance Reproductive Health Commodity Security)
- **Policy advocacy**: Supporting governments in developing and costing national family planning strategies (e.g., Nigeria's Blueprint, Kenya's FP2030 commitment)
- **Demand creation**: Social and behaviour change communication, community engagement, and removal of barriers to access (user fees, provider bias, commodity stockouts)
- **Method mix expansion**: Promoting access to a full range of modern methods, including long-acting reversible contraceptives (LARCs) which have shown rapid uptake in East Africa

### Harmful Practices

SSA has the highest global rates of both child marriage and FGM:

**Child marriage**: The UNFPA-UNICEF Global Programme to End Child Marriage operates in 12 countries, with heavy SSA focus (Ethiopia, Mozambique, Niger, Sierra Leone, Zambia, and others). Interventions include girls' empowerment programmes, community engagement, keeping girls in school, and legislative reform.

**FGM**: The UNFPA-UNICEF Joint Programme to Eliminate FGM operates in 17 countries, all in Africa except one. The programme combines community-level engagement (community declarations to abandon FGM), legal reform, health sector response, and data collection. Prevalence has declined in several countries (Kenya, Egypt, Burkina Faso) but remains entrenched in others.

### Humanitarian Response

A significant and growing share of UNFPA's work in SSA is humanitarian response. Major operations include:
- **Sudan crisis (2023–)**: Conflict displacing millions; UNFPA providing MISP implementation, GBV services, and reproductive health kits
- **DRC**: Protracted conflict in eastern provinces; GBV response and reproductive health services
- **Horn of Africa**: Climate-related drought, displacement, and food insecurity affecting SRHR
- **Sahel**: Conflict and displacement in Burkina Faso, Mali, and Niger; collapsing health systems
- **South Sudan**: Ongoing conflict and humanitarian crisis since 2013

## CURRENT STATUS

- SSA remains UNFPA's largest programme region, absorbing approximately 40–50% of programme expenditure
- The "three zeros" are furthest from achievement in SSA — making the region the primary test of whether UNFPA's strategy can deliver at scale
- Demographic momentum ensures that SSA's population challenges will intensify through 2050 regardless of current programming
- Climate change is adding a new layer of vulnerability, with drought, flooding, and displacement compounding SRHR challenges
- The humanitarian-development nexus is particularly acute in SSA, where chronic underdevelopment and acute crisis co-exist in many countries
- FP2030 commitments from African governments represent significant political commitments, but implementation and financing gaps remain large
