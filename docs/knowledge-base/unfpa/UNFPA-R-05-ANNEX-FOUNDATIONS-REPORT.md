## Chapter 1: Introduction and Methodology

### 1.1 Purpose of This Report

This report is a deep-dive companion to the structured knowledge-base document UNFPA-R-05. Where UNFPA-R-05 provides a concise reference directory of Singapore-based foundations relevant to UNFPA's mandate in maternal, reproductive, and children's health (MRCH), this annex report offers comprehensive narrative profiles of each funder, cross-cutting analysis of the philanthropic ecosystem, and strategic recommendations for UNFPA's engagement approach.

The report is intended for use by UNFPA programme staff and the LKYSPP team engaged in partnership and resource mobilisation in Singapore and Southeast Asia. It should be read alongside UNFPA-R-05 and updated as new data becomes available. The current edition reflects publicly available information verified as of April 2026.

### 1.2 Scope

The report covers:

- All foundations and philanthropic entities categorised in the four-tier MRCH directory
- The structural features of Singapore's philanthropic ecosystem that shape how foundations operate, give, and partner
- The emerging family office philanthropy layer
- Cross-cutting analysis of funding flows, gender lens, geographic focus, and systemic gaps
- Strategic engagement recommendations for UNFPA's partnership-building

The scope is intentionally limited to Singapore-registered or Singapore-headquartered entities, with relevant notes on regional programming where Singapore foundations operate internationally.

### 1.3 Methodology

Research for this report drew on four main categories of sources:

**Public annual reports and foundation websites.** All major foundations publish annual reports, many of which disclose grant recipients, programme descriptions, and aggregate disbursement data. Where available, the most recent published annual report (FY2024 in most cases) was used as the primary reference.

**Third-party sector databases.** Soristic's Giving in Singapore report (2024/2025 edition) provides aggregate and entity-level disbursement data for 126 philanthropic organisations. CharityGuidePoint.sg, the Commissioner of Charities' published data, and Give.sg supplemented foundation-level information. These sources were used to cross-check foundation self-reporting.

**Institutional and media sources.** Hospital websites, government press releases, and reputable Singapore media (CNA, Straits Times, Business Times) provided programme-level detail — especially for funding announcements that post-dated the most recent foundation annual reports.

**International organisation databases.** AVPN, PAA, Quantedge, and Gates Foundation materials were cross-referenced to verify pooled fund data and regional programming relationships.

Where a specific figure is cited, the source is identified in parentheses. Where data could not be independently verified, this is noted explicitly. This report does not speculate or estimate; all figures are drawn from named sources.

### 1.4 Limitations

Several significant foundations — particularly those in Tier 2 such as Moh Family Foundation and Low Tuck Kwong Foundation — publish limited public information. Disbursement figures for these entities rely on third-party sector reports (primarily Soristic) rather than foundation self-disclosure. Readers should treat these figures as indicative rather than audited.

Family office philanthropy data is structurally opaque by design. Chapter 8 addresses this layer on a structural rather than entity-specific basis, as individual SFO giving data is generally unavailable.

This report does not cover foundations outside Singapore, nor does it address bilateral donors, multilateral agencies, or public sector grant-making bodies except where they interact directly with the Singapore philanthropic ecosystem.

### 1.5 Why Singapore Matters for UNFPA

UNFPA's engagement in Singapore is relatively recent compared to its decades-long presence in developing countries. Singapore is an upper-income city-state where the traditional UNFPA programme model — government technical assistance for health systems — has limited direct application. The rationale for UNFPA's Singapore engagement is primarily one of resource mobilisation, knowledge brokerage, and South-South learning facilitation.

Singapore occupies a unique position at the intersection of Southeast Asian regional identity and global financial sophistication. It is simultaneously a regional hub for development finance (the Asian Development Bank has a Singapore office, as do multiple development finance institutions), a convening centre for regional philanthropy (PAA, AVPN, and CFS all operate from Singapore), and a site of concentrated private wealth with growing philanthropic ambitions. For UNFPA, Singapore is not a programme country in the traditional sense but a strategic engagement environment that can unlock capital, convening power, and technical partnerships for UNFPA's global MRCH mandate.

The LKYSPP partnership — UNFPA's formal institutional anchor in Singapore — is the appropriate vehicle for this engagement. LKYSPP's credibility with Singapore's government, foundations, and business community provides UNFPA with an institutional entry point that would take years to develop independently. The partnership creates a permission structure for UNFPA to engage Singapore's philanthropic community as a peer rather than as an external supplicant.

### 1.6 Report Structure

The report proceeds as follows:

## Chapter 2: Singapore's Philanthropic Landscape — Structural Overview

### 2.1 Scale and Growth

Singapore's philanthropic sector has grown substantially over the past decade, driven by wealth accumulation, a maturing civil society, and deliberate government policy to cultivate a culture of giving. The most current aggregate figure — drawn from Soristic's 2024/2025 Giving in Singapore Report — places total disbursements by the 126 tracked philanthropic organisations at S$419 million for FY2024. This figure covers only the organised philanthropic sector (foundations, corporate foundations, and giving circles) and does not include individual direct giving, government grants, or community chest distributions, meaning the true total flow to civil society is substantially larger.

The sector's growth trajectory is visible in the expansion of tracked entities: Soristic's 2022 edition tracked 102 organisations; the 2025 edition tracks 126, a 24% increase in three years. This reflects both the establishment of new foundations and improved disclosure practices among existing ones.

Singapore consistently ranks in the top tier of international philanthropy indices. In the 2024 Charities Aid Foundation (CAF) World Giving Index, Singapore ranked third globally, a significant achievement for a city-state of 5.9 million people. This ranking reflects both the density of wealth and the strength of social norms around organised giving.

### 2.2 Structural Features of the Ecosystem

#### Government-Linked Foundations

Singapore's philanthropic ecosystem is unusual by international standards in the prominent role played by government-linked foundations. Temasek Foundation is the philanthropic arm of Temasek Holdings, the Singapore government's investment company. DBS Foundation operates within DBS Bank, in which the Singapore government is the largest shareholder through Temasek. Even foundations that are nominally private, such as those associated with GIC-linked families, often have deep structural ties to the state apparatus.

This government-linkage is not a limitation for engagement purposes — in fact, it means that government policy priorities are often reflected in foundation programme design. When the Singapore government identified falling birth rates and maternal health outcomes as a concern, Temasek Foundation responded with the Richard Magnus Endowment. This creates a degree of predictability: foundations linked to government are likely to maintain MRCH as a funding priority as long as it aligns with national population and health policy objectives.

#### Family Foundations with Concentrated Wealth

The second major structural feature is the presence of several very large family foundations — most prominently the Lee Foundation and Ngee Ann Kongsi — that make infrequent but very large anchor gifts. These foundations do not typically operate open grant cycles and are not accessible through standard proposal submission. Their giving tends to be relationship-driven, long-term in nature, and often directed at endowment rather than programme funding.

The Lee Foundation, with an estimated S$13.6 billion in assets, is almost certainly among the five largest private foundations in Asia by total assets. Its giving patterns — S$50 million to SingHealth Duke-NUS being a representative example — reflect a preference for transformational gifts rather than recurring grants. Engagement with this tier requires patient relationship cultivation through existing institutional partnerships.

#### Charitable Structures and Registration

Most major foundations are registered as public companies limited by guarantee (CLGs) under the Companies Act, and separately registered as charities under the Charities Act. This dual registration structure is standard in Singapore and provides both the legal personality of a company and the charitable status needed for tax-exemption purposes.

The IPC (Institution of a Public Character) designation is a key structural feature. IPC status allows a charity to issue tax-deductible receipts for donations. For foundations giving to health charities, the IPC framework is the primary mechanism for tax efficiency. Health-sector IPCs must obtain MOH endorsement, which creates a regulatory interdependency between the Ministry and the major health charities that receive philanthropic funding.

#### The Role of Hospitals as Philanthropic Conduits

Singapore's major hospitals — particularly SingHealth, NUH, KKH, and National Healthcare Group — serve as significant nodes in the philanthropic ecosystem. Large foundations frequently give through hospital-linked charitable foundations rather than directly to specific programmes. This pattern is evident across all four Tier 1 foundations in this report, all of which have funding relationships with KKH, SingHealth, or both.

Hospitals function as trusted intermediaries: they have IPC status, established governance structures, clinical research capacity, and government relationships that foundations value. For UNFPA, this means that hospital partnerships (particularly with KKH) serve dual purposes: operational collaboration and access to the funding relationships that hospitals carry.

### 2.3 Health Philanthropy — Sector Dominance

Health is consistently the largest sector in Singapore's philanthropic landscape. Soristic's 2024/2025 report indicates that health receives the greatest share of total disbursements among all cause categories, reflecting both the magnitude of healthcare costs in Singapore and the close alignment between health philanthropy and the city-state's founding values around physical capability and economic productivity.

Within health philanthropy, the major sub-sectors — in approximate order of funding volume — are:

1. Hospital infrastructure and medical research (dominant, driven by large anchor gifts from Tier 2 foundations)
2. Cancer care (NKF and related charities are major beneficiaries)
3. Chronic disease management and eldercare
4. Maternal and child health (growing rapidly, primarily through Tier 1 foundations)
5. Mental health (emerging, with Octava and several others growing in this space)
6. Reproductive health (historically underfunded; growing through AWARE and SRHR advocacy)

The rapid growth of MRCH-focused giving — represented by the S$100M Temasek Foundation endowment (2023), the Tanoto MRF launch (2023), and the PAA Health Community launch (May 2025) — signals a structural shift in where Singapore philanthropic capital flows. The convergence of falling birth rates, an ageing population requiring longer reproductive-health care spans, and increased clinical research capacity at KKH and SingHealth has created the conditions for sustained MRCH investment.

### 2.4 Geographic Focus

Singapore foundations overwhelmingly direct their giving to Singapore-based organisations and beneficiaries. Cross-border or regional giving is the exception rather than the rule for most foundations. The exceptions tend to fall into two categories:

**Diaspora-linked regional giving.** Several foundations — notably the Ishk Tolaram Foundation (Nigeria and Indonesia) and Low Tuck Kwong Foundation (Indonesia, Cambodia, and others) — give regionally based on the founders' business and personal ties to specific countries.

**Regional convening vehicles.** PAA and AVPN represent a different model: they create pooled mechanisms that aggregate Singapore philanthropic capital for regional deployment, primarily in Southeast Asia and South Asia. AVPN's MNCHN pooled fund is the clearest example — a mechanism explicitly designed to channel Singapore-adjacent capital to SE Asian maternal and child health priorities.

The 2025 Budget pilot of 100% tax deductions for qualifying overseas humanitarian donations is a deliberate policy attempt to shift this domestic orientation. If the pilot succeeds, it may catalyse a more sustained expansion of Singapore foundation giving into international MRCH programming.

### 2.5 Gender Lens and SRHR

Singapore's philanthropic sector has historically been gender-neutral in its framing, focusing on health and welfare rather than gender rights. Explicit sexual and reproductive health and rights (SRHR) framing — a core component of UNFPA's global mandate — is present only in a small segment of the ecosystem, primarily through AWARE Singapore.

Major foundations do not publicly engage with SRHR framing; their maternal and child health giving is framed through clinical outcomes (neonatal mortality, fertility rates, nutritional health) rather than rights-based language. This does not represent a fundamental barrier to engagement — UNFPA's MRCH work in Singapore's context is compatible with clinical framing — but it does mean that explicit SRHR advocacy must primarily flow through AWARE and allied civil society channels rather than through foundation relationships.

### 2.6 The Soristic Giving Report as a Reference Framework

Soristic's annual Giving in Singapore report has become the de facto benchmark for the sector. The 2024/2025 edition covers 126 organisations, tracks year-on-year disbursement trends, and provides comparative data across cause categories. For the purposes of this report, Soristic data is used as the primary aggregate reference, with foundation-level data triangulated against foundation-published accounts where available.

The Soristic methodology tracks organised philanthropic entities (foundations, corporates, giving circles) but excludes individual giving, government grants, and distributions by community foundations where the underlying donor cannot be attributed. This means Soristic data underestimates the true scale of health philanthropy but provides a consistent, comparable basis for cross-entity analysis.

### 2.7 Corporate Philanthropy and Foundations

Singapore's philanthropic landscape includes a significant corporate philanthropy layer alongside family foundations. Major banks, insurance companies, and technology firms operate corporate social responsibility programmes and, in some cases, dedicated corporate foundations. DBS Foundation is the most prominent example in the health space, but other corporate players — OCBC Bank, United Overseas Bank (UOB), Prudential, Great Eastern — maintain health-related community investment programmes.

Corporate foundations in Singapore typically operate at smaller scale than family foundations, with more structured application processes and CSR-aligned priorities that may shift with annual reporting cycles. For UNFPA, corporate foundations are most relevant as partners for specific, time-bound programmes — particularly programmes with visible public health impact in Singapore — rather than as sustained institutional funders.

The distinction between a corporate foundation (DBS Foundation) and a corporate CSR programme (most other banks) is important for engagement strategy. Foundations have dedicated programme teams and multi-year commitments; CSR programmes often fund on a year-by-year basis aligned with regulatory requirements. DBS Foundation's scale and programme ambition — S$1 billion over a decade — reflects a deliberate decision to elevate DBS's philanthropic activity to foundation-level institutional commitment, which is why it is classified as Tier 1 in this analysis.

### 2.8 Volunteerism and the Giving Culture

Singapore's position as third in the 2024 CAF World Giving Index reflects not only organised philanthropic giving but also a strong culture of volunteerism. The NVPC (National Volunteer and Philanthropy Centre) promotes and tracks volunteering, and many major foundations complement their financial giving with volunteer engagement programmes.

For UNFPA, the volunteerism dimension is relevant primarily for awareness and stakeholder cultivation rather than for programme delivery. However, foundation volunteerism programmes create a pathway for engaging the employees of Temasek, DBS, and other major institutions in UNFPA's MRCH work — building a grassroots constituency within the corporate community that can support relationship development at the senior level.

The government's National Day of Philanthropy, run by NVPC, is an annual platform for demonstrating sector-wide commitment to giving. UNFPA's participation in NVPC-hosted events would signal its engagement with Singapore's giving culture and provide access to the broader foundation and civil society community.

## Chapter 3: Tier 1 Foundations — Direct Maternal and Child Health Funders

The four foundations in this tier share a common characteristic: they have made explicit, publicly announced programmatic commitments to maternal, reproductive, or children's health. Their giving is not merely incidental to a broader health portfolio — in each case, MRCH is an identified priority area with dedicated funding structures. Together, they represent the highest-alignment and highest-priority engagement targets for UNFPA in Singapore.

### 3.1 Temasek Foundation

#### Overview and Institutional Structure

Temasek Foundation is the philanthropic entity of the Temasek group, established as a public company limited by guarantee and registered charity in Singapore. It is institutionally linked to Temasek Holdings, the Singapore state investment company whose portfolio spans financial services, telecommunications, life sciences, consumer, real estate, transportation, industrials, and technology. Temasek Holdings had a net portfolio value of S$389 billion as of March 2024.

Temasek Foundation's structural position within the Temasek group gives it access to an institutional network that includes SingHealth, SingTel, Singapore Airlines, CapitaLand, and hundreds of other portfolio companies, many of which are active in healthcare, logistics, and community services. This network translates directly into programme delivery capacity: the Integrated Maternal and Child Wellness Hub (IMCWH), for example, was co-developed with SingHealth Polyclinics — a SingHealth entity — leveraging Temasek's intra-group relationships.

Temasek Foundation's grantmaking is characterised by a systems-change orientation. Rather than simply funding operational services, the Foundation tends to fund pilot programmes, infrastructure investments, and research initiatives that are designed to generate replicable models for broader adoption. This is evident in the IMCWH: a pilot at selected polyclinics, designed to demonstrate that integrated maternal and child wellness services could be delivered efficiently at a primary-care level and subsequently scaled nationally.

#### The Richard Magnus Endowment for Mothers and Children

The centrepiece of Temasek Foundation's MRCH commitment is the Richard Magnus Endowment for Mothers and Children, announced in June 2023. Named after Richard Magnus, a retired Singapore judge and former President of the National Council of Social Service, the endowment was established with a S$100 million capital base, making it the single largest philanthropic commitment to maternal and child health ever announced in Singapore.

The endowment's purpose, as stated at its launch, is to improve maternal and child wellness outcomes in Singapore through sustained, long-term investment in programmes across the continuum from preconception care through early childhood. The S$100 million principal is intended to generate returns that fund ongoing programme grants in perpetuity, ensuring that Temasek Foundation's MRCH commitment is structural rather than time-limited.

The specific focus areas of the endowment include:
- Maternal mental health, including perinatal and postnatal depression screening and support
- Child developmental health, including early identification of developmental delays
- Integrated care models that connect clinical and community-based support for mothers and young children
- Research on risk factors for adverse maternal and infant outcomes in Singapore's specific demographic context

The endowment structure — rather than a recurring annual budget allocation — signals Temasek Foundation's intent to make MRCH a permanent institutional priority. It also means that the Foundation has a standing mandate to identify and fund MRCH programmes, creating a structural receptivity to well-designed proposals from credible partners.

#### Integrated Maternal and Child Wellness Hub (IMCWH)

Operating alongside but distinct from the endowment, the Integrated Maternal and Child Wellness Hub is a pilot initiative developed with SingHealth Polyclinics. The IMCWH received S$2.5 million or more in Temasek Foundation funding to establish integrated wellness services within the polyclinic setting — co-locating obstetric, paediatric, nutritional, mental health, and social work support for pregnant women and new mothers in a single clinical environment.

The polyclinic setting is significant. Singapore's polyclinics serve as the primary care access point for the majority of the population, particularly lower-income residents. By embedding MRCH services within polyclinics rather than tertiary hospitals, the IMCWH model targets accessibility and early intervention for populations who might otherwise only engage with healthcare reactively rather than preventively.

From UNFPA's perspective, the IMCWH model is directly aligned with UNFPA's global advocacy for integrated primary-care approaches to maternal health. The programme demonstrates that Temasek Foundation is receptive to funding models that serve lower-income populations and are designed for scale, two features that align closely with UNFPA's mandate and approach.

#### Public Health Innovations Grant Stream

Separate from the Richard Magnus Endowment, Temasek Foundation maintains a Public Health Innovations grant stream that issues open calls for proposals. This stream is designed to fund innovative approaches to public health challenges in Singapore, and health outcomes for women, mothers, and children are among the stated priority areas.

For UNFPA, the Public Health Innovations stream is the most accessible formal entry point to Temasek Foundation. Unlike the endowment (which supports specific partner programmes) or the IMCWH (which is linked to SingHealth), the Innovations stream involves a competitive proposal process that external organisations can enter. UNFPA or its LKYSPP partners could develop a proposal framing Singapore MRCH research or knowledge-management work as a contribution to Temasek Foundation's health innovations priorities.

#### UNFPA Engagement Assessment

Temasek Foundation is the highest-priority engagement target in the Singapore philanthropic ecosystem. Three factors converge to make this assessment:

First, the Richard Magnus Endowment establishes MRCH as a permanent institutional priority with a S$100 million capital base, meaning Temasek Foundation will be actively seeking credible MRCH programmes to fund for decades.

Second, the SingHealth network connection provides an institutional bridge. UNFPA's existing or prospective partnerships with KKH (a SingHealth entity) create a relationship pathway to Temasek Foundation's programme officers that does not require cold outreach.

Third, the Foundation's systems-change orientation and focus on scalable, evidence-based models aligns closely with UNFPA's technical approach to MRCH programming. UNFPA's global evidence base and regional comparative experience is an asset that Temasek Foundation would find valuable for the design and evaluation of its Singapore-based programmes.

The recommended initial engagement is through the Public Health Innovations call, supported by a parallel relationship-building effort through SingHealth and KKH contacts.

### 3.2 Tanoto Foundation

#### Overview and Institutional Structure

Tanoto Foundation was established by Sukanto Tanoto, an Indonesian-born entrepreneur whose Royal Golden Eagle (RGE) group of companies spans pulp and paper, palm oil, energy, and property across Asia. The Foundation is registered in Singapore as a public company limited by guarantee and operates internationally, with programmes in Indonesia, China, and Singapore.

Unlike Temasek Foundation, which has a diffuse institutional mandate, Tanoto Foundation's philanthropic focus is relatively concentrated across three pillars: education and leadership development, health and nutrition, and early childhood development. The health pillar is further concentrated in medical research — specifically, research addressing conditions relevant to Singapore's demographic context, including fertility, maternal health, paediatric allergies, and neonatal outcomes.

Tanoto Foundation's medical giving is centred on Singapore, reflecting the Foundation's registered base and the Tanoto family's long-term Singapore residency. The Foundation has partnered with KKH, NUS, and Duke-NUS Medical School on specific research initiatives and has committed over S$20 million to medical research since 2009, a track record that places it among Singapore's most sustained health-research funders.

#### Medical Research Fund (MRF)

The Tanoto Foundation Medical Research Fund, launched in 2023, is the Foundation's most significant recent health initiative. The MRF is structured as a grant fund awarding up to S$5 million per year to priority research areas in maternal and paediatric health. The launch year focused specifically on:

- Maternal fertility, including investigation of environmental and dietary factors affecting fertility outcomes in Singapore's population
- Neonatal and infant allergies, including research on the early onset of allergic sensitisation and the protective factors in maternal nutrition and breastfeeding

The fertility focus is of particular relevance to Singapore's national context. Singapore's total fertility rate (TFR) has declined consistently, reaching approximately 0.97 in 2023 — below replacement level and among the lowest in the world. The government has identified fertility decline as a critical long-term challenge, and multiple foundations (Temasek, Tanoto, and through the PAA Health Community, Gates) have aligned their MRCH giving with fertility-related research priorities.

The MRF's structure — up to S$5 million per year on an open research-grant basis — means Tanoto Foundation is one of the few Singapore foundations with an accessible, recurring funding mechanism specifically for MRCH research. This is distinct from the endowment structure of the Richard Magnus fund and the programme grant structure of DBS Foundation.

#### TF Centre for Comprehensive Health and Maternal Paediatrics (CHaMP) at KKH

Tanoto Foundation made a S$3 million donation to establish the TF Centre for Comprehensive Health and Maternal Paediatrics (CHaMP) at KK Women's and Children's Hospital. This centre is dedicated to improving care for women and children, with research programmes in maternal complications, paediatric chronic conditions, and integrated care pathways for high-risk pregnancies.

The CHaMP centre represents a different mode of philanthropic engagement than the MRF: rather than funding research grants across a field, it builds dedicated institutional capacity within KKH. This distinction is strategically important for UNFPA. The MRF is accessible through research proposals; the CHaMP centre creates a dedicated institutional partner at KKH with a specific MRCH mandate and Tanoto Foundation backing.

#### Total Medical Research Commitment

Tanoto Foundation reports having committed over S$20 million to medical research in Singapore since 2009. This figure encompasses a range of investments beyond the MRF and CHaMP, including donations to NUS medical research, Duke-NUS collaboration grants, and support for KKH's broader paediatric and maternal research agenda. The sustained, 15-year track record makes Tanoto Foundation one of the most established health-research funders in Singapore, second only to the Lee Foundation in longevity of engagement.

The S$20 million figure is reported by Tanoto Foundation itself and has not been independently audited, but is consistent with the Foundation's disclosed programme commitments across multiple institutions. The track record matters for engagement purposes: foundations with long-term health research commitments typically have stable programme teams, established relationships with research institutions, and predictable funding cycles — all features that make partnership development more straightforward.

#### UNFPA Engagement Assessment

Tanoto Foundation is the second highest-priority Tier 1 engagement target, closely behind Temasek Foundation.

The Foundation's MRF provides a concrete, accessible mechanism for UNFPA-affiliated research proposals. UNFPA's technical resources in reproductive health research — including its global databases on maternal mortality, fertility interventions, and reproductive health system design — represent a direct asset for Tanoto Foundation's research agenda.

The KKH bridge is equally important for Tanoto as it is for Temasek. The TF Centre for CHaMP at KKH means that Tanoto Foundation's programme team works closely with KKH clinical and research staff. UNFPA's institutional relationship with KKH, particularly through the WAH Foundation connection (KKH co-operates WAH Foundation's midwifery training in Cambodia), creates a shared institutional context.

The recommended engagement approach is a meeting request framed around research collaboration: UNFPA's global MRCH data and technical resources, combined with Tanoto Foundation's Singapore-based clinical research capacity through CHaMP/KKH, could produce comparative research outputs that neither organisation could achieve independently. This is a genuine value proposition rather than a funding pitch, which is the correct initial framing for a foundation of Tanoto Foundation's profile and sophistication.

### 3.3 Quantedge Advancement Initiative

#### Overview and Institutional Structure

Quantedge Advancement Initiative (QAI) is the philanthropic entity linked to Quantedge Capital, a Singapore-based quantitative hedge fund founded by Quah Mei Lee and Yan Huo in 2006. Quantedge Capital has generated strong long-term returns and manages significant assets under management, though the firm does not publicly disclose its AUM.

QAI operates with a notably lower public profile than Temasek Foundation or Tanoto Foundation. The Foundation does not publish an annual report with the same level of detail as its peers, and its giving is primarily disclosed through its partners' publications and through Soristic's sector data. Despite this lower public profile, QAI has made programmatically significant commitments in MRCH, specifically through its co-investment in the PAA Health for Human Potential Community and its direct grant to Nutrition International in the Philippines.

#### PAA Health for Human Potential Community

QAI is one of four anchor co-funders — alongside Temasek Foundation, Tanoto Foundation, and the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation — of the Philanthropy Asia Alliance's Health for Human Potential Community (HHPC), launched in May 2025 with a stated target of US$100 million by 2030.

The HHPC is structured as a thematic co-investment community within PAA, in which member foundations commit to collective resource mobilisation, joint programme design, and shared learning around health as a driver of human potential. The US$100 million target is intended to be reached by recruiting additional foundation members to the community over the five-year period from 2025 to 2030, with the four anchor funders providing the initial capital and governance framework.

QAI's inclusion as an anchor funder alongside Gates, Temasek, and Tanoto is indicative of its scale and credibility in health philanthropy, despite the Foundation's low public profile. It suggests that QAI's leadership has made a deliberate strategic commitment to MRCH as a philanthropic priority area at the highest tier of Singapore's giving ecosystem.

The geographic scope of the HHPC aligns closely with UNFPA's programming: maternal, newborn, child, and adolescent health in Southeast and South Asia. UNFPA's presence and technical leadership in these geographies make it a credible and desirable participant in or partner to the HHPC.

#### Nutrition International — Philippines Grant

QAI has made a direct grant to Nutrition International supporting programmes for women and adolescents in the Philippines. The Philippines is a priority country for UNFPA's regional MRCH programming, and Nutrition International is a UNFPA programme partner in multiple geographies.

This QAI grant is significant because it demonstrates QAI's willingness to fund cross-border, non-Singapore MRCH work — a distinction from many Singapore foundations that concentrate their giving domestically. The Philippines focus also suggests familiarity with UNFPA's Southeast Asian programming context, creating a natural conversation starting point.

#### UNFPA Engagement Assessment

QAI's engagement with UNFPA is best pursued through PAA rather than bilaterally. QAI's low public profile and apparent preference for convening-vehicle participation (HHPC) over bilateral programme grants suggests that direct outreach is less likely to be productive than positioning UNFPA as a technical partner within the HHPC structure.

The Nutrition International Philippines connection is a secondary engagement angle. UNFPA and Nutrition International have established co-programming relationships globally; surfacing that institutional connection to QAI through Nutrition International is a natural warm-introduction pathway.

### 3.4 DBS Foundation

#### Overview and Institutional Structure

DBS Foundation is the philanthropic entity of DBS Bank, Southeast Asia's largest bank by assets, headquartered in Singapore. DBS Bank is majority-owned by Temasek Holdings, creating a structural government-linkage similar to Temasek Foundation, though DBS Foundation operates with a distinct programme mandate centred on social enterprise and social innovation.

DBS Foundation is notable among corporate foundations in Singapore for the scale and ambition of its financial commitment: DBS Group committed to contribute S$1 billion over a decade to social impact activities through DBS Foundation. In FY2024 alone, DBS Foundation disbursed S$102.6 million across its programmes, making it one of the largest active corporate philanthropic disbursers in Southeast Asia.

DBS Foundation's programme model blends grant-making with social enterprise capacity-building. Its Social Enterprise Grant Programme (SEGP) funds organisations that use business models to create social impact, including in health. Grant sizes range from S$50,000 to S$250,000 per award, with a focus on scalable, sustainable models. This mid-range grant size makes DBS Foundation accessible to a broader range of organisations than Temasek or Tanoto Foundation, which tend toward larger, fewer investments.

#### KidSTART Pregnant Mum and Baby Nutrition Programme

Launched in September 2025, the KidSTART Pregnant Mum and Baby Nutrition Programme is DBS Foundation's most direct MRCH commitment. The programme, operated through KidSTART Singapore (a Ministry of Social and Family Development-linked early childhood programme), provides nutritional support and health education to pregnant women and mothers in lower-income families.

The programme's design is aligned with UNFPA's global advocacy for integrated nutrition support during the first 1,000 days — the period from conception through the child's second birthday — a period recognised as disproportionately influential on long-term health outcomes. The combination of nutritional support with health literacy programming reflects a public-health model rather than a pure charity model.

DBS Foundation's partnership with KidSTART is significant because KidSTART is an MSF-linked programme, meaning DBS Foundation has demonstrated a willingness to work alongside government programmes rather than operating independently. This government-aligned approach mirrors UNFPA's own programming philosophy of working within and strengthening public systems.

#### Social Enterprise Grant Programme (SEGP)

The SEGP funds social enterprises across multiple cause areas, with health as an eligible category. Grant sizes of S$50,000 to S$250,000 are most appropriate for social enterprises or non-profits at the pilot or early-scale stage. For UNFPA, the SEGP is a potential channel for MRCH social enterprises or service organisations in Singapore that align with UNFPA's programme priorities — organisations that UNFPA could support technically while DBS Foundation provides financial resources.

The SEGP also has a Southeast Asia component, with grants available to social enterprises in other DBS operating markets including Indonesia, India, China, Hong Kong, and Taiwan. This regional scope creates alignment with UNFPA's SE Asian programme geographies.

#### Scale and Infrastructure

DBS Foundation's S$102.6 million FY2024 disbursement and S$1 billion decade commitment distinguish it from most foundations in this report in terms of financial scale. This scale is matched by significant programme infrastructure: DBS Foundation operates with a professional grant-making team, regional offices, and a structured application and monitoring process.

For UNFPA, DBS Foundation's scale means that a programme partnership — whether through KidSTART, the SEGP, or a new thematic initiative — would not strain the Foundation's capacity. Conversely, the Foundation's structured processes mean that engagement requires investment in proposal development and compliance rather than informal relationship-based approaches.

#### UNFPA Engagement Assessment

DBS Foundation is a high-priority Tier 1 target with two distinct engagement pathways:

The KidSTART Pregnant Mum and Baby Nutrition Programme creates an immediate operational intersection. UNFPA's technical expertise in maternal nutrition and first-1,000-days programming is directly relevant to DBS Foundation's programme design and evaluation needs. An offer of technical partnership — UNFPA providing evidence-base, curriculum design, or programme evaluation support — alongside DBS Foundation's financial resources and KidSTART's operational infrastructure is a credible three-way collaboration model.

The SEGP provides a parallel pathway for UNFPA to channel funding to MRCH social enterprises in Singapore and the region, without requiring UNFPA itself to be the direct grantee.

## Chapter 4: Tier 2 Foundations — Significant Health Givers

The five foundations in this tier make substantial philanthropic contributions to health in Singapore but do not have explicit mandates in maternal, reproductive, or children's health. Their giving tends to be directed at hospital infrastructure, medical research broadly defined, or disease-specific programmes. For UNFPA, these foundations represent a secondary engagement tier: the approach is to identify specific programme elements within their existing health portfolios that have MRCH relevance, and to build relationships through shared institutional partners rather than through direct programme proposals.

### 4.1 Lee Foundation

#### Overview

The Lee Foundation is the oldest major private philanthropic foundation in Singapore, established in 1952 by Lee Kong Chian, a businessman, banker, and plantation magnate who was one of the most prominent ethnic Chinese leaders in pre-independence Singapore. Today the Foundation is among the largest private foundations in Asia by total assets, with an estimated asset base of approximately S$13.6 billion.

The Lee Foundation operates with limited public disclosure. It does not maintain an active website with grant application information, does not publish an annual report accessible to the general public, and does not have publicly named programme staff. Its giving is known primarily through the announcements of recipient institutions rather than through the Foundation's own communications.

#### Health Philanthropy

The Lee Foundation's health philanthropy is characterised by transformational anchor gifts rather than recurring programme grants. The most significant recent example is a S$50 million donation to SingHealth and Duke-NUS Medical School, supporting medical research and education infrastructure. This gift reflects the Foundation's preference for investments that create lasting institutional capacity — endowed chairs, research centres, and capital infrastructure — rather than time-limited programme funding.

Disbursements in FY2024 are reported by Soristic at approximately S$33.2 million, though given the Foundation's limited disclosure, this figure may not reflect the full picture of its giving in any given year. The Foundation's asset base of S$13.6 billion means that its annual giving, whatever the precise figure, represents a small fraction of potential capacity — indicating that the Foundation could scale its giving significantly if it chose to do so.

The Lee Foundation gives primarily to education and health, with a secondary focus on community and social welfare. Within health, its giving appears concentrated in hospitals and medical education rather than in community or preventive health programmes. Maternal and child health as a standalone priority is not publicly identified, though the SingHealth relationship encompasses KKH.

#### UNFPA Engagement Assessment

Direct engagement with the Lee Foundation is not recommended as a near-term priority. The Foundation's relationship-driven, non-public giving approach means that outreach requires warm introductions through existing Lee Foundation trustees or programme connections. The most viable pathway is through the SingHealth relationship: as UNFPA develops partnerships with SingHealth institutions (including KKH), it may over time encounter Lee Foundation trustees in the context of those institutional relationships.

A longer-term cultivation approach — demonstrating UNFPA's track record and credibility within SingHealth's institutional circle — is more likely to yield results than any direct outreach.

### 4.2 Moh Family Foundation

#### Overview

Moh Family Foundation is among the largest private foundations in Singapore by annual disbursements, reporting S$22.4 million in FY2024 according to Soristic's data. The Foundation is linked to the Moh family, whose business interests include real estate and investment. Like the Lee Foundation, it operates with limited public disclosure and does not maintain a public grant portal.

#### Health Philanthropy

What distinguishes Moh Family Foundation in this analysis is the exceptional concentration of its giving in health: approximately 75.1% of its total disbursements in FY2024 were directed to the health sector. This is an unusually high health concentration for a generalist family foundation, and it places Moh Family Foundation among the most health-committed philanthropic entities in Singapore when measured as a proportion of total giving.

The specific health recipients are not publicly detailed, but the overall scale — S$16.8 million in estimated health disbursements in FY2024 alone, based on the 75.1% health proportion of S$22.4 million total — makes Moh Family Foundation a significant health funder by any measure. The Foundation's health giving likely includes hospital operating support, medical research funding, and potentially community health programmes, though the absence of public information makes programme-level analysis impossible.

#### UNFPA Engagement Assessment

Moh Family Foundation's engagement potential is real but access is constrained. The high health concentration suggests deep institutional commitment to health outcomes, and the scale of giving means the Foundation has meaningful programme capacity. However, without public grant guidelines or named programme contacts, initial engagement requires an intermediary introduction.

The most viable approach is through existing hospital relationships: if Moh Family Foundation supports SingHealth or KKH (likely, given the Foundation's health orientation and Singapore's philanthropic concentration in those institutions), an introduction through those hospital contacts is the preferred pathway. UNFPA should flag Moh Family Foundation for longer-term cultivation with a two-year timeline.

### 4.3 Low Tuck Kwong Foundation

#### Overview

Low Tuck Kwong Foundation is linked to Low Tuck Kwong, a Singapore-based businessman whose Bayan Resources is one of Indonesia's largest coal companies. The Foundation's giving profile is notable for its scale — S$127.6 million in FY2023 (an exceptional year) and S$23.2 million in FY2024 — and for its regional giving footprint, which extends beyond Singapore to Indonesia and other parts of Southeast Asia.

The Foundation operates without a public grant portal and its programme-level giving is not publicly detailed. Soristic and CharityGuidePoint.sg data provide the aggregate disbursement figures; foundation-level programme descriptions are not publicly available.

#### Health Philanthropy and Regional Scope

The Foundation's health giving is understood to include support for hospitals and health infrastructure in Singapore and Indonesia, reflecting Low Tuck Kwong's long-term business presence and personal ties to both markets. The Indonesian angle is particularly relevant for UNFPA: Indonesia is a priority country for UNFPA's Southeast Asian maternal and child health programming, and a Singapore-registered foundation with active Indonesian health giving is an unusual and potentially valuable partner.

The exceptional FY2023 disbursement of S$127.6 million (compared to S$23.2 million in FY2024) suggests that the Foundation makes periodic large gifts rather than maintaining a smooth annual disbursement profile. This pattern — common among wealth-concentrated family foundations — means that engagement timing matters: approaching the Foundation in a year of limited giving is less likely to yield results than approaching when a new giving cycle appears to be opening.

#### UNFPA Engagement Assessment

Low Tuck Kwong Foundation's Indonesia connection is the most distinctive feature for UNFPA. A proposal framing Singapore-Indonesia collaboration on maternal and child health — building on the Foundation's existing Indonesian health giving and UNFPA's Indonesia programming — creates a genuinely differentiated value proposition that few other Singapore foundations could engage with.

The engagement pathway, however, is indirect: as with Lee Foundation and Moh Family Foundation, there is no public grant process, and warm introductions through existing institutional networks are necessary. AVPN and CFS both have relationships with family foundations of this type and could serve as introduction channels.

### 4.4 Shaw Foundation

#### Overview

Shaw Foundation is linked to the Shaw Organisation, the entertainment and real estate conglomerate established by Sir Run Run Shaw, one of Singapore's most prominent philanthropists of the twentieth century. The Foundation continues to make significant philanthropic gifts to education, health, and community development in Singapore, with a giving profile that reflects both the Shaw family's long-standing community ties and its specific healthcare interests.

FY2024 health disbursements are estimated at approximately S$5 million, based on Soristic data. While this is smaller than the Tier 2 peers, the Shaw Foundation's longstanding institutional relationships and the prestige of the Shaw name in Singapore's philanthropic community make it a relevant entity in this landscape.

#### Health Philanthropy

Shaw Foundation's most significant health relationship is with the National Kidney Foundation (NKF), which it is understood to be one of the NKF's largest institutional donors. While kidney disease is not directly within MRCH scope, the NKF relationship demonstrates Shaw Foundation's capacity for sustained, long-term institutional giving.

In paediatrics and maternal health, Shaw Foundation has supported paediatric research programmes, including work at KKH and through various paediatric research funds in Singapore. The paediatric connection is the primary MRCH relevance.

#### UNFPA Engagement Assessment

Shaw Foundation is a lower-priority target for UNFPA, with engagement most viable through KKH's fundraising relationships. The Foundation's giving is not primarily aligned with UNFPA's mandate, and its scale (relative to Tier 1 foundations) limits the potential impact of engagement. It should be noted in UNFPA's stakeholder register but active cultivation is not recommended as a near-term priority.

### 4.5 Ngee Ann Kongsi

#### Overview

Ngee Ann Kongsi is a distinctive entity in Singapore's philanthropic landscape: a Teochew clan association and clan-linked philanthropic organisation, established in 1845, that has evolved into one of the largest philanthropic givers in Singapore. Unlike the family foundations in this tier, Ngee Ann Kongsi is a community organisation with a governance structure reflecting its clan roots, administered by a board of trustees.

Total disbursements in FY2024 were S$40.6 million, making Ngee Ann Kongsi one of the top five philanthropic givers in Singapore by annual disbursement volume.

#### Health Philanthropy

Ngee Ann Kongsi's most significant health gift in recent years is a S$40 million donation to SingHealth, directed at hospital infrastructure and research. This is a transformational gift at the level of the Lee Foundation's SingHealth donation, and it cements Ngee Ann Kongsi's position as a major health philanthropist in Singapore.

Additionally, Ngee Ann Kongsi made a S$12.5 million gift to Nanyang Technological University (NTU) for the establishment of a Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) centre. TCM integration with maternal and reproductive health — including its role in fertility support, prenatal care, and postnatal recovery — is an area of growing clinical interest in Singapore, and the NTU TCM centre is a potential intersection point for UNFPA's work on integrative reproductive health approaches.

#### UNFPA Engagement Assessment

Like the Lee Foundation, Ngee Ann Kongsi's giving tends to be large, infrequent, and relationship-driven. Direct engagement is best pursued through the SingHealth relationship. The TCM angle is a secondary but genuinely distinctive angle: UNFPA's work on integrating traditional health practices into reproductive health programming in the region (particularly in Southeast Asia) creates a conversation opportunity around Ngee Ann Kongsi's NTU TCM investment that most other MRCH funders would not offer.

## Chapter 5: Tier 3 — Ecosystem Enablers and Philanthropic Intermediaries

The four entities in this tier do not primarily give direct grants to MRCH programmes. Instead, they serve as infrastructure for the philanthropic ecosystem: pooling capital, convening funders, facilitating regional deployment of Singapore philanthropic resources, or providing administrative vehicles through which philanthropic capital can flow tax-efficiently to international causes. For UNFPA, engagement with Tier 3 entities is a high-leverage activity because positioning UNFPA within these convening structures provides access to multiple Tier 1 and Tier 2 foundations simultaneously.

### 5.1 Community Foundation of Singapore (CFS)

#### Overview

The Community Foundation of Singapore is the principal community foundation operating in Singapore, established in 2008 and modelled on the international community foundation tradition. CFS serves as a cause-neutral donor-advised fund (DAF) sponsor, allowing individuals, families, and corporations to establish named giving funds and deploy capital across charitable causes through CFS's administrative infrastructure.

As of FY2024, CFS had disbursed S$28.4 million across its grant funds and had raised S$353 million over its lifetime. CFS is a registered charity and IPC, enabling it to issue 250% tax-deductible receipts to Singapore taxpayers for contributions to its funds.

#### Role as Philanthropic Infrastructure

CFS's significance for UNFPA lies not in its own grant-making but in its function as a channel for other donors. When a Singapore-based family office, high-net-worth individual, or corporate entity wishes to support an international cause but does not have the administrative infrastructure to give directly, CFS can serve as the conduit — receiving the donation, handling the Singapore tax administration, and disbursing to the ultimate beneficiary charity.

For international organisations like UNFPA, CFS engagement means establishing a mechanism through which Singapore donors who wish to support UNFPA's work can do so through a locally recognised, IPC-status vehicle. This is particularly relevant in the context of the 2025–2028 pilot for overseas humanitarian donations, which creates a time-limited window for Singapore donors to receive tax deductions for international giving.

CFS's disbursement profile covers a wide range of causes but is increasingly oriented toward international development and health, reflecting the growing cosmopolitan orientation of Singapore's philanthropic community. Several family offices in Singapore use CFS as their primary grant-making vehicle, and CFS has relationships across the full spectrum of Singapore's institutional philanthropic community.

#### UNFPA Engagement Assessment

Establishing a formal relationship with CFS is a structural priority for UNFPA, independent of any specific programme. A CFS engagement would allow UNFPA to receive Singapore-sourced philanthropic capital through a tax-efficient structure, access CFS's donor community for cultivation events, and participate in CFS's thematic forums on international health and development.

The engagement timeline is relatively short — CFS has a professional team with an established process for working with international organisations — and the required investment is primarily in relationship-building and compliance rather than programme design.

---

### 5.2 Lien Foundation

#### Overview

Lien Foundation is a Singapore-based philanthropy with a long history, established from the estate of Lien Ying Chow, founder of the Oversea-Chinese Banking Corporation (OCBC). It operates as a purpose-driven foundation with an explicit commitment to systems change rather than direct service delivery. Lien Foundation's primary programme areas have been eldercare, palliative care, and — historically — early childhood development (ECD).

FY2024 disbursements were S$24.4 million, placing Lien Foundation in the top tier of Singapore philanthropic givers by disbursement volume.

#### Programme Areas and MRCH Relevance

Lien Foundation's primary relevance to MRCH has historically been through its early childhood development work. The Foundation was an early and sustained investor in preschool quality improvement, integrated ECD services, and evidence-based ECD programming in Singapore. However, the Foundation has signalled a strategic shift in recent years toward greater focus on eldercare and palliative care, reducing its ECD programming.

This shift reduces Lien Foundation's direct MRCH relevance. However, three secondary connections remain worth noting:

First, Lien Foundation's palliative care work encompasses end-of-life care for women, including those with reproductive cancers — an intersection with UNFPA's broader mandate on dignity and reproductive health across the life course.

Second, Lien Foundation's systems-change methodology — building evidence, demonstrating models, and influencing policy — is closely aligned with UNFPA's technical approach, creating a natural methodological affinity that could support learning partnerships or joint knowledge-sharing.

Third, Lien Foundation has an international programme footprint in Southeast Asia, particularly in early childhood and education, which creates shared geographies with UNFPA's regional programmes.

#### UNFPA Engagement Assessment

Lien Foundation is a medium-priority Tier 3 target. It is less directly relevant to MRCH than PAA or AVPN, and its strategic shift away from ECD reduces the most direct intersection. However, its systems-change orientation and SE Asia footprint make it a relevant peer in Singapore's development philanthropy community, and a relationship with Lien Foundation strengthens UNFPA's credibility and connectivity within the broader ecosystem.

### 5.3 AVPN

#### Overview

AVPN (Asian Venture Philanthropy Network) is a Singapore-headquartered network of social investors operating across Asia. Founded in 2011, AVPN now counts more than 700 member organisations across 33 countries, including foundations, family offices, corporate foundations, development finance institutions, impact investors, and social enterprises.

AVPN's mission is to build a connected ecosystem of social investors, catalyse capital flows to high-impact organisations, and build the capacity of social sector actors. It operates through thematic communities, convening events, deal sourcing, and co-investment facilitation. AVPN is not itself a grantmaker in most cases; it is a network and platform that facilitates capital movement among its members.

#### MNCHN Pooled Fund

AVPN operates a Maternal, Newborn, Child and Adolescent Health and Nutrition (MNCAHN, often abbreviated MNCHN) pooled fund for Southeast Asia. The fund, with a current mobilisation of approximately US$1 million, pools contributions from multiple AVPN members for deployment to MRCH programmes in SE Asian priority countries.

The MNCHN fund is the most directly operational MRCH vehicle in Singapore's philanthropic intermediary landscape. It is explicitly aligned with UNFPA's mandate, covers UNFPA's SE Asia geographies, and is already active — unlike the PAA HHPC, which was launched in May 2025 and is still in its mobilisation phase.

AVPN's 700-member network includes many of the foundations profiled in this report — QAI is an active AVPN member, Lien Foundation has participated in AVPN forums, and the Foundation network across Asia that AVPN convenes is precisely the audience UNFPA needs to reach for regional resource mobilisation.

#### UNFPA Engagement Assessment

AVPN engagement should be a top-priority near-term action for UNFPA. Several specific engagement pathways exist:

First, joining the MNCHN working group as a technical partner. UNFPA's technical authority on maternal, newborn, child, and adolescent health is unmatched in the AVPN ecosystem, and AVPN would benefit from UNFPA's participation as an evidence and standards resource.

Second, participating in AVPN's annual conference and health-focused forums. AVPN's convening events bring together hundreds of social investors and are a primary point of contact for relationship-building with foundations that are otherwise difficult to access individually.

Third, proposing UNFPA-linked programmes as deal-sourcing opportunities within the AVPN network. AVPN actively sources investment opportunities for its members; UNFPA's SE Asian MRCH programmes that have a philanthropic co-investment component could be presented as AVPN deal-sourcing candidates.

### 5.4 Philanthropy Asia Alliance (PAA)

#### Overview

The Philanthropy Asia Alliance is a Temasek-linked body established to convene philanthropists across Asia, facilitate co-investment, and build a culture of strategic giving. PAA operates as a membership organisation for foundations, family offices, and corporate philanthropies, and convenes an annual summit that is Singapore's largest gathering of philanthropic actors.

PAA's institutional relationship with Temasek gives it access to government networks, state-linked foundations, and the broader Temasek institutional family. Its position as Singapore's de facto philanthropic convener makes PAA membership or partnership a high-value positioning for international organisations seeking access to Singapore's philanthropic community.

#### Health for Human Potential Community

PAA's Health for Human Potential Community (HHPC), launched in May 2025 with a US$100 million target by 2030, is the most significant new MRCH funding vehicle in Asia's philanthropic landscape. The four anchor funders — Temasek Foundation, Tanoto Foundation, Quantedge Advancement Initiative, and the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation — collectively represent the most influential intersection of Singapore philanthropic capital and global MRCH expertise.

The HHPC is designed as a co-investment community in which member foundations collectively resource health initiatives aligned with the community's defined priorities. The community's focus on health as a driver of human potential — rather than health as charity — reflects a development-finance logic that is well-aligned with UNFPA's mandate framing.

The geographic focus of the HHPC is not yet publicly specified in detail, but the composition of the anchor funders (which includes Gates, with its SE Asia and South Asia MRCH priorities) and the PAA secretariat's regional mandate strongly suggest a Southeast and South Asia focus.

#### UNFPA Engagement Assessment

PAA is the highest-priority Tier 3 engagement for UNFPA, with the PAA HHPC representing the single most important new MRCH funding vehicle in Asia. UNFPA's strategic positioning options within the HHPC are several:

Technical partnership: UNFPA could seek to be designated as the HHPC's technical anchor for MRCH programme design and evaluation — contributing evidence-based standards, comparative country data, and programme quality assurance while the HHPC member foundations provide financial resources.

Programme partner: UNFPA-led programmes in SE Asia that meet HHPC investment criteria could be presented to the community as priority co-investment opportunities.

Convening collaborator: PAA's annual summit is a primary convening event for UNFPA's target funders; securing a speaking or panel role at the summit creates visibility with the full PAA membership.

UNFPA's engagement with PAA should begin immediately, given the HHPC's 2025 launch and the five-year mobilisation window to 2030. The opportunity to participate in the community's formative phase — influencing its programme design, investment criteria, and geographic focus — is time-sensitive.

## Chapter 6: Tier 4 — Civil Society and Operational Partners

The organisations in this tier are not primarily funders; they are implementation partners, advocacy organisations, operational charities, and service providers whose work is directly relevant to UNFPA's mandate. Several are beneficiaries of funding from Tier 1–3 foundations, making them nodes in the funding ecosystem and potential collaborators for UNFPA across multiple programme dimensions.

### 6.1 AWARE Singapore

AWARE (Association of Women for Action and Research) is Singapore's longest-established women's rights organisation, founded in 1985. It operates as a membership organisation and registered charity, combining direct services (counselling, legal support, training) with policy advocacy and public education.

AWARE's work is more directly aligned with UNFPA's mandate than any other civil society organisation in Singapore. Its programme portfolio includes:
- Sexual assault care and support, including the Sexual Assault Care Centre (SACC)
- Comprehensive sexuality education (CSE) advocacy and programme delivery
- Workplace gender equality research and advocacy
- Reproductive rights policy advocacy
- Support for migrant domestic workers, many of whom are women of reproductive age

AWARE's CSE work is of particular relevance. Singapore's school-based sexuality education has historically been conservative, with limited coverage of contraception, consent, and bodily autonomy. AWARE has advocated for more evidence-based, comprehensive sexuality education and has developed its own CSE resources and training programmes that align with international standards — including the UNESCO International Technical Guidance on Sexuality Education — which UNFPA co-publishes.

For UNFPA, AWARE is a natural and immediate partner for SRHR advocacy in Singapore. While the foundation funding landscape does not readily support explicit SRHR advocacy (most foundations use clinical rather than rights-based framing), AWARE's CSE and reproductive rights work provides the civil society infrastructure for this component of UNFPA's mandate. Joint UNFPA-AWARE advocacy on CSE standards, reproductive health access, and gender-based violence response would be a credible and mutually beneficial partnership.

AWARE receives funding from multiple sources including government grants, membership fees, and philanthropy. Its size and profile make it a credible co-convener for UNFPA events in Singapore and a respected voice in any government consultation on reproductive health policy.

---

### 6.2 KK Women's and Children's Hospital (KKH)

KKH is Singapore's tertiary women's and children's hospital, part of the SingHealth cluster, and one of the busiest obstetric hospitals in Southeast Asia. Founded in 1858, KKH celebrated its centenary in October 2024, raising S$30 million or more in philanthropic funding for its centenary campaign.

KKH's role in this landscape extends beyond that of a health service provider. As described in earlier chapters, KKH serves as the institutional bridge between UNFPA and Singapore's major health philanthropists: Temasek Foundation (IMCWH and SingHealth network), Tanoto Foundation (CHaMP centre), and Shaw Foundation (paediatric research) all have active giving relationships with KKH or SingHealth more broadly.

Clinically, KKH's areas of expertise directly align with UNFPA's programme priorities: high-risk obstetrics, foetal medicine, neonatal intensive care, paediatric subspecialties, women's oncology, and sexual and reproductive health services. KKH's research output in these areas positions it as a credible technical partner for UNFPA-led comparative health research across the region.

KKH's international programme activity — including the WAH Foundation partnership (detailed below) — demonstrates an institutional appetite for engagement beyond Singapore's borders. This makes KKH a particularly valuable partner for UNFPA's regional programming.

The recommended partnership model is a formal Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) between UNFPA and KKH, covering research collaboration, shared data, and co-programme design for Southeast Asian MRCH initiatives. Such an MOU would also serve as a signal to KKH's philanthropic supporters — particularly Temasek Foundation and Tanoto Foundation — of UNFPA's institutional credibility and operational engagement in the Singapore health ecosystem.

### 6.3 WAH Foundation

WAH Foundation (Women and Health Foundation) was established to address the critical shortage of skilled midwives in Southeast Asia. Operating in partnership with KKH, WAH Foundation runs a midwifery training programme in Cambodia that has trained more than 400 midwives since its inception.

The programme's reported outcome — approximately a 90% reduction in maternal mortality rates in the areas served by WAH Foundation-trained midwives — is a striking indicator of impact, and is consistent with global evidence on the relationship between skilled birth attendance and maternal mortality. UNFPA's global advocacy for universal skilled birth attendance and its investments in midwifery workforce development globally make WAH Foundation among the most directly aligned civil society partners in this entire landscape.

WAH Foundation is relatively small by the standards of other organisations in this report, but its operational model — high-impact, evidence-grounded, KKH-affiliated — makes it a credible co-implementer for UNFPA's SE Asia midwifery and maternal health programmes. The KKH partnership also means that WAH Foundation has institutional visibility with Temasek Foundation and Tanoto Foundation, the two largest health funders in Singapore.

UNFPA should pursue a programme partnership with WAH Foundation, potentially including co-funding from Tanoto Foundation's MRF (which covers maternal health in the region) or from AVPN's MNCHN fund. The three-way structure — UNFPA as technical leader, WAH Foundation as operational implementer, Tanoto/AVPN as funders — is a model that could be scaled to other SE Asian countries.

### 6.4 Singapore Children's Society

Singapore Children's Society (SCS) is one of Singapore's oldest and largest children's charities, providing services for child welfare, child protection, and family support across Singapore. In FY2024, SCS reached 20,187 children and families through its programmes.

SCS's primary relevance to UNFPA is in the intersection of child welfare and the broader MRCH continuum: children who are born into low-income or high-risk family environments, whose mothers may have experienced complications in pregnancy or delivery, and who require early developmental support. SCS's case management and family support services serve as the post-natal and early childhood continuation of the maternal health journey.

SCS also provides services relevant to adolescent reproductive health, including programmes for at-risk youth and family life education. These services align with UNFPA's adolescent sexual and reproductive health priorities.

For UNFPA, SCS is a potential operational partner for programmes at the intersection of maternal health outcomes and child welfare — particularly programmes addressing the consequences of postnatal depression, early childhood neglect, or family breakdown for child developmental outcomes. SCS also provides a link to MSF's child welfare infrastructure, which is relevant for UNFPA's engagement with Singapore's government social service system.

### 6.5 Club Rainbow

Club Rainbow is a Singapore charity providing comprehensive support to children with chronic life-threatening conditions and their families. The charity's programmes include medical equipment assistance, educational support, psychological support, caregiver respite, and holiday programmes.

Club Rainbow's MRCH relevance is primarily at the paediatric end of the health continuum: children with chronic conditions including congenital heart disease, childhood cancers, thalassaemia, and other conditions acquired in the neonatal or early childhood period. The link to UNFPA's mandate is indirect but real: neonatal health outcomes, early childhood nutrition, and antenatal screening programmes directly affect the incidence of some of the conditions that Club Rainbow's families manage.

Club Rainbow receives philanthropic support from multiple sources in Singapore and has an established public profile. For UNFPA, Club Rainbow is a relevant stakeholder for awareness and advocacy purposes — particularly in conversations about the value of antenatal screening, maternal nutrition, and integrated maternity services that can prevent or mitigate the conditions that Club Rainbow's families face.

### 6.6 KidSTART Singapore

KidSTART Singapore is a national early childhood development programme administered by the Ministry of Social and Family Development (MSF) for children in low-income families from birth to six years of age. The programme provides integrated support across health, nutrition, early learning, and family wellbeing.

KidSTART's relevance to UNFPA is multi-dimensional. First, the DBS Foundation-funded KidSTART Pregnant Mum and Baby Nutrition Programme (described in Chapter 3) places KidSTART directly within the maternal nutrition component of MRCH. Second, KidSTART's focus on the first 1,000 days aligns with UNFPA's global first-1,000-days advocacy. Third, KidSTART's MSF linkage means it is part of the government social service infrastructure, giving it access to the most vulnerable families that UNFPA's MRCH work aims to reach.

For UNFPA, KidSTART is an operational programme partner rather than a standalone entity to engage with independently. The DBS Foundation relationship (Chapter 3.4) provides the primary engagement pathway, with KidSTART as the co-implementer within that three-way structure.

### 6.7 Ishk Tolaram Foundation

The Ishk Tolaram Foundation is a Singapore-registered company limited by guarantee with an international programme footprint, primarily in Nigeria and Indonesia. Established by the Tolaram Group — a Singapore-based conglomerate with major operations in Nigeria (including the Indomie noodles brand through a joint venture with Indofood) — the Foundation operates health and education programmes in communities where the Tolaram Group has a commercial presence.

In health, Ishk Tolaram Foundation operates medical facilities and health services in Nigeria and Indonesia. The Nigerian health work is in a region with some of the highest maternal and infant mortality rates in the world; the Indonesian work covers a country that is a UNFPA priority programme market.

For UNFPA, Ishk Tolaram Foundation is a distinctive potential partner: a Singapore-registered philanthropy with active health operations in UNFPA programme geographies, particularly Nigeria. UNFPA's Nigeria country office and Ishk Tolaram Foundation's Nigeria health programmes likely operate in overlapping geographic and programmatic spaces. Surfacing this connection through AVPN or CFS — both of which are likely to have relationships with Tolaram Group — could open a partnership conversation that few other Singapore philanthropies can offer.

### 6.8 Octava Foundation

Octava Foundation is a Singapore-based philanthropy focused on mental wellbeing and education, operating in Singapore and across Southeast Asia. Its mental wellbeing work encompasses community mental health, trauma-informed care, and institutional mental health capacity building.

Octava Foundation's MRCH relevance is at the intersection of mental health and maternal health — specifically, perinatal mental health, which includes antenatal anxiety and depression, postnatal depression, and the psychological dimensions of pregnancy loss and infertility. This intersection is an area of growing clinical attention in Singapore: Temasek Foundation's Richard Magnus Endowment explicitly includes maternal mental health as a priority area.

For UNFPA, Octava Foundation is a potential collaborator on the mental health dimension of MRCH programming. A programme partnership with Octava Foundation and Temasek Foundation (through the Richard Magnus Endowment) on perinatal mental health — combining Octava's mental health expertise, Temasek's financial resources, and UNFPA's global MRCH technical framework — would address a genuine gap in Singapore's MRCH landscape.

## Chapter 7: Cross-cutting Analysis

### 7.1 Funding Flows and Concentration Risk

Singapore's MRCH philanthropic landscape is characterised by a high degree of concentration in a small number of large institutions. The four Tier 1 foundations — Temasek Foundation, Tanoto Foundation, Quantedge Advancement Initiative, and DBS Foundation — dominate active MRCH giving, and all four are structurally linked to the Singapore government through Temasek Holdings, DBS's ownership structure, or government-adjacent institutional networks.

This concentration creates two structural risks for the sector:

**Coordination risk.** When the same group of foundations — Temasek, Tanoto, Quantedge, Gates, through the PAA HHPC — are co-investing in the same pool, the apparent diversity of funders masks underlying interdependencies. If any one anchor funder shifts priorities, the entire HHPC structure may be destabilised. UNFPA should map these interdependencies carefully and avoid building programme structures that depend on a single funder's sustained commitment.

**Government policy alignment risk.** Because all four Tier 1 foundations are government-linked, their MRCH priorities track Singapore government priorities closely. The current MRCH focus is driven in part by Singapore's concern about its falling total fertility rate. If government policy on population shifts — for example, if immigration policy is used more aggressively to compensate for low fertility — foundation MRCH priorities may shift in turn. UNFPA's engagement strategy should be framed around enduring health outcomes rather than demographic policy proxies.

### 7.2 Funding Gaps in the MRCH Landscape

Analysis of the funding landscape reveals several notable gaps that are relevant for UNFPA's positioning:

**Sexual and reproductive health rights (SRHR) framing.** As noted in Chapter 2, no major Singapore foundation uses SRHR rights-based framing. AWARE stands essentially alone as the institutional voice for SRHR in Singapore's civil society. This gap means that UNFPA's rights-based mandate is an area of genuine differentiation, but one that requires careful framing to avoid disconnecting from foundations that use clinical rather than rights-based language.

**Adolescent reproductive health.** Singapore's MRCH philanthropy is almost entirely focused on maternal health (pregnancy, delivery, postnatal) and child health (neonatal, early childhood). Adolescent reproductive health — contraception access, sexuality education, adolescent-friendly services — is largely absent from the foundation funding landscape. AWARE addresses some of this through CSE advocacy, but no major foundation has an adolescent SRHR programme. This represents an opportunity for UNFPA to position itself as the leading voice in a neglected sub-field.

**Regional programming.** Most Singapore foundations give domestically. The three exceptions — Low Tuck Kwong (Indonesia, Cambodia), Ishk Tolaram (Nigeria, Indonesia), and QAI (Philippines) — are insufficient to represent a robust SE Asian MRCH funding ecosystem. AVPN's MNCHN fund and the PAA HHPC are the primary pooled mechanisms for regional giving, but both are early-stage. UNFPA has an opportunity to shape these mechanisms' geographic and programmatic priorities before they become set.

**Mental health in the perinatal period.** Perinatal mental health — antenatal and postnatal depression, pregnancy anxiety, psychological trauma from obstetric complications — is inadequately addressed in Singapore's MRCH funding landscape. Temasek Foundation's Richard Magnus Endowment has identified it as a priority, but operational programmes remain limited. The gap represents an UNFPA-Octava-Temasek collaboration opportunity.

### 7.3 Gender Lens Philanthropy

Gender lens philanthropy — investing with an explicit commitment to improving gender equity outcomes — is an emerging but not yet mainstream practice in Singapore's philanthropic sector. Major foundations do not describe their giving through a gender lens framework, even when the programmes they fund (maternal health, women's fertility, reproductive health) are inherently gendered.

The absence of explicit gender lens framing matters for UNFPA's positioning. In global philanthropy, gender lens investing has become an increasingly standard framework for MRCH funders, used by the Gates Foundation, UN Women, and multiple international development foundations. Singapore's foundations are behind this curve.

UNFPA has an opportunity to introduce gender lens frameworks to Singapore foundations through knowledge-sharing events and workshops — positioning UNFPA as a leader in this methodological evolution and helping foundations understand their existing MRCH giving through a more analytical framework. This kind of technical contribution (education, not fundraising) is a low-cost, high-credibility entry point that opens subsequent partnership conversations.

### 7.4 Geographic Focus: Singapore-First with Selective Exceptions

As Chapter 2 noted, Singapore philanthropy is primarily domestic. Even the large foundations with regional ambitions (Tanoto, which operates in Indonesia) tend to maintain their primary health-giving focus in Singapore.

For UNFPA — whose mandate is global and whose programme work is primarily in developing countries — this Singapore-first orientation is a fundamental challenge. The gap between where Singapore foundations give (Singapore) and where UNFPA programmes operate (SE Asia, Africa, South Asia) must be bridged through three mechanisms:

1. **Establishing UNFPA's Singapore-based work.** UNFPA's LKYSPP partnership and Singapore-based advocacy are the foundations for credibility within Singapore's philanthropic community. Demonstrating programmatic presence in Singapore — through research, knowledge-sharing, and advocacy — is a prerequisite for accessing Singapore philanthropic capital for international programmes.

2. **Using intermediary mechanisms.** PAA's HHPC and AVPN's MNCHN fund are specifically designed to aggregate Singapore-adjacent capital for regional deployment. UNFPA's alignment with these mechanisms is the most efficient path to accessing Singapore capital for international programming.

3. **Leveraging the 2025–2028 overseas donation pilot.** The time-limited tax incentive for overseas humanitarian donations creates a window to attract Singapore foundations and family offices to UNFPA's international work that may not reopen after 2028.

### 7.5 Institutional Memory and Succession

Several of the foundations in this landscape — particularly the Lee Foundation and Ngee Ann Kongsi — have giving traditions rooted in earlier generations of Singapore's founding philanthropists. As those foundations transition to new generations of leadership (the Lee Foundation is now in its third generation of family stewardship), their programme priorities may evolve.

UNFPA should monitor generational transitions in major foundations as signals of potential strategic shift. In global philanthropy, generational transitions have often been accompanied by shifts from traditional institutional giving (hospitals, universities) toward more programmatic, systems-change, or issue-specific giving — a shift that could increase the receptivity of Tier 2 foundations to MRCH-specific proposals.

### 7.6 The Role of Academic Institutions

Singapore's research universities — National University of Singapore (NUS), Nanyang Technological University (NTU), and Duke-NUS Medical School — are significant nodes in the health philanthropy ecosystem, acting as both recipients of philanthropic capital and generators of the research evidence that foundations use to make investment decisions.

Tanoto Foundation's relationship with NUS and Duke-NUS, Ngee Ann Kongsi's S$12.5 million TCM gift to NTU, and the Lee Foundation's endowment of SingHealth Duke-NUS research chairs all demonstrate the academic-philanthropy nexus. For UNFPA, academic partnerships in Singapore serve a dual function: they provide research credibility and evidence-base for MRCH programming, and they create proximity to the foundation programme officers who also engage with these institutions.

The LKYSPP — a Lee Kuan Yew School of Public Policy — is itself part of NUS, meaning UNFPA's LKYSPP partnership gives it an institutional presence within one of the primary academic nodes in Singapore's health philanthropy ecosystem. This institutional positioning should be leveraged actively in all foundation engagement: UNFPA's LKYSPP affiliation signals academic credibility, government adjacency, and regional research authority simultaneously. In conversations with Tanoto Foundation and Temasek Foundation — both of which have active NUS and Duke-NUS relationships — the NUS-LKYSPP connection is a tangible institutional common ground that should be foregrounded from the first meeting.

## Chapter 8: The Family Office Layer

### 8.1 Scale and Context

Singapore is home to approximately 1,400 single-family offices (SFOs) as of 2023, according to the Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS). These SFOs manage wealth accumulated by Singapore's ultra-high-net-worth families under the 13O and 13U fund tax incentive schemes, which provide favourable tax treatment for qualifying funds managed in Singapore.

Total assets under management in Singapore reached S$5.4 trillion in 2022 (MAS data), of which SFO-managed assets represent a significant but not publicly quantified portion. The Singapore government has actively marketed the city-state as the preferred domicile for Asian and global family office capital, with regulatory stability, political neutrality, and tax incentives as the primary draw.

### 8.2 Philanthropic Behaviour of Singapore SFOs

Singapore's SFOs are structurally distinct from foundations. A foundation has a defined philanthropic mandate, a board of trustees, a programme team, and formal grant-making processes. A family office is primarily a wealth management entity; its philanthropic activity may be handled by a separate family foundation, through a donor-advised fund at CFS, through direct giving, or through informal family decision-making.

As a result, SFO philanthropy in Singapore is structurally opaque. Unlike foundations, SFOs are not required to publish grant recipient information or annual reports. Their giving is known primarily through recipient disclosures and sector surveys.

What is known from sector research and industry reporting is that:

- A significant and growing proportion of Singapore SFOs include philanthropy as a formal component of their family office mandate
- SFO philanthropy in Singapore is growing in scale, sophistication, and organisation — moving from ad hoc personal giving toward structured family philanthropy programmes
- Health is among the top cause areas for Singapore SFOs, alongside education and environmental sustainability
- MRCH-adjacent causes — maternal health, child development, nutrition, reproductive health — appear within the health priority portfolio of Singapore SFOs, though specific entity-level data is not available

### 8.3 Engagement Strategy for the SFO Layer

UNFPA's engagement with Singapore's SFO philanthropic layer is necessarily different from its engagement with foundations. The pathway to SFO philanthropy is through intermediaries and convening platforms rather than through direct bilateral outreach. Three mechanisms are most relevant:

**CFS.** As noted in Chapter 5.1, CFS is the primary administrative vehicle for structured SFO giving in Singapore. SFOs that wish to establish a named giving fund, maintain a philanthropy programme with administrative support, and access Singapore tax deductions for their giving typically use CFS as the vehicle. UNFPA's engagement with CFS creates indirect access to CFS's SFO donor community.

**AVPN and PAA.** Both AVPN and PAA have SFO membership segments within their broader membership structures. Singapore SFOs that are at the earlier stages of professionalising their philanthropy often join these networks for education, peer learning, and deal sourcing. UNFPA's participation in AVPN and PAA events creates visibility with these SFOs.

**Thematic forums.** Singapore's family office community responds to curated, high-quality thematic learning events — dinners, briefings, and forums that combine peer exchange with expert presentations. UNFPA's global evidence base in MRCH makes it a credible host or co-host for such events, targeted at SFOs whose principals have health or MRCH interests.

### 8.4 The Variable Capital Company (VCC) Framework

Singapore's Variable Capital Company framework, introduced in 2020, enables SFOs to structure pooled giving more efficiently. Several Singapore SFOs are exploring VCC-based philanthropy vehicles as an alternative to CFS donor-advised funds. For UNFPA, VCC-based pooled funds could represent a future channel for accessing Singapore SFO capital for MRCH programmes — particularly if PAA or AVPN develop VCC-based co-investment vehicles for health philanthropy.

The VCC framework is still evolving, and its application to philanthropy (rather than impact investing) is not yet established. UNFPA should monitor developments in this space but does not need to invest significant engagement resources in VCC-related activities in the near term.

## Chapter 9: Strategic Engagement Recommendations for UNFPA

This chapter synthesises the analysis across the preceding chapters into a prioritised, actionable engagement strategy. Recommendations are organised by time horizon and priority tier.

### 9.1 Immediate Actions (0–3 Months)

**1. Initiate AVPN engagement.**
UNFPA should contact AVPN's partnership team to discuss participation in the MNCHN working group as a technical partner. AVPN's 700-member network, active MNCHN pooled fund, and convening infrastructure make this the lowest-cost, highest-reach immediate engagement in Singapore's philanthropic ecosystem. A technical partnership role — providing MRCH evidence, standards, and programme quality inputs — requires no financial commitment from UNFPA and positions UNFPA immediately within Singapore's active MRCH co-investment community.

**2. Establish PAA dialogue.**
PAA's Health for Human Potential Community launched in May 2025 and is in its formative year. UNFPA's window to influence the HHPC's programme design, investment criteria, and governance is narrow. An initial meeting with PAA's secretariat — framed around UNFPA's technical contribution to the HHPC's programme design — should be arranged within the first three months of this strategy's implementation.

**3. Formalise KKH partnership.**
UNFPA's relationship with KKH should be formalised through a Memorandum of Understanding covering research collaboration, data sharing, and co-programme design. This MOU serves both operational purposes and signals to Temasek Foundation, Tanoto Foundation, and other KKH philanthropic supporters that UNFPA is an institutionally credible Singapore health partner.

**4. Launch AWARE co-advocacy dialogue.**
UNFPA should open a formal partnership conversation with AWARE on comprehensive sexuality education, perinatal mental health awareness, and SRHR policy advocacy. This partnership requires no financial commitment and immediately strengthens UNFPA's civil society positioning in Singapore.

### 9.2 Medium-Term Actions (3–12 Months)

**5. Develop a Temasek Foundation proposal.**
UNFPA should develop a proposal for Temasek Foundation's Public Health Innovations grant stream, framed around UNFPA's technical contribution to Singapore's MRCH outcomes. The proposal should align with the Richard Magnus Endowment's stated priorities — maternal mental health, integrated care models, child developmental health — and should demonstrate UNFPA's global evidence base and Southeast Asian comparative experience as distinctive assets.

**6. Engage Tanoto Foundation through the KKH bridge.**
An introduction to Tanoto Foundation's programme team, facilitated through KKH's institutional relationships, should lead to a meeting framed around research collaboration rather than funding. UNFPA's contribution to Tanoto Foundation's MRF research agenda — specifically around fertility interventions and neonatal health in Southeast Asian populations — is the value proposition.

**7. Establish CFS relationship.**
UNFPA should approach CFS to discuss how Singapore-sourced philanthropic capital can be channelled to UNFPA's international programmes through CFS's donor-advised fund infrastructure. This engagement is primarily administrative but is strategically important for accessing the SFO philanthropic layer.

**8. Attend PAA Annual Summit and AVPN Conference.**
UNFPA's programme leadership should be present at both annual convening events — PAA's summit and AVPN's annual conference — in the first year of engagement. These events are the primary venues for relationship-building with Singapore philanthropic leaders and provide UNFPA with visibility across the full ecosystem simultaneously.

### 9.3 Long-Term Actions (12–36 Months)

**9. Develop a DBS Foundation co-programme on first-1,000-days nutrition.**
Building on DBS Foundation's KidSTART Pregnant Mum and Baby Nutrition Programme, UNFPA should develop a co-programme proposal that extends the programme model to UNFPA's SE Asian geographies — using Singapore as the design and evidence base and deploying the model in Indonesia, the Philippines, or Vietnam. DBS Foundation's regional infrastructure and UNFPA's SE Asian programming network create a natural complementarity.

**10. Cultivate Moh Family Foundation and Low Tuck Kwong Foundation.**
Both foundations require intermediary introductions and longer cultivation timelines. UNFPA should register interest with CFS and AVPN as potential introduction channels, and should monitor both foundations' giving patterns and any disclosed programme priorities. The Low Tuck Kwong Foundation's Indonesia connection is the highest-potential angle; the Moh Family Foundation's health concentration is the primary signal of receptivity.

**11. Position WAH Foundation for expanded co-funding.**
UNFPA should work with WAH Foundation and Tanoto Foundation to explore whether the WAH Foundation midwifery training model — currently operating in Cambodia — can be expanded to additional SE Asian countries with co-funding from Tanoto Foundation's MRF and UNFPA's regional programme budgets.

**12. Develop a perinatal mental health programme with Octava and Temasek.**
Building on the intersection between Temasek Foundation's Richard Magnus Endowment priorities and Octava Foundation's mental health expertise, UNFPA should develop a perinatal mental health programme proposal that addresses the documented gap in Singapore's MRCH landscape. This three-way programme model — UNFPA as MRCH technical anchor, Octava as mental health specialist, Temasek Foundation as funder — could become a model for integrated MRCH programming in Singapore.

### 9.4 Framing and Positioning Principles

Across all engagements, UNFPA should observe four framing principles derived from the analysis in this report:

**Use clinical framing, not rights-based framing, with foundations.** Singapore foundations respond to evidence, outcomes data, and health system logic. Rights-based SRHR language should be reserved for AWARE and civil society engagements.

**Lead with technical value, not funding needs.** UNFPA's primary asset in Singapore's philanthropic ecosystem is its global evidence base and technical authority in MRCH. Engagement should be framed as UNFPA contributing technical value to foundation programmes, not as UNFPA seeking funding. This framing is accurate, credible, and strategically more effective.

**Leverage Singapore as a model for the region.** Several Tier 1 foundations have regional ambitions but concentrate their giving in Singapore. UNFPA's opportunity is to connect Singapore's MRCH investments to regional programme deployment — using Singapore as the evidence-generating laboratory and UNFPA's SE Asian network as the scale-deployment vehicle.

**Respect long cultivation timelines for Tier 2 foundations.** The Lee Foundation, Ngee Ann Kongsi, and Moh Family Foundation give through relationships, not grant cycles. UNFPA should invest in the relationships without expecting short-term programmatic outcomes.

### 9.5 Monitoring and Evaluation of the Engagement Strategy

UNFPA should establish a simple tracking system for its Singapore philanthropic engagement, covering: (a) status of each bilateral relationship by tier; (b) proposals submitted, under review, or approved; (c) co-investment vehicles joined (HHPC, AVPN MNCHN); and (d) events attended and contacts made.

The engagement strategy should be reviewed annually, aligned with the publication of Soristic's Giving in Singapore report and the PAA annual summit. Material changes in foundation strategies — new endowments, strategic reorientations, leadership transitions — should trigger an ad hoc review of affected entities in this directory.

UNFPA-R-05 (the structured KB document) should be updated whenever material new data is available, with a corresponding note in the LAST_VERIFIED field. This annex report should be substantially updated every two years to reflect evolving foundation profiles and updated disbursement data.

Key performance indicators for the engagement strategy include:
- At least one formal partnership agreement (MOU or programme agreement) with a Tier 1 foundation within 18 months
- Participation in AVPN MNCHN working group within 3 months
- UNFPA represented at PAA annual summit within the first year
- CFS relationship established within 6 months
- KKH MOU signed within 12 months

## Chapter 10: Conclusion

Singapore's philanthropic landscape offers UNFPA a genuinely distinctive and high-value engagement environment. The concentration of major MRCH-aligned foundations in a single city-state, the emergence of the PAA Health for Human Potential Community as a US$100 million co-investment vehicle, and the active convening infrastructure provided by AVPN and CFS create conditions that UNFPA will rarely find in a single national context.

The critical strategic insight from this analysis is that Singapore should not be approached primarily as a fundraising market. The foundations profiled in this report — particularly Temasek Foundation, Tanoto Foundation, and Quantedge — are sophisticated institutional actors with their own programme expertise, research partnerships, and strategic frameworks. The most productive UNFPA engagement will be peer collaboration: UNFPA contributing its global evidence base, technical authority, and SE Asian programme experience to foundations that have Singapore-based capital but desire regional scale and global credibility.

The PAA Health for Human Potential Community represents the most time-sensitive and highest-priority opportunity in this landscape. Launched in May 2025 with a five-year mobilisation horizon to 2030, the HHPC is precisely the kind of sustained, pooled, multi-foundation vehicle through which UNFPA can access Singapore philanthropic capital for SE Asian MRCH programming at scale. UNFPA's technical participation in the HHPC's formative phase — influencing programme design, investment criteria, and geographic focus — would position UNFPA as the de facto technical anchor for the region's most significant new MRCH co-investment mechanism.

Alongside the PAA engagement, the KKH institutional partnership, the AVPN MNCHN working group, and the AWARE civil society partnership form the minimum viable engagement portfolio for UNFPA in Singapore. These four relationships — a hospital, a convening network, a civil society advocate, and a pooled fund — cover the full spectrum of the Singapore MRCH ecosystem and provide UNFPA with the institutional presence needed to access the broader landscape over the medium term.

Singapore's philanthropic sector is maturing rapidly. The professionalism of foundation programme teams, the emergence of intermediary infrastructure (PAA, AVPN, CFS), and the growing appetite for evidence-based, systems-change philanthropy all favour UNFPA's technical approach and mandate. The timing of engagement — 2026, in the formative year of the PAA HHPC and the early years of the Richard Magnus Endowment — is favourable. The strategy outlined in this report, if pursued with consistency and patience, positions UNFPA to become a trusted institutional partner in Singapore's MRCH philanthropic ecosystem over the next five years.

## Appendix B: Methodology and Data Sources

### Primary Sources

All data in this report is drawn from publicly available materials. No proprietary data, confidential briefings, or non-public foundation disclosures were used. The principal sources are:

**Foundation websites and annual reports.** Where foundations publish annual reports — Temasek Foundation, Tanoto Foundation, DBS Foundation, Lien Foundation, CFS, Ngee Ann Kongsi, Singapore Children's Society — these are the primary source for programme descriptions, disbursement figures, and strategic priorities. Annual reports cited are the most recent publicly available edition as of April 2026, generally FY2024 or FY2023/24 editions.

**Soristic Giving in Singapore 2024/2025 Report.** Soristic's annual report is the primary aggregate sector reference and the principal source for disbursement figures for foundations that do not publish their own annual reports (including Moh Family Foundation, Low Tuck Kwong Foundation, Shaw Foundation, and Lee Foundation). Soristic tracks 126 organisations; its methodology is proprietary but its data has been consistent and credible across multiple editions.

**CharityGuidePoint.sg and Commissioner of Charities data.** For registered charity and IPC information, CharityGuidePoint.sg (which aggregates COC data) is the primary reference. Charity registration status and IPC designations are drawn from this source.

**Government and regulatory sources.** MAS publications are the source for SFO count (2023) and AUM (2022) figures. IRAS publications are the source for IPC tax deduction information. Singapore Budget 2025 is the source for the overseas humanitarian donation pilot.

**Institutional and media sources.** Hospital websites (KKH, SingHealth), programme organisation websites (AVPN, PAA, WAH Foundation, AWARE, KidSTART, Club Rainbow, Ishk Tolaram, Octava), and Singapore media (CNA, Business Times) provided programme-level detail and announcement verification.

### Data Reliability Notes

- Foundation self-reported figures (e.g., Tanoto Foundation's "S$20M+ since 2009") are not independently audited. They are reported as stated by the source.
- Soristic disbursement figures for foundations without public annual reports should be treated as indicative rather than audited.
- SFO philanthropy data is structurally unavailable at the entity level; Chapter 8 relies on sector-level reports and MAS aggregate data.
- All figures are in Singapore dollars (S$) unless otherwise noted. US dollar figures are as stated in source documents and have not been converted.

### Update Cadence

This report should be updated annually, aligned with the publication cycle of Soristic's Giving in Singapore report (typically released in the first half of each calendar year). Material changes — new funding announcements, foundation strategic shifts, new convening vehicles — should be incorporated as they occur through amendments to UNFPA-R-05 (the structured KB document) with a note that the annex report requires corresponding update.
