## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML) applications relevant to UNFPA's mandate are emerging rapidly across population data, health service delivery, commodity supply chains, and programme targeting. The most developed applications include: AI-assisted population estimation from satellite imagery, machine learning for contraceptive demand forecasting, natural language processing for analysing GBV case data, and AI-enhanced cervical cancer screening. Less mature but promising applications include predictive models for maternal mortality risk, AI chatbots for SRHR counselling, and algorithmic tools for optimising health facility placement.

WHO published its guidance on the ethics and governance of AI for health in 2021, establishing six principles: protecting human autonomy, promoting well-being and safety, ensuring transparency and explainability, fostering accountability, ensuring inclusiveness and equity, and promoting responsive and sustainable AI. These principles are directly relevant to UNFPA's mandate, where AI applications must navigate sensitive SRHR data, serve populations with limited digital literacy, and avoid perpetuating biases against women, marginalised groups, and LMICs.

The honest assessment is that AI in SRHR programming is at an early stage. Most applications are pilots with limited evidence on effectiveness, scalability, or cost-effectiveness. The risk of "AI washing" — branding conventional digital tools as AI for novelty or funding appeal — is real. UNFPA's approach should be evidence-based: adopting AI where it demonstrably improves outcomes over existing methods, investing in data infrastructure that enables responsible AI use, and resisting the pressure to deploy AI as a substitute for the human resources, funding, and political will that SRHR progress ultimately requires.

## KEY FACTS

- **WHO AI guidance (2021)**: "Ethics and governance of artificial intelligence for health" established six guiding principles for health AI
- **AI for population estimation**: Machine learning applied to satellite imagery can estimate population density at fine spatial resolution; used by WorldPop, Meta, and GRID3 — all UNFPA partners
- **AI for cervical cancer screening**: Visual Inspection with Acetic Acid (VIA) enhanced with AI image analysis has shown sensitivity rates of 80–95% in pilot studies — comparable to expert colposcopy
- **AI for demand forecasting**: ML models predicting contraceptive demand have shown 10–20% improvement in forecast accuracy over traditional methods in pilot settings
- **NLP for GBV data**: Natural language processing can analyse GBV case management data to identify patterns, risk factors, and service gaps — piloted in a small number of UNFPA-supported settings
- **Chatbots for SRHR**: AI-powered conversational agents providing FP information and counselling have been piloted in several countries; evidence on effectiveness is very limited
- **Bias risk**: AI systems trained on biased data can perpetuate discrimination — e.g., models trained on health data that underrepresents women, rural populations, or specific ethnic groups
- **Data infrastructure**: Most LMICs lack the data infrastructure (digitised health records, standardised data formats, computing capacity) needed for effective AI deployment
- **UNFPA Innovation Fund**: Supports AI-related pilot projects in population data, SRHR, and GBV
- **Responsible AI frameworks**: UN Secretary-General's Roadmap for Digital Cooperation (2020) and the AI Advisory Body (2023) both call for human rights-based AI governance

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### AI in the Development Context

AI has moved from a niche technology to a mainstream topic in international development. The World Bank, UNDP, WHO, and bilateral donors have all invested in AI for development applications. However, the AI landscape in LMICs is fundamentally different from high-income countries:
- Data infrastructure is weaker (less digitised health data, less internet connectivity)
- Computing resources are limited (cloud computing costs, electricity unreliability)
- Technical expertise is concentrated in a few hubs (Nairobi, Lagos, Bangalore) rather than distributed
- Regulatory frameworks for AI are absent in most LMICs
- The populations that AI could most benefit are those least represented in training data

### AI Applications in UNFPA's Mandate Areas

**Population data and census**: AI and ML are used to estimate population distribution from satellite imagery (building footprint detection, settlement classification). This is directly relevant to UNFPA's census support mandate and is the most mature AI application in UNFPA's work.

**Maternal health**: Predictive models that identify high-risk pregnancies based on clinical and demographic data have been developed in research settings. However, deployment in LMICs requires electronic health records that are often unavailable, and clinical validation in diverse populations is limited.

**Family planning**: ML for demand forecasting — predicting how many contraceptives of which types will be needed in which locations — can improve supply chain efficiency. UNFPA has supported pilots in several countries, with promising early results on forecast accuracy.

**GBV**: NLP and text mining can analyse large volumes of GBV case reports to identify patterns (seasonal variation, geographic clustering, repeat perpetration) that are not visible from manual review. This is at the research stage.

**Cervical cancer**: AI-enhanced VIA screening is one of the most promising AI health applications for LMICs. The technology enables community health workers or nurses (not specialist pathologists) to perform cervical cancer screening with AI providing diagnostic support. UNFPA supports cervical cancer prevention in several countries where this technology could be deployed.

## DETAIL

### Opportunities

**Population estimation**: This is the clearest value-add for UNFPA. AI-assisted building detection from satellite imagery, combined with statistical modelling, produces population density estimates at approximately 100m resolution. For countries where the last census is 10+ years old, this provides critical interim data for health planning, resource allocation, and humanitarian response. The technology is relatively mature and the data products are openly available.

**Supply chain optimisation**: ML-based demand forecasting and optimisation of distribution routes can reduce contraceptive stockouts and wastage. The data requirements (historical consumption data, seasonal patterns, service delivery statistics) are available in countries with functioning LMIS, making this a feasible near-term application.

**Screening and diagnosis**: AI-enhanced cervical cancer screening has the potential to dramatically expand screening coverage in settings without pathology infrastructure. If validated at scale, this could be transformative for cervical cancer prevention — a growing UNFPA programme area.

### Risks and Limitations

**Bias and fairness**: AI models trained on data from high-income countries or majority populations may perform poorly or discriminate against LMICs, ethnic minorities, or women. In health AI, misdiagnosis due to training data bias can have direct harm. UNFPA must insist on validation in the populations where AI tools will be deployed.

**Privacy**: SRHR data is among the most sensitive personal information. AI applications that process data on contraceptive use, GBV experiences, HIV status, or abortion require robust data governance — encryption, access controls, informed consent, and compliance with data protection laws (where they exist).

**Accountability**: When an AI system contributes to a clinical decision (e.g., cervical cancer screening result), who is accountable if the system is wrong? The "black box" nature of some ML models makes accountability difficult. WHO's 2021 guidance calls for explainability and human oversight.

**Digital colonialism**: AI tools developed by well-resourced institutions in high-income countries and deployed in LMICs without local input, adaptation, or ownership can perpetuate dependency. UNFPA should prioritise partnerships with local AI developers and support capacity building in national institutions.

**AI washing**: Labelling conventional digital tools (spreadsheet automation, basic data visualisation) as "AI" to attract funding or attention is a real phenomenon in the development sector. UNFPA should maintain honest language about what constitutes genuine AI innovation versus competent digital implementation.

---

## CURRENT STATUS

- AI in UNFPA programming is at early/pilot stages for most applications
- Population estimation from satellite imagery is the most mature application and is operationally integrated
- Cervical cancer AI screening is promising but not yet deployed at scale through UNFPA
- Supply chain ML is being piloted in a handful of countries
- UNFPA's Innovation Fund continues to support AI-related projects
- The UN system is developing AI governance frameworks; UNFPA's approach aligns with WHO and Secretary-General guidance
- The risk of overinvestment in AI relative to foundational health system needs (staffing, infrastructure, supply chains) should be monitored
