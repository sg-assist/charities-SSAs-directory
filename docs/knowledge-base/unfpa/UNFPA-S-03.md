## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Approximately 281 million people are international migrants (IOM 2024), and over 117 million are forcibly displaced (UNHCR 2024) — the highest numbers ever recorded. Migrants, refugees, internally displaced persons (IDPs), and stateless populations face specific SRHR challenges: disrupted access to maternal care, contraception, and STI treatment; heightened GBV risk (trafficking, exploitation, conflict-related sexual violence); legal barriers to healthcare in host countries; language and cultural barriers; and discrimination. Women and girls constitute approximately half of all displaced populations and face disproportionate reproductive health risks.

UNFPA's role in migration and displacement SRHR intersects with its humanitarian mandate (MISP implementation, GBV sub-cluster leadership) and its development programming (supporting national health systems to include migrant populations). The organisation works alongside UNHCR (the lead agency for refugees), IOM (the lead agency for migration), and national governments to ensure that SRHR services reach mobile populations.

Key policy frameworks include the Global Compact on Refugees (2018) and the Global Compact for Safe, Orderly and Regular Migration (2018), both of which reference health including SRHR. However, implementation varies enormously — some host countries integrate refugees into national health systems (Uganda, Turkey), while others maintain parallel systems or restrict access entirely.

## KEY FACTS

- **International migrants**: 281 million (IOM 2024) — 3.6% of global population
- **Forcibly displaced**: 117 million+ (UNHCR mid-2024) — including 37 million refugees, 6.9 million asylum seekers, and 68+ million IDPs
- **Women and girls**: Approximately 50% of displaced populations; face disproportionate SRHR and GBV risks
- **Maternal health**: Refugees and migrants face higher maternal mortality due to disrupted care, late presentation, language barriers, and discrimination
- **GBV in displacement**: Estimated 1 in 5 displaced women experiences sexual violence; actual rates likely higher
- **Contraceptive access**: Disrupted supply chains, lack of method choice, and provider unfamiliarity with refugee health needs reduce FP access
- **Transit migration**: Women and girls migrating through Central America, the Mediterranean, and Southeast Asian routes face extreme sexual violence risks
- **Venezuela displacement**: 7+ million displaced; major SRHR needs across host countries
- **Syria displacement**: 6+ million refugees, 6+ million IDPs; UNFPA's largest sustained humanitarian SRHR operation
- **Climate displacement**: Estimated 21.5 million people displaced annually by weather-related events; SRHR needs during climate displacement are emerging
- **Global Compact on Refugees (2018)**: Includes health and SRHR in the comprehensive response framework
- **Host country integration**: Uganda (progressive approach, refugees in national health system) vs. Bangladesh (restricted Rohingya access to national services)

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### Types of Mobile Populations

SRHR needs and service delivery approaches vary by population type:

**Refugees** (fled across international border, protected by 1951 Convention): May be in camps (where UNFPA provides direct/partner services) or urban settings (where they should ideally access national health systems). Legal status typically allows some healthcare access, though in practice barriers are significant.

**IDPs** (displaced within their own country): Legally remain citizens with rights to national services, but in practice face access barriers due to conflict, infrastructure destruction, and discrimination. UNFPA reaches IDPs through humanitarian operations and government health system support.

**Labour migrants**: Documented and undocumented migrants in foreign countries. SRHR access depends on legal status, insurance coverage, and host country policies. Undocumented migrants face the most severe access barriers.

**Transit migrants**: People in transit through countries (e.g., Central Americans through Mexico, Africans through Libya) face extreme vulnerability — sexual violence, exploitation, absence of any health services.

**Stateless persons**: Approximately 4.4 million stateless people (likely an undercount) lack nationality and often lack access to any government services, including SRHR.

## DETAIL

### SRHR Challenges for Displaced Populations

**Maternal health disruption**: Displacement disrupts ANC continuity — a pregnant woman who was receiving regular ANC in her home country may present at a camp or host community facility at advanced gestational stage with no previous records. Emergency obstetric care capacity in displacement settings is often limited. Maternal mortality among displaced populations is consistently higher than host community rates.

**Contraceptive access**: Family planning supply chains are disrupted during displacement. Method mix is often restricted (condoms and pills available; implants, IUDs, and injectables less so). Provider counselling may be unavailable in refugees' languages. Post-conflict and post-displacement, fertility desires may change (some women want to replace lost children; others want to delay pregnancy in unstable conditions) — flexible FP services must accommodate diverse desires.

**GBV in displacement**: Sexual violence in displacement occurs across the displacement cycle — during conflict (conflict-related sexual violence), during flight (sexual exploitation at checkpoints, by smugglers, in transit), in camps (intimate partner violence, exploitation by camp authorities), and in host communities (exploitation, trafficking). UNFPA leads the GBV sub-cluster and provides safe spaces, case management, and referral in major displacement operations.

**Adolescent SRHR in displacement**: Displaced adolescent girls face compounded risks — child marriage as a "coping mechanism" by families, sexual exploitation, disrupted education, limited access to youth-friendly services, and absence of CSE. UNFPA prioritises adolescent SRHR in humanitarian settings but reach is limited relative to need.

### UNFPA's Operational Response

UNFPA responds to displacement-related SRHR needs through:
- MISP implementation in acute displacement
- GBV sub-cluster coordination
- Reproductive health kits pre-positioned and distributed
- Clinical management of rape
- Safe spaces for women and girls
- Midwife deployment in camp and host community settings
- Integration of refugees/IDPs into national SRHR service delivery
- Data collection on reproductive health needs of displaced populations

## CURRENT STATUS

- Forced displacement is at historic highs and increasing due to conflict (Sudan, Ukraine, Myanmar, Gaza) and climate
- UNFPA's humanitarian SRHR operations span all major displacement crises
- Integration of displaced populations into national health systems remains the most sustainable approach but faces political and fiscal barriers
- Climate-related displacement is adding a new category of SRHR-vulnerable mobile populations
- The humanitarian-development nexus requires UNFPA to balance emergency response with long-term health system strengthening
