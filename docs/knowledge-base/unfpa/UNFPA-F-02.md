## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The distinction between core (regular) and non-core (other) resources is the most consequential structural issue in UNFPA's financing. Core resources — un-earmarked voluntary contributions that UNFPA can allocate according to its strategic plan — have declined from approximately 55–60 per cent of total revenue a decade ago to roughly 35 per cent by 2023. Non-core resources — earmarked by donors for specific countries, themes, or emergencies — now account for the majority of UNFPA's budget.

This shift is not unique to UNFPA. Across the UN development system, the trend toward earmarked funding is well-documented. The 2019 UN Funding Compact committed member states to increasing the share of core contributions to at least 30 per cent of total funding to UN development entities; most have not met this target. The Quadrennial Comprehensive Policy Review (QCPR) resolutions have repeatedly called for more predictable, flexible funding, but donor behaviour has moved in the opposite direction.

For UNFPA specifically, the consequences are severe. Core funding underwrites country office presence, staff positions, and flexible programming in the poorest countries with the greatest SRHR needs. Non-core funding directs resources toward donor priorities, which may or may not align with the greatest need. The transaction costs of managing hundreds of earmarked contributions fragment the organisation's attention and create perverse incentives to chase funding rather than pursue strategy.

This document analyses the core/non-core dynamic, its causes, consequences, and the range of responses available to UNFPA and its governing board.

## KEY FACTS

- **Core funding share (2013)**: Approximately 55% of total UNFPA revenue
- **Core funding share (2023)**: Approximately 35% of total UNFPA revenue — a 20-percentage-point decline in one decade
- **Number of non-core contributions managed**: UNFPA manages several hundred distinct earmarked funding agreements annually, each with specific reporting and accountability requirements
- **UN Funding Compact target**: Member states committed in 2019 to providing at least 30% of contributions as core/un-earmarked; most donors have not met this target
- **QCPR**: General Assembly resolution 77/283 (2024 QCPR) reiterated calls for increased core funding; similar language has appeared in every QCPR since 2012
- **Thematic funds**: UNFPA has created pooled thematic funds (e.g., the UNFPA Supplies Partnership, the Maternal Health Thematic Fund) as "softly earmarked" alternatives to project-specific earmarking — these represent a compromise between core and non-core
- **Nordic donors**: Provide the highest core-to-non-core ratios; Sweden typically contributes 50–60% of its UNFPA funding as core
- **UK and Germany**: Have shifted toward more heavily earmarked contributions over the past decade
- **Humanitarian funding**: Almost entirely non-core; CERF allocations, country-based pooled funds, and bilateral emergency contributions are by definition earmarked
- **Cross-subsidy concern**: Core resources effectively subsidise the administration of non-core projects, as the programme support cost recovery rate (typically 8%) does not fully cover indirect costs
- **Staff costs**: Approximately 50–60% of core resources go to staffing and institutional costs; this means core cuts directly threaten country office viability
- **Resource allocation formula**: Only applies to core programme resources; non-core allocations bypass the needs-based formula entirely

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### Why Donors Earmark

The shift toward earmarked funding reflects several donor-side dynamics:

1. **Domestic accountability pressure**: Parliaments and taxpayers demand visibility on where aid money goes. "We funded 10,000 dignity kits in Yemen" is more communicable than "we contributed to UNFPA's general operating budget."

2. **Results frameworks**: Bilateral aid agencies are under pressure to demonstrate results. Earmarked contributions allow donors to claim credit for specific outputs and link their funding to measurable indicators.

3. **Risk management**: Donors perceive earmarked funding as lower-risk because they can control where money goes and audit specific projects. Core funding requires trust in the multilateral's own allocation and oversight systems.

4. **Political signalling**: Earmarking allows donors to demonstrate political priorities — funding GBV programming, or family planning, or humanitarian response — rather than making an undifferentiated contribution.

5. **Fragmentation of aid architecture**: The proliferation of global funds, vertical initiatives, and multi-donor trust funds has created a culture of earmarking across the aid system.

### The System-Wide Problem

UNFPA is not alone. Across the UN development system:

- UNDP's core funding has fallen from over 60% in the early 2000s to approximately 20% by 2023
- UNICEF's regular resources represent approximately 20–25% of total revenue
- UN Women, UNAIDS, and other smaller entities face even more acute core funding shortfalls
- The Secretary-General's "Our Common Agenda" (2021) and the 2024 Summit of the Future both flagged inadequate core funding as a systemic risk to multilateral effectiveness

The Dag Hammarskjöld Foundation's annual "Financing the UN Development System" reports have documented this trend comprehensively, noting that the UN development system increasingly operates as a "funding pass-through" rather than as a strategic actor with the flexibility to allocate resources based on need.

## DETAIL

### What Core Funding Pays For

Understanding why core matters requires understanding what it finances:

**1. Country office presence**: UNFPA maintains offices in approximately 150 countries. The basic costs of a country office — representative, programme staff, operations, office space — are funded primarily from core resources. In small, low-profile countries (e.g., Timor-Leste, Lesotho, Bhutan), core funding may be the only source of revenue. Without it, the office closes and UNFPA loses presence precisely where SRHR needs may be acute but donor interest is low.

**2. Staff positions**: UNFPA's global workforce is funded through a mix of core and non-core. Core-funded positions provide organisational stability and institutional memory. Non-core funded positions are project-dependent — when the project ends, the position ends, creating high staff turnover and loss of expertise.

**3. Flexible programming**: Core resources allow UNFPA country offices to respond to emerging needs, pilot new approaches, and invest in areas that do not attract earmarked donor interest. This includes sensitive programming areas like comprehensive sexuality education, SRHR for marginalised populations, and advocacy on reproductive rights — areas where donors may be reluctant to be publicly identified.

**4. Normative work**: UNFPA's contribution to global norms, standards, and policy frameworks (through technical guidance, research, advocacy, and intergovernmental engagement) is funded primarily from core resources. This work does not produce the tangible, countable outputs that earmarked donors typically require.

**5. Evaluation and oversight**: The Evaluation Office, internal audit, and ethics functions are funded from institutional budgets that depend on core revenue. Cutting core resources can weaken the very oversight mechanisms that donors rely on.

### Consequences of the Core Decline

**Geographic distortion**: The resource allocation formula directs core resources to countries with the greatest SRHR needs (high maternal mortality, high unmet need for family planning, high adolescent fertility). When core shrinks, these needs-based allocations shrink proportionally. Meanwhile, non-core funding flows to wherever donors direct it — often humanitarian emergencies (Middle East, Ukraine) or politically visible contexts. The result is that the poorest, most neglected countries receive less while high-profile crises receive more.

**Thematic distortion**: Some SRHR issues attract strong donor earmarking (GBV, maternal health, humanitarian response). Others do not (population data, census support, comprehensive sexuality education, adolescent SRHR outside of HIV). The non-core funding landscape creates a two-tier programme: well-funded visible themes and underfunded but critical background work.

**Transaction cost burden**: Each earmarked contribution requires a separate project document, workplan, budget, reporting cycle, and often a separate audit. Managing hundreds of these agreements consumes senior management attention, financial staff time, and programme officer bandwidth that could otherwise be directed toward delivery. Studies across the UN system have estimated that earmarked project management can consume 15–25% of programme staff time in reporting and compliance activities alone.

**Strategic incoherence**: When the majority of funding is earmarked, the strategic plan becomes aspirational. UNFPA's Executive Board approves a four-year strategic plan with defined priorities, but actual programming is shaped by what donors fund rather than what the plan prioritises. This creates a gap between stated strategy and operational reality.

**Perverse incentives**: Country offices under financial pressure have incentives to chase non-core funding by designing projects that match donor interests rather than country needs. This can lead to mission drift — a country office in a Pacific island nation writing proposals for gender-based violence programming (because that's where the money is) rather than investing in census support (which is what the country most needs).

### Mitigation Strategies

UNFPA and its peers have developed several approaches to manage the core/non-core challenge:

**1. Thematic/pooled funds**: UNFPA has created several pooled funds that function as "softly earmarked" — donors contribute to a thematic pool (e.g., the UNFPA Supplies Partnership for family planning commodities, the Maternal Health Thematic Fund) with broad thematic direction but without country-level or project-level earmarks. These provide more flexibility than bilateral non-core while giving donors thematic visibility.

**2. Multi-year framework agreements**: Long-term agreements with anchor donors (typically Nordic countries) provide multi-year core commitments with joint strategic reviews. These improve predictability even within the core funding channel.

**3. Cost recovery reform**: UNFPA has sought to improve programme support cost recovery rates to ensure that non-core projects pay their fair share of institutional overhead. The standard 8% recovery rate is widely considered insufficient; UNFPA and other agencies have explored differentiated rates for different types of contributions.

**4. Resource mobilisation diversification**: Expanding the donor base beyond traditional European donors — engaging Gulf states, Asian middle-income countries, private sector, and foundations — is a stated priority, though progress has been slow.

**5. Efficiency gains**: UNFPA has pursued internal efficiency reforms (business operations strategy, shared services, common premises under UN reform) to reduce institutional costs and protect programme delivery from core funding cuts.

**6. Advocacy**: Through the Executive Board, UNFPA leadership consistently advocates for increased core funding, citing the Funding Compact commitments and QCPR language. This advocacy has not reversed the trend but may have slowed the decline.

---

## CURRENT STATUS

As of 2026, the core/non-core challenge remains unresolved:

- Core funding continues to decline as a share of total revenue, despite repeated General Assembly and Executive Board calls for reversal
- The 2026–2029 Strategic Plan is being developed in this constrained environment, with explicit attention to revenue diversification
- Several innovative financing mechanisms are under exploration, including development impact bonds and structured private sector partnerships
- The humanitarian portfolio continues to grow as a share of UNFPA's work, reinforcing the non-core bias (humanitarian funding is almost entirely earmarked)
- The UN Funding Compact has been renewed but compliance remains weak
- Internal reforms (cost recovery, efficiency, shared services) continue but cannot substitute for adequate core funding
