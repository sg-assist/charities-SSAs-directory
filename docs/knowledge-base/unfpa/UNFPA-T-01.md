## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Digital health — the use of information and communication technologies for health — has become a significant component of UNFPA's programming toolkit. UNFPA deploys digital systems across its mandate: health management information systems (HMIS) for tracking maternal health and family planning services; digital logistics management for reproductive health commodity supply chains; mobile health (mHealth) platforms for SRHR education and counselling; electronic civil registration and vital statistics (eCRVS); and, increasingly, data analytics tools for population estimation and programme targeting.

The WHO published its first guideline on digital health interventions in 2019, classifying evidence-based digital interventions and recommending specific applications for client-side and provider-side use. UNFPA's digital health work aligns with this framework but is at varying stages of maturity across country offices. Some deployments — particularly DHIS2 (District Health Information Software 2), used in over 100 countries — are well-established and effective. Others — such as AI-assisted analysis, chatbot-based SRHR counselling, or predictive analytics for commodity demand — remain at the pilot stage with limited evidence of effectiveness at scale.

Key challenges include: the digital divide (most SRHR-underserved populations have the least digital access); data governance and privacy concerns (SRHR data is extremely sensitive — contraceptive use, GBV experiences, abortion); interoperability between fragmented digital systems; sustainability after pilot funding ends; and the risk of "digital solutionism" — deploying technology before the analogue foundations (trained health workers, functioning facilities, reliable supply chains) are in place.

## KEY FACTS

- **DHIS2 deployment**: Used in over 100 countries for health information management; UNFPA-supported SRHR modules track maternal health, family planning, and GBV data
- **WHO digital health guidelines (2019)**: Classified 10 digital health interventions with evidence assessments; recommended targeted client communication, decision support for health workers, and digital tracking of health status
- **Digital CRVS**: UNFPA supports electronic civil registration and vital statistics systems in approximately 30 countries — critical for accurate population data and maternal death registration
- **Mobile phone penetration in LMICs**: Approximately 75% of the population in LMICs has access to a mobile phone; smartphone penetration growing rapidly but still under 50% in many SSA countries
- **Internet access**: Approximately 2.6 billion people remain offline globally (ITU 2024); gender digital divide means women in LMICs are 16% less likely than men to use mobile internet
- **Digital health investment**: Global digital health investment exceeded USD 50 billion in 2021 (peak); dropped to approximately USD 15 billion by 2023; most investment in high-income markets, not LMIC SRHR
- **Data privacy concerns**: SRHR data (contraceptive use, STI status, GBV experience, abortion) is among the most sensitive health data; breaches can lead to stigma, violence, criminalisation
- **Interoperability**: Most digital health systems in LMICs are fragmented — different donors fund different platforms that don't communicate with each other
- **Pilot-to-scale gap**: WHO estimates that over 75% of digital health pilots in LMICs do not scale beyond the initial implementation phase
- **UNFPA Innovation Fund**: Supports digital health innovations including AI, data analytics, and mobile platforms for SRHR

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### The Digital Health Landscape

Digital health in the development context encompasses a wide spectrum:
- **Health Information Systems**: DHIS2, OpenMRS, and other platforms for health facility reporting and surveillance
- **mHealth**: SMS, apps, and voice-based health communication and service delivery
- **eCRVS**: Digital birth, death, and marriage registration
- **Telemedicine**: Remote clinical consultations via phone or video
- **Digital supply chain**: eLMIS, CHANNEL, and other logistics management systems for commodity tracking
- **Data analytics**: GIS mapping, machine learning, and predictive modelling for health planning
- **Client-facing tools**: Chatbots, apps, and websites for SRHR information and counselling

### UNFPA's Digital Strategy

UNFPA does not have a standalone digital health strategy but integrates digital tools within its programmatic areas. The organisation's approach has evolved:

**2010–2015**: Early adoption of DHIS2 for SRHR data; pilot mHealth projects in a few countries.

**2016–2020**: Expansion of digital CRVS support; commodity supply chain digitisation through CHANNEL; increased use of social media for SRHR communication and youth engagement.

**2020–2023**: COVID-19 accelerated digital adoption — telemedicine for ANC, digital GBV reporting, virtual training for health workers. Post-pandemic, some digital innovations were sustained while others reverted to pre-pandemic modalities.

**2024–2026**: Emerging focus on AI and machine learning applications; geospatial population data; digital identity for SRHR access; and enhanced data governance frameworks.

## DETAIL

### Established Digital Health Tools in UNFPA Programming

**DHIS2**: The most widely deployed health information system globally. UNFPA supports SRHR-specific modules within DHIS2, including maternal health dashboards, family planning service statistics, and GBV case management data. DHIS2's strength is its open-source architecture and government ownership — when implemented well, it becomes a sustainable national system rather than a donor-dependent project.

**CHANNEL**: UNFPA's contraceptive supply chain management system, used in approximately 50 countries. Tracks commodity shipments from UNFPA warehouses to country-level distribution points. Digitisation has improved visibility on commodity movements and stockout prediction.

**Digital CRVS**: UNFPA supports countries in transitioning from paper-based to electronic civil registration systems. Birth and death registration are foundational for population data, maternal death surveillance, and identity documentation. Examples include support to Bangladesh, Ethiopia, and Mozambique's CRVS modernisation.

### Emerging Applications

**AI for cervical cancer screening**: Automated visual inspection with AI-assisted analysis is being piloted in several countries (Bangladesh, Zambia) to enable screening by lower-level health workers. UNFPA supports some of these pilots, though the evidence base is still developing.

**Chatbots for SRHR information**: AI-powered chatbots providing family planning counselling, GBV referrals, and adolescent SRHR information have been piloted in several countries. Concerns include: accuracy of information, privacy of conversations, appropriateness for sensitive topics, and exclusion of populations without smartphone access.

**Geospatial population data**: UNFPA uses satellite imagery, machine learning, and geospatial models to estimate population distribution for census support, health facility planning, and humanitarian response. Partnerships with WorldPop, GRID3, and Meta's population density maps enable fine-grained population estimation where traditional census data is unavailable or outdated.

### Risks and Challenges

**Digital divide and equity**: The populations with the greatest SRHR needs — rural women in SSA, refugees, adolescent girls, persons with disabilities — often have the least digital access. Digital health tools can exacerbate rather than reduce inequality if deployed without attention to access.

**Data governance**: SRHR data requires the highest standards of privacy and security. In countries where contraceptive use, STI status, or abortion are stigmatised or criminalised, data breaches can have life-threatening consequences. UNFPA must ensure that digital systems meet data protection standards — but many LMICs lack adequate data protection legislation.

**Sustainability**: Digital health pilots funded by donors or innovation grants frequently fail to scale or sustain because: government ICT budgets are insufficient; technical maintenance capacity is limited; and the cost of ongoing server hosting, software updates, and user support exceeds available resources.

**Interoperability**: SRHR data sits across multiple systems — HMIS, supply chain, GBV case management, CRVS — that often do not communicate. A health worker may need to enter the same data into three different systems, wasting time and creating inconsistencies.

---

## CURRENT STATUS

- Digital health is a growing but not yet systematically integrated component of UNFPA programming
- COVID-19 accelerated adoption but sustainability post-pandemic varies
- AI applications are at early/pilot stages with limited evidence on SRHR-specific effectiveness
- Data governance and privacy frameworks need strengthening, particularly for GBV and abortion data
- The digital divide remains a fundamental equity challenge
- UNFPA's 2026–2029 Strategic Plan is expected to include stronger digital health commitments
