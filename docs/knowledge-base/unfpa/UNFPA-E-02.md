## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Sexual and reproductive health interventions are among the most cost-effective investments in global health. The evidence — consolidated through the Disease Control Priorities project (DCP3), the Copenhagen Consensus, and the Guttmacher/UNFPA "Adding It Up" analyses — consistently shows that investments in family planning, maternal health, and related SRHR services deliver high health and economic returns relative to cost. Every dollar invested in modern contraceptive services saves an estimated USD 2.20 in pregnancy-related care. Midwifery interventions can avert an estimated 67 per cent of maternal and newborn deaths if scaled to universal coverage. HPV vaccination is among the most cost-effective cancer prevention interventions available.

These investment cases are powerful advocacy tools, but they carry important limitations. Cost-effectiveness analysis (CEA) is inherently utilitarian — it measures health outcomes per dollar — and can conflict with the rights-based framework that underpins UNFPA's mandate. The most cost-effective intervention may not be the most equitable; the cheapest contraceptive method may not be the one women prefer. CEA also tends to undervalue the non-health benefits of SRHR interventions (education, economic participation, gender equality, demographic dividends) that are difficult to monetise.

UNFPA uses investment case evidence strategically — in donor advocacy, government resource mobilisation, and SDG financing discussions — while maintaining that SRHR is fundamentally a human right, not merely an efficient investment.

## KEY FACTS

- **Family planning ROI**: Every USD 1 invested in modern contraceptive services saves approximately USD 2.20 in pregnancy-related care (Guttmacher/UNFPA "Adding It Up" 2019)
- **Cost per DALY averted**: Family planning: approximately USD 50–100 per DALY averted in SSA (among the most cost-effective of all health interventions); maternal health packages: approximately USD 100–300 per DALY averted
- **DCP3 ranking**: SRHR interventions are classified as "best buys" and "good buys" in the Disease Control Priorities 3rd edition (2018) — essential surgery (including C-section) is a "best buy" at approximately USD 100 per DALY averted
- **Midwifery investment case**: SoWMy 2021 estimated that investing in midwifery to achieve universal coverage would avert 67% of maternal deaths, 64% of neonatal deaths, and 65% of stillbirths — approximately 4.3 million lives saved per year
- **HPV vaccination**: Cost-effectiveness varies by setting; in LMICs, estimated at USD 500–1,500 per DALY averted — cost-effective by WHO thresholds
- **Copenhagen Consensus**: Family planning ranked as one of the top development investments; every dollar spent yields approximately USD 120 in total benefits (health, education, economic) — one of the highest benefit-cost ratios of any development intervention
- **Adding It Up costs**: Meeting all women's needs for modern contraception would cost approximately USD 12.6 billion annually in developing countries; the full SRHR package approximately USD 68.8 billion
- **Current spending gap**: Actual SRHR spending in developing countries falls approximately USD 30 billion short of the full package cost
- **Non-health benefits**: Contraceptive access enables women's education, economic participation, and intergenerational wellbeing — benefits difficult to capture in standard CEA
- **Equity considerations**: The most cost-effective interventions may not reach the most marginalised; equity-weighted analyses show higher costs per outcome for the hardest-to-reach

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### The Investment Case Tradition

The practice of making economic arguments for SRHR investment has a long history. The 1994 ICPD itself included cost estimates for implementing its programme (USD 17 billion annually by 2000). Subsequently:

- **Adding It Up** (Guttmacher Institute, with UNFPA support): First published 2003, updated in 2008, 2014, 2017, and 2019. The landmark analytical series estimating the costs and benefits of investing in SRHR.
- **DCP3** (2018): Third edition of Disease Control Priorities, published as a series of volumes by the World Bank. Includes comprehensive cost-effectiveness data for SRHR interventions.
- **Copenhagen Consensus** (2012): Expert panel ranked the best development investments; family planning and maternal health ranked among the top 10 across all sectors.
- **Lancet commissions**: The Guttmacher-Lancet Commission (2018) and the Lancet Commission on Investing in Health (2013, updated 2017) both made strong investment cases for SRHR.

### Key Interventions and Their Cost-Effectiveness

**Family planning**: Modern contraception is one of the most cost-effective health interventions. It prevents unintended pregnancies, reducing maternal deaths (from unsafe abortion and pregnancy complications), neonatal deaths, and the downstream costs of unwanted births. The cost per couple-year of protection (CYP) varies by method: condoms approximately USD 1–3/CYP, pills approximately USD 3–6/CYP, injectables approximately USD 4–7/CYP, implants approximately USD 8–15/CYP, IUDs approximately USD 3–5/CYP. All are highly cost-effective by global health standards.

**Maternal and newborn care**: The essential package (ANC, skilled birth attendance, EmONC, postnatal care) costs approximately USD 900–1,500 per maternal death averted in SSA. C-section capacity (essential surgery) is classified as a "best buy" in DCP3 at approximately USD 100 per DALY averted.

**Midwifery**: The investment case is compelling — midwives, when trained, deployed, and supported, can deliver 87% of essential SRHR services. The SoWMy 2021 calculated that achieving universal midwifery coverage would save 4.3 million lives annually.

## DETAIL

### Strengths of the Investment Case

1. **Donor persuasion**: Finance ministries and development agencies respond to economic arguments. Demonstrating that SRHR investments deliver measurable, quantifiable returns makes the case for budget allocation.
2. **Comparison across sectors**: CEA allows comparison of SRHR interventions with investments in other sectors (education, infrastructure, agriculture), helping policymakers allocate scarce resources.
3. **Galvanising political commitment**: The Copenhagen Consensus finding that family planning has a benefit-cost ratio of 120:1 has been cited in countless advocacy documents and political statements.

### Limitations and Critiques

1. **Rights vs. efficiency**: SRHR is a human right. Making the case purely on cost-effectiveness grounds risks implying that SRHR would not be warranted if it were expensive. UNFPA's position is that rights are non-negotiable regardless of cost-effectiveness.
2. **Equity blind spots**: Standard CEA favours interventions that are cheap per unit of outcome — which often means reaching the easiest-to-reach populations. Reaching the most marginalised (last mile, conflict-affected, disabilities, indigenous) costs more per outcome but is the ethical imperative.
3. **Non-health benefits undervalued**: The full benefits of SRHR (women's empowerment, education access, economic growth, demographic dividend, social cohesion) are difficult to quantify and are underrepresented in standard CEA.
4. **Methodological debates**: DALYs as a metric are contested (they embed value judgements about disability and age); discount rates affect long-term benefit calculations; cost data from LMICs is often unreliable.
5. **Implementation costs ignored**: CEA typically estimates the cost of an intervention at scale under ideal conditions; it does not account for the implementation costs (training, supervision, logistics, governance reform) required to achieve scale.

## CURRENT STATUS

- The investment case for SRHR is well-established and used extensively in advocacy
- A new edition of "Adding It Up" is anticipated to update cost and benefit estimates
- DCP3 findings continue to inform WHO essential packages and national health planning
- UNFPA uses investment case evidence alongside rights-based arguments — not as a substitute
- Growing interest in equity-weighted CEA and wider societal benefit measurement
