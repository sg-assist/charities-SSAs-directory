## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Asia is the most rapidly urbanising continent, with megacities (populations exceeding 10 million) expanding in scale and complexity. Cities like Dhaka, Mumbai, Jakarta, Manila, Karachi, and Ho Chi Minh City face compounding challenges: population density, informal settlement growth, climate vulnerability (flooding, heat, sea-level rise), and health systems strained by scale. SRHR in urban settings presents a paradox: health facilities are geographically closer than in rural areas, yet urban poor women — particularly those in slums and informal settlements — may have worse SRHR outcomes than rural women due to cost barriers, social exclusion, and service fragmentation.

UNFPA's urban SRHR work in Asia addresses: maternal health in informal settlements (where home births without skilled attendance persist despite proximity to hospitals), family planning access for urban poor and migrant populations, GBV in dense urban environments, adolescent SRHR in rapidly growing youth populations, and the climate-health intersection in flood-prone and heat-affected urban areas.

This document is directly relevant to LKYSPP Challenge B, which examines resilience partnerships in Asia. Urban resilience for SRHR requires: health system strengthening at the primary care level in underserved urban areas, community health worker deployment in informal settlements, climate-resilient health infrastructure, and partnerships with municipal governments and the private sector.

## KEY FACTS

- **Asian urbanisation**: Over 55% of Asia's population is urban (2023); projected to reach 65% by 2050
- **Asian megacities**: Over 20 megacities (10M+ population) including Tokyo, Delhi, Shanghai, Mumbai, Beijing, Dhaka, Karachi, Osaka, Manila, Jakarta
- **Urban slum population (Asia)**: Approximately 370 million people live in slum conditions (UN-Habitat 2022)
- **Urban health paradox**: Urban averages mask extreme inequality; women in the poorest urban quintile often have worse SRHR indicators than rural women
- **Dhaka**: Population density exceeds 50,000/km² in some areas; flooding affects reproductive health facilities annually; maternal mortality in slums estimated 2–3x the city average
- **Mumbai**: Informal settlements house approximately 50% of the population; SRHR services in public facilities are overcrowded; private sector fills gaps at high cost
- **Jakarta**: Subsidence and flooding threaten health infrastructure; UNFPA supports climate-resilient SRHR services
- **Climate vulnerability**: Asian coastal megacities (Dhaka, Mumbai, Manila, Jakarta, Ho Chi Minh City) face acute climate risks that affect health infrastructure and populations
- **Urban GBV**: Dense living conditions, poverty stress, and substance abuse contribute to high GBV rates in urban informal settlements

## DETAIL

### The Urban SRHR Challenge in Asia

Urban SRHR challenges differ from rural ones in important ways:

**Access vs. affordability**: Urban women may be near health facilities but unable to afford services. Public facilities are overcrowded; private facilities are expensive. This creates a "coverage gap" that national statistics (which show high urban CPR and SBA) may miss.

**Informal settlement dynamics**: Slum and informal settlement residents are often unregistered, mobile, and excluded from government health programmes. Community health worker programmes designed for stable rural communities may not function in transient urban populations.

**Adolescent SRHR in cities**: Urban youth face specific SRHR risks — early sexual debut, commercial sex work, substance abuse, and limited access to youth-friendly services — alongside opportunities (proximity to information and services, greater social openness).

**Climate and health infrastructure**: Flooding in cities like Dhaka, Jakarta, and Mumbai regularly damages health facilities, destroys commodity stocks, and makes roads impassable for emergency obstetric referral. Climate-resilient health infrastructure (elevated buildings, flood-proof storage, backup power) is essential.

### Relevance to LKYSPP Challenge B

The Challenge asks how innovative PPPs can strengthen community resilience in Asia. Urban SRHR resilience requires:
- Municipal government engagement (urban health is city-managed, not just national)
- Private sector health service partnerships (private facilities serve urban populations but need quality regulation and affordability mechanisms)
- Climate-resilient infrastructure investment
- Community-based approaches adapted for urban informal settlements
- Digital health solutions leveraging urban connectivity
