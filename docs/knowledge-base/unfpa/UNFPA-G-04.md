## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Latin America and the Caribbean (LAC) is a region of profound contrasts for SRHR. It includes upper-middle-income countries with functioning health systems, relatively low maternal mortality, and high contraceptive prevalence (Brazil, Chile, Costa Rica, Uruguay), alongside countries with severe SRHR deficits (Haiti, Guatemala, Honduras) and populations facing extreme inequality in access based on ethnicity, income, geography, and age. LAC has the second-highest adolescent birth rate of any world region (approximately 53/1,000 for ages 15–19), and extreme inequality means that SRHR outcomes for indigenous, Afro-descendant, and rural populations are dramatically worse than national averages suggest.

UNFPA's Latin America and Caribbean Regional Office (LACRO), based in Panama City, oversees programming in approximately 24 countries. The region's middle-income status creates a specific funding challenge: many LAC countries are "graduating" from ODA eligibility, meaning UNFPA's core resource allocations are declining even as significant pockets of SRHR need persist. UNFPA has responded by shifting toward policy advocacy, technical assistance, and knowledge generation rather than direct service delivery.

Key regional issues include: persistently high adolescent pregnancy (often linked to sexual violence), abortion law reform movements and counter-movements, the Venezuela humanitarian crisis and its displacement effects, indigenous and Afro-descendant health disparities, comprehensive sexuality education (with significant progress in some countries and fierce opposition in others), and the Zika epidemic's impact on reproductive rights discourse.

## KEY FACTS

- **Regional population**: Approximately 660 million
- **Adolescent birth rate (LAC)**: Approximately 53/1,000 girls aged 15–19 — second highest globally after SSA; approximately 1.5 million adolescent births annually
- **MMR (LAC, 2020)**: Regional average approximately 88/100,000; ranges from 22/100,000 (Chile) to 480/100,000 (Haiti)
- **Modern CPR**: Regional average approximately 70%; highest in Brazil (77%), lowest in Haiti (32%) and Guatemala (44%)
- **Abortion legal landscape**: Highly variable — fully legal in Argentina (2020), Colombia (2022), Uruguay, Cuba, Mexico City and most Mexican states; completely prohibited in Honduras, El Salvador, Nicaragua, Dominican Republic
- **Venezuela displacement**: Over 7 million Venezuelans displaced since 2015; major SRHR needs among displaced women and girls in Colombia, Peru, Ecuador, Brazil, and Chile
- **Indigenous population**: Approximately 50 million indigenous peoples in LAC; systematically worse SRHR outcomes (higher MMR, lower CPR, higher adolescent pregnancy)
- **Afro-descendant population**: Approximately 130 million; significant SRHR disparities, particularly in Brazil, Colombia, and Central America
- **Femicide**: LAC has extremely high femicide rates; 14 of the 25 countries with the highest femicide rates globally are in LAC
- **CSE**: Significant progress in some countries (Colombia, Argentina, Uruguay) and fierce opposition from conservative and religious groups ("con mis hijos no te metas" movement) in others (Peru, Brazil, Paraguay)
- **Zika (2015–2016)**: The epidemic exposed the intersection of infectious disease, reproductive rights, and poverty — women in affected areas were told to delay pregnancy but lacked contraceptive access and faced restrictive abortion laws

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### The Middle-Income Challenge

Most LAC countries are classified as middle-income by the World Bank, which affects their eligibility for ODA and UNFPA core resource allocations. Several countries have "graduated" from UNFPA programme support or transitioned to primarily upstream technical assistance and policy advisory roles.

This creates a paradox: national-level indicators may be acceptable, but sub-national disparities are extreme. In Brazil, the MMR for Black women is approximately 2–3 times that of white women. In Guatemala, indigenous women's access to skilled birth attendance is 40 percentage points below the national average. These within-country inequalities persist precisely because they affect marginalised populations with limited political voice.

UNFPA's LAC programming has shifted accordingly — from large-scale service delivery to:
- Policy advocacy (CSE policy reform, abortion law reform, adolescent pregnancy prevention strategies)
- Data generation and analysis (disaggregated data on inequality)
- Technical assistance to government programmes
- Support for civil society organisations

### Historical Context

LAC was one of the earliest regions for family planning programmes in the 1960s and 1970s, with significant investment from USAID, IPPF, and UNFPA. The region achieved substantial fertility declines — from an average TFR of approximately 5.9 in 1960 to approximately 1.9 in 2023.

However, the history includes coercive practices: Peru's forced sterilisation programme under President Fujimori (1996–2000), which primarily targeted indigenous women, remains a profound historical stain. Brazil's high rate of female sterilisation (the dominant contraceptive method for decades) also raised questions about informed consent and method choice.

## DETAIL

### Adolescent Pregnancy

LAC's adolescent birth rate is stubbornly high and has proven resistant to the fertility decline that reduced overall TFR. Key factors include:

**Sexual violence**: A significant proportion of adolescent pregnancies in LAC result from sexual violence, including incest. In countries like Guatemala, Nicaragua, and El Salvador, studies have found that 30–40% of pregnancies among girls under 15 involve sexual violence.

**Inequality**: Adolescent pregnancy in LAC is overwhelmingly concentrated among poor, rural, indigenous, and Afro-descendant girls. It is both a cause and consequence of poverty, creating intergenerational cycles.

**Limited CSE and service access**: Conservative opposition to CSE and adolescent-friendly SRHR services restricts access in many countries. Pharmacies in some countries refuse to sell emergency contraception to adolescents; health facilities require parental consent for contraceptive services.

UNFPA's regional adolescent pregnancy prevention strategy focuses on CSE, adolescent-friendly health services, GBV prevention, and keeping girls in school. Several countries (Argentina, Colombia, Uruguay, El Salvador) have developed national adolescent pregnancy prevention plans with UNFPA technical support.

### Abortion Law Reform

LAC has seen the most dynamic abortion law changes of any region in recent years:
- **Argentina (2020)**: Legalised abortion up to 14 weeks after decades of feminist mobilisation ("marea verde" / green wave)
- **Colombia (2022)**: Constitutional Court decriminalised abortion up to 24 weeks
- **Mexico**: Supreme Court decriminalised abortion federally (2023); most states have reformed laws
- **Chile**: Legalised abortion on three grounds (2017) after 28 years of total prohibition under the Pinochet-era law

UNFPA's role in these reforms is supportive but careful: providing technical evidence on public health impacts of restrictive abortion laws, supporting post-abortion care training, and advocating for safe motherhood — without taking explicit positions on national legislation in countries where abortion is politically contested.

### The Venezuela Displacement Crisis

The Venezuelan displacement crisis — the largest in Latin American history, with over 7 million Venezuelans leaving since 2015 — has created significant SRHR needs. Displaced Venezuelan women and girls face:
- Limited access to contraception and maternal health care in host countries
- High rates of GBV (exploitation, trafficking, survival sex)
- Irregular migration status limiting health system access
- Disrupted HIV treatment and STI management

UNFPA has established SRHR service points for Venezuelan migrants and refugees in Colombia, Peru, Ecuador, Brazil, and Chile, often in partnership with government health systems and civil society organisations.

## CURRENT STATUS

- Adolescent pregnancy remains the region's most persistent SRHR challenge, with limited progress despite decades of programming
- Abortion law reform continues to advance in some countries while facing backlash in others
- The Venezuelan displacement crisis remains a major humanitarian and SRHR challenge across the region
- CSE implementation is uneven — progress in some countries is countered by conservative opposition movements in others
- UNFPA's LAC portfolio is increasingly focused on upstream policy work, inequality analysis, and knowledge generation as countries graduate from traditional programme support
- Indigenous and Afro-descendant SRHR inequalities are receiving increased attention through disaggregated data and targeted programmes
- Femicide and GBV prevention have become high-profile political issues in several countries, creating opportunities for UNFPA engagement
