## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The 2018 UN development system reform — initiated by General Assembly resolution 72/279 and driven by Secretary-General António Guterres — represents the most significant restructuring of the UN's development operations in decades. The reform repositioned the UN Resident Coordinator (RC) system as an independent coordination mechanism, delinked from UNDP and funded through a hybrid model. For UNFPA, this reform has both positive and negative implications: it creates a more coherent country-level UN presence that can amplify SRHR advocacy, but it also risks diluting UNFPA's distinct mandate within broader "One UN" frameworks and imposes additional coordination costs on an already resource-constrained organisation.

The reform's core premise is that the UN development system must shift from a collection of competing agencies delivering fragmented projects to a coherent system that supports countries in achieving the Sustainable Development Goals. The Resident Coordinator, now reporting to the Secretary-General rather than to the UNDP Administrator, is responsible for coordinating all UN development activities at country level. UN Country Teams (UNCTs) — comprising all resident and non-resident agencies — are expected to develop joint UN Sustainable Development Cooperation Frameworks (UNSDCFs) that align agency-specific programming with national priorities.

For UNFPA, the practical consequences include: participation in joint programming and pooled funding mechanisms; alignment of country programme documents with the UNSDCF; coordination with the RC on advocacy and government engagement; and the challenge of maintaining UNFPA's distinct identity and mandate within increasingly integrated UN teams.

## KEY FACTS

- **GA resolution 72/279 (2018)**: The foundational reform resolution, adopted by consensus, repositioning the UN development system
- **Resident Coordinator delinking**: RCs were separated from UNDP in January 2019; previously, the UNDP Resident Representative served as RC in most countries
- **RC funding**: A hybrid model — 1% coordination levy on earmarked contributions to UN development entities, voluntary contributions, and cost-sharing arrangements; total RC system budget approximately USD 281 million (2023)
- **UN Country Teams**: Approximately 130 UNCTs worldwide, comprising all resident and non-resident UN development entities
- **UNSDCFs**: Joint frameworks replacing the former UNDAFs; intended to be the primary instrument for country-level UN planning
- **UNFPA country offices**: Approximately 150 country/territory offices, with varying degrees of integration into UNCT structures
- **Multi-Country Offices (MCOs)**: UNFPA operates several MCOs covering multiple small countries (e.g., Pacific, Caribbean), a model encouraged by the reform for efficiency
- **Management and Accountability Framework (MAF)**: Defines the relationship between the RC, agency heads, and UNCTs — including the "dual reporting" of agency country representatives to both their agency head and the RC
- **Efficiency gains**: The Business Operations Strategy (BOS) 2.0 aims for USD 310 million in annual system-wide savings through shared premises, procurement, and services; actual savings are disputed
- **Joint programmes**: UN entities are encouraged to design and deliver jointly; UNFPA participates in joint programmes on GBV, adolescent health, HIV, and other areas

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### The Pre-Reform Landscape

Before 2018, the UN development system at country level was often described as fragmented, competitive, and duplicative. Each agency maintained its own office, staff, programmatic strategy, and reporting relationships. The Resident Coordinator — who was simultaneously the UNDP Resident Representative — had limited authority over other agencies and was perceived as biased toward UNDP's interests.

UNFPA operated relatively independently at country level. The UNFPA Representative reported to the UNFPA Regional Director and through them to the Executive Director in New York. Coordination with other UN agencies occurred through the UNCT, but programming was largely agency-specific. This independence had advantages: UNFPA could pursue its mandate without dilution, maintain direct relationships with government counterparts (typically the Ministry of Health and the National Statistical Office), and preserve the confidentiality required for sensitive SRHR programming.

The disadvantages were equally clear: duplication of administrative functions across agencies, inconsistent UN messaging to governments, competition for donor funding at country level, and an inability to present a coherent "UN offer" to national partners.

### The Guterres Reform Agenda

Secretary-General Guterres, upon taking office in 2017, made UN development system reform a signature initiative. His 2017 report "Repositioning the UN development system to deliver on the 2030 Agenda" proposed:

1. An empowered, independent Resident Coordinator
2. A new generation of UN Country Teams configured to country needs
3. Joint UN Sustainable Development Cooperation Frameworks replacing UNDAFs
4. Shared back-office functions and common premises
5. A system-wide strategic document and updated QCPR implementation

The General Assembly adopted resolution 72/279 in May 2018 by consensus, though with varying degrees of enthusiasm. Programme countries generally supported a more coherent UN system; some agencies (including, reportedly, UNDP) had reservations about losing the RC function.

## DETAIL

### Impact on UNFPA Operations

**Country Programme Alignment**: UNFPA's Country Programme Documents (CPDs) must now be explicitly aligned with the UNSDCF. This means UNFPA's four-year programming cycle and thematic priorities must fit within the broader UN framework negotiated with the host government. In practice, this has been manageable — SRHR, maternal health, GBV, and population data are sufficiently recognised priorities that they appear in most UNSDCFs. However, there are cases where government preferences (e.g., for infrastructure or economic development programming) push SRHR to the margins of the UNSDCF, and UNFPA must negotiate for adequate space.

**Joint Programming**: The reform encourages joint programmes where multiple agencies contribute complementary expertise. UNFPA participates in several joint programme models:
- **UNFPA-UNICEF Joint Programme on FGM**: One of the longest-running joint programmes in the UN system
- **UNFPA-UNICEF Global Programme to End Child Marriage**: Joint design and delivery across 12 target countries
- **H6 Partnership** (now the SDG3 GAP signatories): UNFPA, WHO, UNICEF, World Bank, UNAIDS, and UN Women coordinate on maternal, newborn, child, and adolescent health
- **Spotlight Initiative**: EU-funded, UN-implemented programme on GBV involving UNFPA, UN Women, UNDP, and UNICEF
- **Joint HIV programmes**: UNFPA leads on prevention among key populations as part of the UNAIDS Division of Labour

Joint programming can enhance impact but imposes coordination costs. Agencies must negotiate roles, manage joint budgets, agree on reporting frameworks, and navigate different institutional cultures and accountability requirements.

**Resident Coordinator Relationship**: Under the Management and Accountability Framework, UNFPA Representatives at country level have a "dual key" relationship — they report to the UNFPA Regional Director on programmatic and administrative matters, and to the RC on coordination and joint activities. In practice, the quality of this relationship depends heavily on individual personalities. Strong RCs can amplify UNFPA's advocacy (e.g., on maternal health or GBV in policy discussions with government). Weak or uninterested RCs may marginalise SRHR in the UNCT agenda.

**Common Premises and Shared Services**: The reform promotes common premises (UN Houses) and shared back-office functions (procurement, ICT, fleet management, human resources). UNFPA has participated in Business Operations Strategies at country level, achieving some cost savings. However, shared services can also mean slower procurement, reduced institutional flexibility, and one-size-fits-all solutions that do not suit UNFPA's specific operational needs (e.g., confidential GBV programming spaces, or the specialised logistics of reproductive health commodity management).

### UNFPA's Comparative Advantage in the Reformed System

The reform asks each agency to articulate its "comparative advantage" — what it does that no other UN entity does as well. UNFPA's comparative advantages include:

1. **SRHR technical expertise**: No other UN entity has UNFPA's depth of expertise in family planning, midwifery, obstetric care, and reproductive health commodity management
2. **Population data**: UNFPA is the lead UN entity for census support and civil registration, a function no other agency replicates
3. **Youth and adolescent SRHR**: While UNICEF covers child health, UNFPA's mandate specifically covers adolescent sexual and reproductive health
4. **GBV in humanitarian settings**: UNFPA leads the GBV sub-cluster under the IASC cluster system, a recognised coordination role
5. **Harmful practices**: The joint programmes on FGM and child marriage position UNFPA as the lead agency on these issues

These comparative advantages are well-recognised within the UN system, which protects UNFPA's position in UNCTs. The risk is less about UNFPA losing its mandate and more about UNFPA's mandate being diluted within broader "health" or "gender" programming where its specific technical contributions are subsumed.

## CURRENT STATUS

- The UN development system reform is now in its seventh year of implementation, with mixed reviews from system-wide evaluations
- The 2024 QCPR (GA resolution 77/283) reaffirmed the reform direction while acknowledging implementation gaps, particularly in RC system funding and coherence
- UNFPA has largely adapted to the reformed system, though country offices report varying experiences depending on RC quality, government engagement, and UNCT composition
- The efficiency agenda (common premises, shared services) has produced some savings but has not fundamentally altered UNFPA's cost structure
- The RC funding model remains contested — the 1% coordination levy is resented by some agencies, and voluntary contributions to the RC system have fallen short of targets
- UNFPA's incoming Strategic Plan (2026–2029) explicitly positions the organisation within the reformed UN system, emphasising partnership and coherence while protecting mandate-specific work
