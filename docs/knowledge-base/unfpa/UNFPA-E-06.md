## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

"Adding It Up" is a flagship analytical series produced by the Guttmacher Institute with UNFPA support, providing the most detailed and widely-cited estimates of the costs and benefits of investing in sexual and reproductive health in developing countries. Published in multiple editions (2003, 2008, 2014, 2017, 2019), the series quantifies: how many women need SRHR services, what it would cost to provide them, and what the health, demographic, and economic returns would be.

The 2019 edition — the most recent at the time of writing — estimated that fully meeting all women's needs for modern contraception in developing countries would cost USD 12.6 billion annually and would result in: 76,000 fewer maternal deaths, 1.1 million fewer newborn deaths, 25 million fewer unsafe abortions, and approximately 600,000 fewer unplanned pregnancies annually. The full SRHR package (adding maternal and newborn care, STI services, and other components) would cost approximately USD 68.8 billion — compared to current spending of approximately USD 39 billion, leaving a gap of approximately USD 30 billion.

"Adding It Up" has been UNFPA's most powerful resource mobilisation tool. Its headline findings have been cited in countless advocacy documents, donor consultations, Executive Board presentations, and intergovernmental negotiations. The series demonstrates that SRHR investment is not merely ethical but also economically rational — every dollar invested in contraception saves USD 2.20 in pregnancy-related care, and the broader social returns (women's education, economic participation, demographic dividend) multiply the economic case further.

- **Series history**: First published 2003; updated 2008, 2014, 2017, 2019; new edition anticipated
- **Authors**: Guttmacher Institute researchers (Jacqueline Darroch, Ann Biddlecom, Susheela Singh, and others); UNFPA as partner and funder
- **2019 headline findings**:
  - 218 million women in developing countries have unmet need for modern contraception
  - Fully meeting this need would cost USD 12.6 billion annually
  - Would prevent: 76,000 maternal deaths, 1.1 million newborn deaths, 25 million unsafe abortions
  - Every USD 1 invested saves USD 2.20 in pregnancy-related care
- **Full SRHR package cost**: USD 68.8 billion annually in developing countries
- **Current spending**: Approximately USD 39 billion annually — leaving a USD 30 billion gap
- **Methodology**: Modelling based on: national survey data on FP use/demand, WHO cost data for SRHR services, demographic projections, and health outcome models
- **Geographic coverage**: 132 low- and middle-income countries
- **Use in advocacy**: Cited in UNFPA Executive Board documents, Nairobi Summit materials, SDG financing discussions, and bilateral donor consultations
- **Limitations**: Modelled estimates with significant uncertainty; cost assumptions may not hold in fragile states; does not capture full social returns

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### Why "Adding It Up" Matters

The series fills a critical evidence gap: before "Adding It Up," there was no comprehensive, regularly updated estimate of the global cost of SRHR investment and its returns. This made it difficult for UNFPA and national governments to make the financial case for SRHR budgets. "Adding It Up" provides the numbers that enable evidence-based advocacy.

The methodology has improved across editions:
- **2003**: First comprehensive global estimate of contraceptive service costs
- **2008**: Added maternal and newborn care costs
- **2014**: Expanded to include STI treatment and incorporated improved demographic models
- **2017**: Interim update with revised cost data
- **2019**: Most comprehensive edition; incorporated updated survey data, revised cost estimates, and expanded the scope of the SRHR package to align more closely with the Guttmacher-Lancet Commission's essential package

### How the Estimates Are Produced

"Adding It Up" uses a multi-step modelling approach:

1. **Demand estimation**: Using DHS, MICS, and other survey data, estimate the number of women who want to use modern contraception but are not (unmet need), the number who need maternal care, STI treatment, etc.
2. **Cost estimation**: Using WHO-CHOICE and country-specific cost data, estimate the per-capita cost of providing each service at the required quality
3. **Scaling**: Multiply demand by unit cost to get total cost for universal coverage
4. **Health impact modelling**: Using established epidemiological models, estimate the health outcomes that would result from universal coverage (deaths averted, morbidities prevented, pregnancies prevented)
5. **Economic returns**: Calculate the economic savings from averted health costs and, where possible, the broader economic benefits of improved SRHR

## DETAIL

### Key Findings in Detail

**Unmet need**: An estimated 218 million women in developing countries want to avoid pregnancy but are not using a modern contraceptive method (2019 estimate). This includes women using no method and women using traditional methods. The highest absolute numbers are in South Asia and sub-Saharan Africa.

**Cost of contraceptive services**: The USD 12.6 billion annual cost includes: contraceptive commodities, health worker time, facility costs, supply chain, programme management, and demand creation. The cost per woman served varies by method and setting — from approximately USD 5/year for condoms to approximately USD 30/year for implant insertion and management.

**Maternal and newborn care**: The largest component of the full SRHR package, costing approximately USD 33.4 billion annually. Includes ANC, skilled birth attendance, EmONC, postnatal care, and newborn care. This reflects the high cost of facility-based care, particularly surgical capacity (C-section).

**Health impact**: Fully meeting need for modern contraception alone would result in: 76,000 fewer maternal deaths (21% reduction), 1.1 million fewer newborn deaths, 25 million fewer unsafe abortions, and 12 million fewer unintended births. Adding full maternal and newborn care would save additional lives.

**The savings ratio**: The USD 2.20 savings per dollar invested is calculated from: averted costs of managing complications of unintended pregnancy (unsafe abortion treatment, delivery care for unplanned births, neonatal care for premature infants). This is a conservative estimate that does not include the broader economic benefits of women's educational attainment, workforce participation, and reduced child dependency ratios.

### Limitations

**Modelling uncertainty**: All estimates are modelled, not observed. They depend on assumptions about costs, service quality, population behaviour, and health system capacity that introduce significant uncertainty. The confidence intervals around headline numbers are substantial but rarely reported in advocacy materials.

**Fragile state applicability**: Unit cost assumptions may not hold in conflict-affected or extremely low-capacity settings where the marginal cost of reaching each additional woman is much higher.

**Non-health benefits**: "Adding It Up" focuses primarily on health outcomes and direct cost savings. The broader social and economic benefits of SRHR — women's empowerment, educational attainment, economic productivity, demographic dividend — are acknowledged but not fully quantified. This means the true return on SRHR investment is likely much higher than the reported figures.

**Static analysis**: The estimates represent a snapshot in time; they do not model the dynamic effects of SRHR investment over decades (e.g., how reduced fertility today affects the workforce in 20 years).

## CURRENT STATUS

- The 2019 edition remains the current reference; an updated edition is anticipated
- "Adding It Up" estimates are extensively used in UNFPA advocacy, including the Nairobi Summit and SDG financing discussions
- The series has been adapted for regional and country-level advocacy (country-specific fact sheets)
