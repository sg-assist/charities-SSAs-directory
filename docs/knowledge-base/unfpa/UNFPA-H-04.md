## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Universal Health Coverage (UHC) — defined by WHO as ensuring that all people have access to needed health services of sufficient quality without financial hardship — has become a central organising concept in global health. For UNFPA, the inclusion of SRHR services in UHC essential benefits packages is both an opportunity and a political challenge. When countries design UHC systems that include contraception, maternal care, STI treatment, GBV response, and adolescent SRHR, the coverage and sustainability of these services improve dramatically. When SRHR is excluded or marginalised in UHC design — as happens in countries where political or religious opposition targets specific services — the UHC framework can actually set back reproductive rights by creating a "universal" system that universally excludes contested services.

SDG target 3.8 calls for achieving UHC, including "access to quality essential health-care services and access to safe, effective, quality and affordable essential medicines and vaccines for all." The 2019 UN High-Level Meeting on UHC produced a Political Declaration reaffirming the inclusion of SRHR in UHC, though with contested language. UNFPA, WHO, and the World Bank jointly advocate for SRHR as a core component of UHC benefits packages, and the Guttmacher-Lancet Commission's essential SRHR package provides the most detailed specification of what comprehensive SRHR-inclusive UHC should contain.

The practical challenge is that UHC design is inherently political. What goes into the essential benefits package is determined by national governments, often under pressure from religious institutions, conservative political movements, and fiscal constraints. Contraception for adolescents, comprehensive abortion care, and SRHR for marginalised populations are frequently the services most at risk of exclusion.

## KEY FACTS

- **UHC definition (WHO)**: All people receive the health services they need without financial hardship, covering the full spectrum from promotion to prevention, treatment, rehabilitation, and palliative care
- **SDG 3.8**: "Achieve universal health coverage, including financial risk protection, access to quality essential health-care services and access to safe, effective, quality and affordable essential medicines and vaccines for all"
- **UHC coverage index (WHO/World Bank)**: Global average approximately 68 out of 100 (2021); ranges from over 80 in high-income countries to under 40 in many LDCs
- **Out-of-pocket SRHR spending**: In many LMICs, 30–50% of SRHR costs are paid out of pocket — a major financial barrier, particularly for contraception and maternal care
- **Guttmacher-Lancet essential SRHR package**: Defines 9 essential SRHR service categories that should be included in UHC: contraception, maternal/newborn care, abortion care, post-abortion care, STI treatment, GBV response, infertility counselling, CSE, and cervical cancer screening
- **Cost estimate**: Full SRHR package costs approximately USD 25 per person per year in LMICs (Guttmacher 2019); family planning alone approximately USD 8.56 per person per year
- **Countries with SRHR in UHC benefits packages**: Varies enormously; some include full contraceptive coverage (Thailand, Rwanda); others exclude specific services (contraception for unmarried women, abortion care)
- **2019 UHC Political Declaration**: Reaffirmed SRHR as part of UHC, but language was contested — references to "reproductive health" and "sexual and reproductive health" were negotiated against opposition from conservative states
- **UHC2030**: Multi-stakeholder platform for UHC advocacy; UNFPA is a member alongside WHO, World Bank, and civil society

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### The Rise of UHC

UHC has roots in the 1978 Alma-Ata Declaration on Primary Health Care but gained contemporary momentum in the 2010s as the successor concept to the "health MDGs." Key milestones:
- **2012 UN General Assembly resolution 67/81**: Called for UHC as a priority for sustainable development
- **2015 SDGs**: Target 3.8 established UHC as a specific goal
- **2019 UN High-Level Meeting on UHC**: Produced a Political Declaration with commitments on UHC acceleration
- **2023 HLM on UHC**: Second high-level meeting reviewed progress and set 2030 targets

The UHC concept is attractive because it provides a framework for health system strengthening that goes beyond individual diseases or services. Rather than vertical programmes for HIV, TB, or maternal health operating in parallel, UHC promises integrated, people-centred health systems that address the full range of needs.

### Why SRHR-UHC Integration Matters

**Sustainability**: When SRHR services are integrated into national health systems financed through UHC mechanisms (social health insurance, tax-funded services, pooled financing), they are more sustainable than when funded through external donor projects that may end. UNFPA has long advocated for SRHR integration into health systems rather than parallel service delivery.

**Financial protection**: Out-of-pocket spending on SRHR — particularly maternal care and contraception — can be catastrophic for poor households. UHC financial protection mechanisms (insurance, fee waivers, subsidies) can remove this barrier.

**Coverage**: UHC frameworks typically emphasise primary health care, where most SRHR services are delivered. Strengthening PHC through UHC investment simultaneously strengthens SRHR service capacity.

**Equity**: UHC's "leave no one behind" principle aligns with SRHR goals of reaching marginalised populations — adolescents, rural women, persons with disabilities, migrants.

## DETAIL

### The Political Contestation

SRHR is the most politically contested component of UHC benefits package design. The tensions are well-documented:

**Contraception for adolescents**: Many countries restrict adolescent access to contraception through age-of-consent laws, parental consent requirements, or provider refusal. Including adolescent contraception in UHC packages is politically difficult in conservative contexts.

**Abortion care**: The Guttmacher-Lancet Commission includes "safe abortion care, to the full extent of the law" in the essential SRHR package. Countries with restrictive abortion laws either exclude abortion entirely from UHC packages or include only post-abortion care (treatment of complications). This means UHC coverage is incomplete precisely where unsafe abortion risk is highest.

**STI services for key populations**: HIV and STI services for sex workers, men who have sex with men, and people who inject drugs may be excluded from UHC packages in countries that criminalise these populations.

**GBV response**: Clinical management of rape and GBV response services are often underfunded even in countries with otherwise functional UHC systems, because they fall between health and justice sector responsibilities.

### Country Examples

**Thailand**: Often cited as a UHC success story. Thailand's Universal Coverage Scheme (2001) includes comprehensive SRHR services: full contraceptive coverage, maternal care, STI treatment, and cervical cancer screening. This has contributed to Thailand's strong SRHR indicators (MMR approximately 29/100,000, modern CPR approximately 73%).

**Rwanda**: Community-based health insurance (Mutuelles de santé) covers approximately 80% of the population and includes maternal health services, family planning, and basic SRHR. Rwanda's dramatic reduction in MMR is partly attributed to UHC expansion.

**Indonesia**: National Health Insurance (JKN) covers over 90% of the population but contraceptive coverage has been inconsistent, with stockout and quality issues affecting family planning within the UHC framework.

**Kenya**: The 2023 UHC pilot expanded coverage in several counties, including SRHR services, but financing sustainability and service quality remain challenges.

### UNFPA's Role in UHC

UNFPA advocates for SRHR inclusion in UHC through:
- Technical assistance to governments designing essential benefits packages
- Policy advocacy at global (UHC2030, WHA) and national levels
- Evidence generation on the cost-effectiveness of SRHR investment
- Support for training health workers to deliver integrated SRHR-PHC services
- Monitoring SRHR indicators within UHC coverage assessments

---

## CURRENT STATUS

- UHC is advancing unevenly — approximately 4.5 billion people still lack access to essential health services
- SRHR inclusion in UHC remains politically contested in many countries
- The 2023 UHC HLM reaffirmed commitments but financing gaps are enormous
- UNFPA continues to advocate for comprehensive SRHR in UHC essential benefits packages
- The climate-health nexus is adding new dimensions to UHC design, with SRHR services among those most disrupted by climate-related health system shocks
