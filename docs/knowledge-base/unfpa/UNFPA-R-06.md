## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The COVID-19 pandemic (2020–2023) exposed the fragility of SRHR service delivery during health emergencies. Across LMICs, ANC visits declined by 20–40 per cent during peak lockdown periods, contraceptive supply chains were disrupted, facility deliveries dropped in many countries as women feared infection, GBV increased during home confinement, and health workers were redeployed from SRHR to COVID response. UNFPA estimated that COVID-19 disruptions could result in an additional 7 million unintended pregnancies and 31 million additional cases of GBV over a 6-month period.

The pandemic experience demonstrated that SRHR must be explicitly integrated into pandemic preparedness and response frameworks — not treated as an afterthought. UNFPA's response included: adapting the MISP for pandemic contexts, accelerating self-care interventions (self-injectable contraceptives, telemedicine), scaling GBV hotlines, advocating for SRHR as essential services during lockdowns, and maintaining commodity supply chains.

This document reviews the COVID-19 experience, its SRHR impacts, UNFPA's response, and the lessons for future pandemic preparedness. It also addresses the broader question of health security and SRHR — how pandemic preparedness frameworks (IHR, Pandemic Treaty negotiations) should integrate reproductive health continuity.

## KEY FACTS

- **COVID-19 SRHR disruptions**: UNFPA estimated 12 million women lost access to FP, and 1.4 million unintended pregnancies resulted from disruptions in 2020 alone
- **ANC decline**: Antenatal care visits dropped 20–40% during peak lockdowns in many LMICs (DHS PMA data)
- **GBV surge**: Reports of intimate partner violence increased 25–30% in many countries during lockdowns; UNFPA GBV hotline calls increased significantly
- **Supply chain disruption**: Contraceptive and maternal health commodity production and shipping were disrupted; UNFPA maintained supply through pre-positioning and alternative logistics
- **Health worker redeployment**: Midwives and SRHR nurses were redeployed to COVID response in some countries, reducing SRHR service capacity
- **UNFPA response**: Adapted MISP, scaled telemedicine and self-injectable DMPA-SC, distributed dignity kits, expanded GBV hotlines, maintained commodity procurement
- **Essential services designation**: UNFPA advocated successfully in many countries for SRHR to be classified as "essential services" exempt from lockdown closures
- **SRHR recovery**: By 2022–2023, most SRHR indicators had recovered to pre-COVID levels, but the pandemic caused a measurable setback in progress toward SDG targets
- **Pandemic Treaty negotiations**: Ongoing WHO negotiations for a pandemic accord; UNFPA and SRHR advocates push for inclusion of essential health service continuity provisions

## DETAIL

### Lessons from COVID-19

1. **SRHR must be designated essential from Day 1**: Countries that classified SRHR as essential services early experienced less disruption than those that did not
2. **Self-care is a resilience strategy**: Self-injectable contraception, medical abortion at home, and HIV self-testing proved their value when facility access was restricted
3. **GBV preparedness must be built into pandemic plans**: The "shadow pandemic" of GBV was predictable; preparedness should include GBV hotlines, safe spaces, and referral capacity
4. **Supply chain resilience requires pre-positioning**: Countries with pre-positioned reproductive health kits experienced fewer stockouts
5. **Telemedicine has value but is not a substitute**: Phone-based ANC and FP consultations maintained some access but could not replace physical examination and procedure-based services

### Future Pandemic Preparedness

The International Health Regulations (2005) and ongoing Pandemic Treaty negotiations provide the framework for future pandemic preparedness. UNFPA advocates for:
- Explicit inclusion of SRHR in essential health service packages during emergencies
- Commodity supply chain resilience for reproductive health products
- GBV preparedness as a standard component of epidemic/pandemic response plans
- Health worker protection and retention in SRHR roles during emergencies
- Digital health infrastructure for SRHR service continuity
