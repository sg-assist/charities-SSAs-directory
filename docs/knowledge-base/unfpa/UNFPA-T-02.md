## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Mobile health (mHealth) — the use of mobile devices and wireless technology for health — represents one of the most widely deployed digital health modalities in SRHR programming. mHealth interventions span a wide range: SMS-based health education for pregnant women, mobile apps for family planning method choice, telephone hotlines for GBV crisis response, mobile money integration for health service access, and mobile data collection for community health worker supervision. The appeal is clear: mobile phone penetration in LMICs is high (approximately 75%), reaching populations that fixed-infrastructure health systems do not.

The evidence base for mHealth in SRHR is mixed. Systematic reviews find moderate evidence that targeted SMS reminders improve ANC attendance and medication adherence; weaker evidence for mHealth improving family planning uptake; and very limited evidence for mHealth reducing GBV or improving adolescent SRHR outcomes. The most rigorous studies (RCTs) show modest effect sizes, and few mHealth interventions have demonstrated sustained impact at scale beyond pilot phases.

UNFPA has deployed mHealth in multiple country programmes — including mobile midwife programmes in Ghana, SMS-based family planning counselling in several countries, and GBV hotlines across humanitarian operations. These programmes have reached significant numbers of users but face consistent challenges: sustainability after donor funding ends, reaching the most marginalised users (poorest women, rural areas with poor connectivity), ensuring message quality and accuracy, and avoiding the assumption that information delivery alone changes behaviour.

## KEY FACTS

- **Mobile phone penetration (LMICs)**: Approximately 75% of population; but ownership does not equal regular use — women in LMICs are 16% less likely than men to own a mobile phone (GSMA 2023)
- **Smartphone vs feature phone**: In SSA, feature phones still outnumber smartphones; mHealth programmes must be designed for SMS/USSD, not just apps
- **Evidence — ANC reminders**: Moderate-quality evidence from RCTs that SMS reminders increase ANC attendance by approximately 10–20 percentage points in some settings
- **Evidence — FP**: Weak to moderate evidence; some studies show SMS-based FP information increases knowledge but effect on uptake is inconsistent
- **Evidence — GBV hotlines**: Limited rigorous evidence on outcomes; hotlines provide critical crisis response but measuring impact on GBV reduction is methodologically challenging
- **WHO recommendation**: WHO (2019) recommends "targeted client communication via mobile devices" for behaviour change in reproductive health — conditional recommendation, low certainty evidence
- **Pilot-to-scale challenge**: Over 75% of mHealth pilots in LMICs do not progress to national scale
- **Cost-effectiveness**: Limited data; some analyses suggest mHealth is cost-effective for ANC reminders in high-burden settings but not necessarily for lower-burden contexts
- **Literacy barrier**: Text-based mHealth excludes illiterate users; voice-based and IVR (interactive voice response) alternatives exist but are more expensive
- **Privacy concerns**: SMS messages about FP or GBV can be seen by partners or family members, creating safety risks — programme design must account for this

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

mHealth entered the global health lexicon in the late 2000s amid enthusiasm about mobile phone proliferation in Africa and Asia. Early proponents predicted that mobile technology would leapfrog infrastructure deficits and transform health service delivery. Reality has been more nuanced.

The WHO classifies mHealth interventions into categories: client-to-provider communication, provider-to-client communication (targeted messaging), provider-to-provider communication, and health worker decision support. SRHR-relevant applications include:

- **SMS health education**: Automated text messages with SRHR information (pregnancy milestones, contraceptive options, GBV resources)
- **Appointment reminders**: SMS reminders for ANC visits, FP follow-up, or immunisation
- **FP decision support**: Apps or USSD menus helping users identify appropriate contraceptive methods
- **GBV crisis response**: Phone hotlines and SMS-based reporting/referral for GBV survivors
- **Community health worker tools**: Mobile apps for CHW data collection, supervision, and job aids
- **Mobile money**: Integration with mobile payment for transport subsidies, service fees, or cash transfers related to SRHR

## DETAIL

### UNFPA mHealth Examples

**Mobile Midwife (Ghana)**: One of the longest-running mHealth programmes in SRHR. Pregnant women receive weekly voice or SMS messages in local languages with health information tailored to their gestational stage. Evaluations have shown improved ANC knowledge and some evidence of improved facility delivery rates, but attribution is challenging given concurrent health system investments.

**m4RH (mobile for Reproductive Health)**: Developed by FHI 360 and deployed in several UNFPA-supported countries. An SMS-based family planning information service where users text a short code and receive information about contraceptive methods. Evaluations show increased knowledge but limited evidence on uptake.

**GBV hotlines**: UNFPA operates or supports GBV hotlines in humanitarian settings across the Arab States, SSA, and EECA. These provide crisis counselling, safety planning, and referral to services. Usage data shows significant demand (hundreds of thousands of calls annually across all UNFPA-supported hotlines), but measuring impact on GBV outcomes is extremely difficult.

### Evidence Assessment

A 2020 Cochrane systematic review of mobile phone interventions for improving contraceptive use found that interventions probably increase the use of any contraceptive method (moderate certainty evidence) but may make little or no difference to the use of effective methods specifically. The effect sizes were modest.

Studies from Kenya, Malawi, and Bangladesh on mHealth for ANC have shown more consistent positive effects — SMS reminders and health information can increase ANC attendance, particularly for the recommended four or more visits.

For GBV, the evidence base is inherently limited by the difficulty of measuring prevention outcomes and the ethical constraints on randomised trials involving violence against women.

### Challenges

**Sustainability**: Most mHealth programmes in LMICs are donor-funded. When the project ends, the platform often ends with it. Government adoption of mHealth platforms into routine health systems is rare. The costs — platform hosting, SMS fees (which can be substantial in some markets), content updates, helpdesk support — require ongoing funding.

**Equity**: mHealth can inadvertently widen health disparities. Women most in need of SRHR services (poorest, most rural, least educated) are least likely to own phones, have credit for SMS, or be literate enough to use text-based services. Voice-based services help but are more expensive. Shared phone use (where a woman's phone is actually controlled by a male partner or family member) creates privacy and safety risks for FP and GBV messaging.

**Behaviour change complexity**: SRHR outcomes depend on far more than information access. A woman may know about contraceptive options (via mHealth) but face barriers from partner opposition, provider bias, commodity stockouts, cost, and social norms. mHealth addresses information gaps but not structural barriers.

---

## CURRENT STATUS

- mHealth remains a widely used tool in UNFPA SRHR programming, particularly for ANC and FP messaging
- Post-COVID, smartphone-based tools (apps, WhatsApp-based services) are increasingly used alongside SMS
- AI-powered chatbots for SRHR counselling are being piloted but evidence is limited
- The field is maturing from "pilot enthusiasm" to more realistic assessments of what mHealth can and cannot achieve
- Integration of mHealth into government health systems (rather than parallel donor platforms) is a priority
