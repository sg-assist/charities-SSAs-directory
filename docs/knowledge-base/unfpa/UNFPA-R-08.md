## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Food security and nutrition are deeply interconnected with reproductive health outcomes. Maternal undernutrition increases the risk of maternal mortality, preterm birth, low birth weight, and obstetric complications. Food insecurity drives child marriage (families marry daughters to reduce household mouths to feed), limits access to SRHR services (when food competes with healthcare for household spending), and creates conditions for sexual exploitation and survival sex. Climate change, conflict, and economic shocks simultaneously affect both food systems and SRHR services, making the food-SRHR nexus increasingly relevant to UNFPA's resilience programming.

UNFPA's direct engagement with food security is limited — WFP, FAO, and UNICEF are the lead UN agencies for nutrition and food security. However, UNFPA's mandate intersects at critical points: maternal nutrition during pregnancy (iron, folic acid supplementation); the food-GBV nexus in humanitarian settings; food insecurity as a driver of child marriage and adolescent pregnancy; and the demographic dimension of food systems (population growth affecting food demand).

This document maps the evidence on food-SRHR interconnections, identifies where UNFPA's programming can address these links, and highlights the relevance to the LKYSPP Challenge B on climate resilience in Asia, where food insecurity and SRHR vulnerability often co-occur.

## KEY FACTS

- **Maternal undernutrition**: Approximately 20% of maternal deaths in SSA are associated with nutritional deficiencies (anaemia, underweight)
- **Anaemia in pregnancy**: Approximately 37% of pregnant women globally are anaemic (WHO 2021); severe anaemia increases maternal mortality risk
- **Low birth weight**: Approximately 15% of newborns globally are low birth weight (<2,500g); strongly associated with maternal undernutrition
- **Food insecurity and child marriage**: In famine and food crisis contexts (Horn of Africa, Sahel), child marriage rates spike as families use marriage as a coping strategy
- **Food insecurity and GBV**: Survival sex and sexual exploitation increase during food crises; women and girls trade sex for food in humanitarian settings
- **Climate-food-SRHR nexus**: Climate impacts (drought, flooding) simultaneously disrupt food production and health service delivery, compounding SRHR vulnerability
- **Iron-folic acid supplementation**: WHO-recommended for all pregnant women; reduces anaemia and neural tube defects; UNFPA supports through ANC programming
- **Breastfeeding**: Exclusive breastfeeding for 6 months is WHO-recommended; links to both nutrition and birth spacing (lactational amenorrhoea method)
- **Population-food nexus**: Growing populations (particularly in SSA) increase food demand; family planning investment is part of the food security response — a connection that UNFPA historically emphasised but now frames more carefully to avoid demographic determinism

## DETAIL

### Where Food and SRHR Intersect

**Maternal nutrition**: UNFPA's ANC programming includes iron-folic acid supplementation and nutritional counselling. Adequate maternal nutrition reduces anaemia (which increases PPH risk), preterm birth, and low birth weight. In food-insecure settings, pregnant women may not have access to adequate nutrition even when supplements are provided — the supplement addresses the micronutrient gap but not the caloric deficit.

**Food insecurity as a GBV driver**: In humanitarian settings, food distribution points are known sites of sexual exploitation and abuse (SEA). Women and girls without food access may engage in survival sex. UNFPA's GBV programming in humanitarian settings must account for the food-GBV nexus — ensuring that GBV safe spaces are located near but not dependent on food distribution, and that food assistance and SRHR services are coordinated.

**Child marriage and food crisis**: In the Horn of Africa (2022), the Sahel, and other food-insecure regions, drought and food crisis have driven spikes in child marriage as families use marriage to reduce household size or secure bride price. UNFPA's child marriage prevention work must intensify during food crises, not pause.

**Demographic-food dynamics**: The relationship between population growth and food security remains relevant but must be handled carefully. UNFPA does not promote family planning as a population control measure for food security — this would conflict with the ICPD rights-based approach. Instead, UNFPA frames family planning as a right that, when fulfilled, also has co-benefits for food security through slower population growth and improved maternal and child nutrition.
