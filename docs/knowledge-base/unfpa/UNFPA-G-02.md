## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Asia and the Pacific is UNFPA's most demographically diverse region, encompassing the world's two most populous countries (India and China), rapidly ageing societies (Japan, South Korea, Thailand), extremely young populations (Afghanistan, Timor-Leste), Pacific Island states with populations under 100,000, and countries at every stage of the demographic and epidemiological transition. UNFPA operates through the Asia-Pacific Regional Office (APRO) in Bangkok, with country offices across South Asia, Southeast Asia, East Asia, and the Pacific.

The region has achieved remarkable SRHR progress over the past half-century. Contraceptive prevalence is above 60 per cent in most of East and Southeast Asia. Maternal mortality has declined dramatically in countries like Bangladesh (from 569 to 123/100,000 between 2000 and 2020), Cambodia, and Nepal. Several countries have achieved near-universal skilled birth attendance. But this regional average conceals acute disparities: Afghanistan's MMR remains approximately 620/100,000, parts of Pakistan and Papua New Guinea face severe maternal health challenges, and gender-biased sex selection in India, China, and the Caucasus reflects deep-rooted gender discrimination.

UNFPA's Asia-Pacific programming increasingly focuses on: adolescent SRHR in conservative social contexts; population ageing and its implications; climate change and SRHR (particularly in Pacific SIDS and flood-prone Bangladesh and Myanmar); GBV prevention; and population data systems. The region is also central to the LKYSPP Challenge B on resilience and public-private partnerships.

## KEY FACTS

- **Regional population**: Approximately 4.7 billion (Asia) plus approximately 45 million (Pacific) — over 60% of the world's population
- **MMR range**: From 5/100,000 (Japan) to 620/100,000 (Afghanistan); South Asia regional average approximately 140/100,000
- **Modern CPR**: Ranges from over 70% (China, Thailand, Vietnam) to under 25% (Afghanistan, Timor-Leste, Papua New Guinea)
- **Adolescent birth rate**: Low in East Asia (under 10/1,000) but high in some South Asian and Pacific countries (Bangladesh ~80/1,000, Laos ~65/1,000)
- **Child marriage**: South Asia has the highest absolute numbers; India alone accounts for one-third of the world's child brides (though rates have declined significantly)
- **Gender-biased sex selection**: India's sex ratio at birth approximately 110 boys per 100 girls (improving from 113); China approximately 111 (declining post-one-child policy relaxation)
- **Ageing**: Japan (29% aged 65+), South Korea (18%), Thailand (14%) face population ageing; UNFPA's mandate is expanding to address this
- **Pacific SIDS**: Extremely vulnerable to climate change; limited health infrastructure; high adolescent birth rates in some countries (Solomon Islands, PNG)
- **Afghanistan**: One of the world's worst SRHR profiles — MMR 620/100,000, CPR under 20%, girls' education severely restricted under Taliban rule
- **UNFPA in China**: Sensitive historical relationship due to one-child policy era; UNFPA maintains an office focused on ageing, gender, and data
- **Climate-SRHR nexus**: Bangladesh, Philippines, Myanmar, and Pacific nations face acute climate-related SRHR vulnerabilities
- **GBV prevalence**: Lifetime intimate partner violence prevalence ranges from approximately 15% (East Asia) to over 40% (some South Asian and Pacific contexts)

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### UNFPA's History in Asia-Pacific

UNFPA's engagement in Asia-Pacific dates to the organisation's founding era. In the 1970s and 1980s, Asia was the primary focus of global population programmes, with UNFPA supporting family planning efforts in India, Bangladesh, Indonesia, China, and other countries. Some of these early programmes — particularly in India (the Emergency-era sterilisation campaign, which UNFPA did not fund but which occurred during its engagement) and China (the one-child policy) — remain historically sensitive and continue to shape political debates about UNFPA's work.

The ICPD paradigm shift (1994) reframed UNFPA's Asian programming from population targets to reproductive rights. This transition has been largely successful in programme design but remains contested in some conservative political contexts where governments resist the rights-based language.

### Demographic Diversity

The Asia-Pacific region encompasses virtually every demographic scenario:

**Very low fertility / ageing societies**: Japan (TFR 1.2), South Korea (TFR 0.7 — the world's lowest), Singapore (TFR 1.0), Thailand (TFR 1.3), China (TFR 1.1). These countries face workforce shrinkage, rising elderly dependency ratios, and care economy challenges. UNFPA's role is shifting toward policy advisory on ageing, intergenerational solidarity, and age-friendly systems.

**Transitional fertility**: Indonesia (TFR 2.2), Vietnam (TFR 1.9), India (TFR 2.0 — recently crossing below replacement). These countries have achieved significant fertility decline but still face sub-national disparities and unmet need.

**High fertility**: Afghanistan (TFR 4.6), Timor-Leste (TFR 3.8), PNG (TFR 3.5), Pakistan (TFR 3.3 in rural areas). These countries have significant unmet need for family planning and high adolescent birth rates.

**Pacific SIDS**: Small populations (Tuvalu ~12,000, Nauru ~11,000) with limited health infrastructure, geographic isolation, and extreme climate vulnerability.

## DETAIL

### South Asia: Scale and Disparity

South Asia (India, Bangladesh, Pakistan, Nepal, Sri Lanka, Afghanistan, Bhutan, Maldives) contains the largest concentration of people with SRHR needs globally. India alone has approximately 48 million pregnancies annually. UNFPA's South Asian programming focuses on:

**India**: The largest UNFPA programme in Asia by expenditure. Focus areas include: reducing maternal mortality (India's MMR declined from 370 to 97/100,000 between 2000 and 2020, but with massive interstate variation — Assam at 195 vs Kerala at 19); expanding family planning choice (moving away from historical sterilisation bias toward reversible methods); addressing gender-biased sex selection (the Pre-Conception and Pre-Natal Diagnostic Techniques Act enforcement); adolescent SRHR; and GBV.

**Bangladesh**: A success story in family planning (CPR rose from under 10% in 1975 to approximately 64% in 2022) and maternal health (MMR from 569 to 123/100,000). UNFPA supports continued progress on SRHR, menstrual regulation services, community-based distribution, and climate-SRHR linkages (Bangladesh is one of the most climate-vulnerable nations).

**Afghanistan**: UNFPA's most challenging operating environment. Under Taliban rule (2021–), girls' secondary education is banned, women's mobility is severely restricted, and female health workers face operational constraints. UNFPA maintains essential SRHR service delivery through female health workers and midwives, but the operating environment is extremely difficult.

**Pakistan**: High fertility in rural areas, limited FP access, high maternal mortality in Sindh and Balochistan, and conservative social norms create significant SRHR challenges.

### Southeast Asia: Mixed Progress

Southeast Asian countries show enormous variation in SRHR outcomes. Thailand, Vietnam, and Malaysia have achieved strong SRHR indicators. Myanmar (political crisis since 2021 coup), Laos, and Cambodia face ongoing challenges. The Philippines has an adolescent birth rate of approximately 50/1,000 despite being a middle-income country, reflecting the influence of the Catholic Church on reproductive health policy and the delayed implementation of the Responsible Parenthood Act (2012).

### Pacific Islands: Climate Frontlines

Pacific Island countries and territories face unique SRHR challenges: geographic isolation makes service delivery expensive; small populations limit health workforce training; climate change (sea-level rise, cyclones) threatens health infrastructure; and adolescent SRHR needs are high. UNFPA operates through a multi-country office model in the Pacific, with sub-offices in several countries.

## CURRENT STATUS

- India's SRHR landscape is evolving rapidly as the country reaches below-replacement fertility nationally, shifting focus from family planning uptake to quality of care, method choice, and adolescent SRHR
- China's demographic shift (declining population since 2022, ageing) is redefining UNFPA's engagement toward ageing and gender
- The Afghanistan crisis continues to constrain SRHR programming under one of the world's most restrictive environments for women
- Climate-SRHR linkages are an emerging priority across the region, particularly for LKYSPP Challenge B
- The Pacific's vulnerability to climate disasters makes it a testing ground for resilience-focused SRHR programming
- Southeast Asian economic growth has not automatically translated into SRHR equity; sub-national disparities persist in Indonesia, Philippines, and Myanmar
