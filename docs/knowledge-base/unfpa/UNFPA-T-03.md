## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Geospatial population data — the integration of geographic information systems (GIS), satellite imagery, and statistical modelling to estimate where people live and what health services they can access — has become a critical tool for UNFPA's planning, targeting, and evaluation work. Accurate population maps at fine spatial resolution enable better health facility placement, more equitable resource allocation, improved humanitarian response, and more reliable census support.

UNFPA engages with geospatial data through several channels: supporting national statistical offices in using geospatial tools for census enumeration; partnering with academic and technology providers (WorldPop at the University of Southampton, GRID3, Meta/Facebook population density maps) to generate population estimates in data-scarce environments; using GIS to map SRHR service availability against population need; and applying geospatial analysis in humanitarian settings to estimate displacement and affected populations.

The promise of geospatial population data is significant: in countries where the last census is a decade or more old, satellite-based population estimates can provide interim data for health planning. In humanitarian crises where traditional enumeration is impossible, geospatial estimates of IDP camp populations can inform emergency service provision. For SRHR specifically, overlaying population density with health facility locations, contraceptive supply points, and skilled birth attendant distribution reveals geographic gaps in coverage that aggregate national statistics obscure.

However, geospatial data carries limitations: models are only as good as their input data and assumptions; resolution varies by context; ethical concerns arise from surveillance potential; and the technology requires technical capacity that many national statistical offices lack.

## KEY FACTS

- **WorldPop**: University of Southampton project providing open-access population distribution datasets at approximately 100m resolution for most countries globally; UNFPA is a key partner and user
- **GRID3 (Geo-Referenced Infrastructure and Demographic Data for Development)**: Multi-partner initiative supporting governments in producing and using geospatial data; UNFPA participates in several GRID3 country programmes
- **Meta population density maps**: Meta (formerly Facebook) uses AI and satellite imagery to produce high-resolution population density estimates; used in humanitarian response and development planning
- **Census support**: UNFPA supports census operations in approximately 90 countries per cycle; geospatial tools (satellite imagery for enumeration area delineation, GIS for fieldwork planning) are increasingly integrated
- **CRVS linkage**: Geospatial data combined with civil registration data enables continuous population monitoring between censuses
- **Humanitarian application**: During the Rohingya crisis (2017–), satellite imagery and geospatial modelling estimated camp populations when traditional counting was infeasible
- **SRHR service mapping**: GIS overlays of health facility locations, population distribution, and travel time enable identification of "SRHR deserts" — areas where women cannot reach a facility with skilled birth attendance within two hours
- **Climate vulnerability mapping**: UNFPA and partners overlay population data with climate risk data to identify populations facing combined SRHR and climate vulnerability
- **Resolution**: Gridded population datasets range from approximately 30m to 1km resolution; finer resolution provides better local estimates but requires more input data
- **Open data**: Most geospatial population products are open-access, enabling governments, researchers, and NGOs to use them freely
- **Technical capacity gap**: Many national statistical offices lack GIS expertise; UNFPA's capacity building role is critical for sustainable national adoption

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### Why Population Geography Matters for SRHR

National-level SRHR indicators (maternal mortality ratio, contraceptive prevalence rate) obscure enormous sub-national variation. A country's average CPR may be 60%, but specific districts, ethnic groups, or urban informal settlements may have CPR below 20%. Identifying these pockets of deprivation requires spatially disaggregated data.

Traditional data sources (censuses every 10 years, DHS every 3–5 years) provide limited spatial resolution and are quickly outdated. Geospatial methods fill this gap by providing continuous, high-resolution population estimates that can be combined with health facility data to assess coverage gaps.

### The Evolution of Geospatial Population Data

**2000s**: Early efforts to produce gridded population datasets (GPW — Gridded Population of the World, LandScan) used census data distributed across land area. Resolution was coarse (1km to 5km) and estimates were crude.

**2010s**: Machine learning approaches using satellite imagery (building footprints, nightlight intensity, land use classification) dramatically improved resolution and accuracy. WorldPop, GRID3, and Meta's High Resolution Settlement Layer emerged as leading products.

**2020s**: Integration with AI, building detection algorithms, and frequent satellite revisits enable near-real-time population estimation in some contexts. COVID-19 accelerated interest in geospatial data for health planning. UNFPA's census support increasingly incorporates geospatial tools.

## DETAIL

### Applications in UNFPA Programming

**Census modernisation**: UNFPA supports countries in using satellite imagery to create or update enumeration area maps, which form the geographic backbone of census fieldwork. In countries where population distribution has changed rapidly (urbanisation, displacement, new settlements), outdated enumeration area maps lead to undercounting. Satellite-based updates improve census accuracy.

**Health facility accessibility analysis**: Using GIS tools, UNFPA and partners calculate travel time from every location in a country to the nearest health facility offering EmONC, family planning, or skilled birth attendance. This reveals "SRHR deserts" where no facility is reachable within WHO-recommended travel times (2 hours for emergency obstetric care). Such analyses have informed health infrastructure investment in Nigeria, Mozambique, DRC, and other countries.

**Commodity supply chain targeting**: Combining population data with contraceptive prevalence estimates and supply chain data enables UNFPA to optimise commodity distribution — directing supplies to areas with highest unmet need rather than allocating based on historical patterns.

**Humanitarian response**: In rapid-onset emergencies, geospatial tools provide the first population estimates before ground-based assessments are possible. During Cyclone Idai (Mozambique, 2019), satellite damage assessment combined with pre-event population data estimated affected populations within days.

**Climate-SRHR vulnerability**: Overlaying climate hazard maps (flood zones, drought probability, cyclone tracks) with population distribution and SRHR facility data identifies communities facing compound vulnerability — a growing application for UNFPA's resilience programming.

### Limitations and Risks

**Model uncertainty**: Geospatial population estimates are modelled, not counted. In data-scarce environments (no recent census, limited survey coverage), model uncertainty can be substantial. Users must understand confidence intervals, not just point estimates.

**Ethical concerns**: High-resolution population maps, particularly in conflict or persecution contexts, can be misused for surveillance or targeting of vulnerable populations. The Rohingya population in Myanmar, indigenous communities, or refugee camps mapped at fine resolution raise concerns about data being used by state actors for harmful purposes.

**Technical capacity**: The analytical tools (R, Python, QGIS, Google Earth Engine) require technical skills that many national statistical offices lack. Without sustained capacity building, geospatial data products become externally produced and owned rather than integrated into national systems.

**Update frequency**: While satellite imagery is frequent, the statistical models linking imagery to population estimates need ground-truth validation (through censuses, surveys, or field counts) that is episodic. A model trained on 2010 census relationships may not accurately estimate 2025 populations in rapidly changing contexts.

## CURRENT STATUS

- Geospatial population data is now routinely used in UNFPA census support and humanitarian response
- GRID3 country programmes are active in approximately 10 countries, with UNFPA participation
