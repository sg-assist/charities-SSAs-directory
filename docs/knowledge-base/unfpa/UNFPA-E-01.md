## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

UNFPA's Evaluation Office is an independent function that reports directly to the Executive Board, not to UNFPA management. This structural independence — established to ensure evaluations are credible, impartial, and useful — makes the Evaluation Office one of UNFPA's primary accountability mechanisms. The Office conducts approximately 8–12 evaluations annually, covering country programme evaluations, thematic evaluations, joint evaluations with other UN entities, and meta-evaluations. Its work is guided by the United Nations Evaluation Group (UNEG) Norms and Standards, the gold standard for evaluation practice in the UN system.

Key thematic evaluations in recent years have assessed UNFPA's work on maternal health, GBV, adolescent SRHR, humanitarian response, family planning, and disability inclusion. Recurring findings include: UNFPA is highly relevant and well-positioned within the SRHR landscape; programme effectiveness varies significantly by country context and office capacity; sustainability of results beyond project funding is a persistent challenge; and cross-cutting commitments (disability, leave no one behind, gender transformation) are adopted at the policy level but unevenly implemented in programming.

The Evaluation Office also manages the management response system — requiring UNFPA management to respond to evaluation recommendations with specific action plans. Compliance with this system has improved over time, but implementation of accepted recommendations remains uneven.

## KEY FACTS

- **Independence**: Reports to Executive Board, not management; Director of Evaluation is appointed by the Executive Director with Board consultation
- **Evaluations per year**: Approximately 8–12 (mix of country programme, thematic, joint, and meta-evaluations)
- **UNEG compliance**: Follows UNEG Norms and Standards for Evaluation (2016); peer-reviewed by UNEG
- **Country programme evaluations**: Every UNFPA country programme is expected to be evaluated at least once per programme cycle (4 years); in practice, coverage is approximately 70%
- **Thematic evaluations**: Recent topics include maternal health (2021), GBV (2022), adolescent SRHR (2020), humanitarian response (2023), family planning (2019)
- **Joint evaluations**: Conducted with other UN entities (e.g., joint UNFPA-UNICEF evaluation of the FGM programme, joint evaluation of the Spotlight Initiative)
- **Management response**: Mandatory response to all evaluation recommendations; tracked through a management response tracking system
- **Budget**: Approximately USD 5–6 million annually for the Evaluation Office — modest relative to UNFPA's total budget
- **Decentralised evaluations**: Country offices conduct their own evaluations (with quality assurance from the Evaluation Office); quality varies
- **Evaluation use**: Board discussions reference evaluation findings; evaluations inform strategic plan design and programme adjustments

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### The Evolution of Evaluation at UNFPA

UNFPA established its evaluation function in the 1990s, but the function gained independence and strengthened quality through a series of reforms aligned with UNEG standards. The 2019 evaluation policy update strengthened the Evaluation Office's independence, clarified its relationship to the Board, and established quality assurance mechanisms for both centralised and decentralised evaluations.

### How Evaluations Work

A typical UNFPA evaluation follows a structured process:
1. **Terms of Reference**: Developed by the Evaluation Office with stakeholder input; defines scope, questions, methodology, and timeline
2. **Evaluation team**: External independent evaluators (not UNFPA staff) are hired through competitive procurement
3. **Inception phase**: Literature review, stakeholder mapping, methodology refinement, evaluation matrix development
4. **Data collection**: Country visits, document review, interviews with stakeholders (government, civil society, beneficiaries, donors, UN partners), quantitative data analysis
5. **Analysis and reporting**: Draft report, stakeholder feedback, final report with findings, conclusions, and recommendations
6. **Management response**: UNFPA management provides a formal response accepting, partially accepting, or rejecting each recommendation with planned actions
7. **Board presentation**: Major evaluations are presented to the Executive Board for discussion

## DETAIL

### Key Evaluation Findings (Themes Across Multiple Evaluations)

**Relevance**: UNFPA consistently receives high marks for relevance — its mandate is well-aligned with country needs, and its positioning as the lead UN entity for SRHR is widely recognised. The three transformative results framework provides clear strategic direction.

**Effectiveness**: Programme effectiveness varies significantly. Country programmes with strong government partnerships, adequate funding, and capable staff achieve results. Programmes in fragile states, with underfunded country offices, or facing political opposition to SRHR show weaker effectiveness. Specific findings:
- Maternal health interventions (midwifery training, EmONC support) show strong evidence of contribution to improved coverage
- GBV programming is reaching significant numbers of survivors but evidence on prevention outcomes is limited
- Family planning commodity procurement and supply is effective; demand-side interventions show more mixed results
- Adolescent SRHR programming is expanding but depth and quality vary

**Sustainability**: This is UNFPA's most consistent weakness. Evaluations repeatedly find that results depend on continued UNFPA funding and technical support; when projects end, gains are not always sustained through government systems. The transition from UNFPA-delivered to government-owned services is the critical and often missing step.

**Cross-cutting issues**: Disability inclusion, human rights-based approach, and gender-transformative programming are policy commitments but are inconsistently applied at the country level. Evaluations find that these cross-cutting themes receive attention in programme documents but less in implementation and budgeting.

**Humanitarian-development nexus**: Evaluations of UNFPA's humanitarian work find effective MISP implementation and GBV response but challenges in transitioning from emergency to development programming. The nexus is conceptually embraced but operationally difficult.

## CURRENT STATUS

- The Evaluation Office continues to produce high-quality evaluations that inform UNFPA programming and strategy
- The 2026–2029 Strategic Plan development process is drawing on evaluation evidence from the 2022–2025 cycle
- Evaluation coverage of country programmes has improved but is not yet universal
- Quality of decentralised (country-level) evaluations remains uneven; the Evaluation Office provides quality assurance but capacity varies
- The Board has expressed interest in more synthesis-level evaluation work that draws cross-cutting lessons rather than only programme-specific findings
