## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

South-South cooperation (SSC) and triangular cooperation (TrC) have become increasingly prominent in UNFPA's programming and partnership strategy. SSC refers to the exchange of knowledge, resources, technology, and expertise between developing countries (the "Global South"), while TrC involves a traditional donor or multilateral organisation facilitating or financing South-South exchanges. For UNFPA, these modalities offer opportunities to scale proven SRHR solutions across similar contexts, leverage the growing technical capacity of middle-income countries, and diversify partnerships beyond the traditional OECD donor base.

UNFPA has facilitated SSC in several programme areas: China sharing family planning logistics expertise with African countries; India's pharmaceutical manufacturing capacity supplying affordable reproductive health commodities to other developing nations; Brazil's community health agent model being adapted for SRHR service delivery in Lusophone Africa; and Thailand's HIV prevention experience informing programming in neighbouring countries.

However, the evidence base for SSC's effectiveness in SRHR is thin. Much SSC remains anecdotal, poorly documented, and driven by political narratives of solidarity rather than rigorous assessment of development impact. The "SSC is different from traditional aid" discourse can obscure power asymmetries between larger middle-income countries (China, India, Brazil) and smaller developing nations. UNFPA's challenge is to harness SSC's genuine potential while maintaining quality assurance, rights-based approaches, and honest evaluation.

## KEY FACTS

- **UN definition**: South-South cooperation is "a broad framework for collaboration among countries of the South in the political, economic, social, cultural, environmental and technical domains" (UNOSSC)
- **Buenos Aires Plan of Action (1978)**: The foundational framework for SSC in the UN system, reinforced by the 2019 BAPA+40 conference in Buenos Aires
- **UNFPA SSC strategy**: UNFPA adopted a South-South cooperation strategy aligned with its strategic plan, focusing on knowledge exchange, technical assistance, and partnership facilitation
- **China-Africa SSC**: China has provided technical assistance on population data, family planning logistics, and maternal health facility design to several African countries, facilitated by UNFPA
- **India's pharmaceutical exports**: India manufactures approximately 60% of the world's vaccines and significant volumes of generic contraceptives and reproductive health medicines; UNFPA's procurement leverages this capacity
- **Brazil's Community Health Agents**: Brazil's Programa Agentes Comunitários de Saúde (PACS) has been studied and adapted by several countries for SRHR community outreach
- **Thailand's 100% condom programme**: Thailand's successful HIV prevention model has been shared with neighbouring countries in Southeast Asia
- **SSC funding flows**: Difficult to quantify; estimates range from USD 20–40 billion annually across all sectors (not SRHR-specific), but methodologies vary widely and reporting is not standardised
- **Triangular cooperation**: UNFPA has facilitated TrC arrangements where traditional donors fund SSC exchanges (e.g., Japan funding UNFPA-facilitated knowledge transfer from Southeast Asian countries to Africa)
- **UNOSSC (UN Office for South-South Cooperation)**: The UN system's dedicated office, hosted by UNDP; UNFPA coordinates with UNOSSC on SSC initiatives

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### The Political Economy of South-South Cooperation

SSC is both a development modality and a political project. Its roots lie in the Non-Aligned Movement, the Bandung Conference (1955), and decades of Global South solidarity discourse. For countries like China, India, and Brazil, SSC is an expression of emerging power status and an alternative to the donor-recipient relationship that characterises traditional development assistance.

The political framing matters for UNFPA because SSC providers often resist the conditionalities, monitoring requirements, and rights-based frameworks that traditional donors attach to their aid. When UNFPA facilitates SSC, it must navigate between: (a) respecting SSC principles of mutual benefit, sovereignty, and non-conditionality; and (b) maintaining UNFPA's own mandate commitments to human rights, gender equality, and evidence-based programming.

### SSC in SRHR: The Landscape

SRHR-specific SSC is a relatively small subset of overall South-South exchanges. The most significant modalities include:

**Knowledge exchange**: Government officials, health workers, or programme managers from one developing country visiting another to learn about successful SRHR programmes. UNFPA frequently facilitates study tours, expert exchanges, and peer learning platforms.

**Technical assistance**: Countries with strong SRHR systems (e.g., Thailand on HIV, Bangladesh on community-based family planning, Rwanda on maternal health) providing technical advisors to other countries.

**Commodity supply**: India and China as manufacturers of affordable reproductive health commodities (contraceptives, maternal health medicines, medical devices) that UNFPA procures for programme countries.

**Institutional model transfer**: Adapting institutional innovations (Brazil's family health strategy, Sri Lanka's maternal death surveillance, Iran's family planning programme) for different contexts.

## DETAIL

### UNFPA-Facilitated SSC Examples

**China-Africa Population Data**: UNFPA has facilitated exchanges between China's National Bureau of Statistics and African national statistical offices on census methodology, data processing, and demographic analysis. China's experience with large-scale population enumeration (its 2020 census covered 1.4 billion people) provides relevant technical expertise, though the political context of China's population policies adds complexity.

**India-Africa Pharmaceutical Supply**: India's generic pharmaceutical industry manufactures significant quantities of the contraceptives and reproductive health medicines that UNFPA procures and distributes in Africa and Asia. This is SSC in economic terms — a developing country's manufacturing capacity serving other developing countries' health needs — though it operates through commercial procurement rather than aid modalities.

**Bangladesh Community-Based Family Planning**: Bangladesh's success in reducing fertility from over 6 children per woman (1970s) to approximately 2.3 (2022) through community-based family planning distribution is one of the most-cited SRHR success stories. UNFPA has facilitated knowledge sharing from Bangladesh's experience to other countries, particularly in sub-Saharan Africa.

**Rwanda Maternal Health Model**: Rwanda's dramatic reduction in maternal mortality (from approximately 1,270/100,000 in 2000 to approximately 248/100,000 in 2020, per WHO estimates) through community health workers, performance-based financing, and universal health coverage has attracted SSC interest from other African countries.

### Limitations and Critiques

**Evidence gap**: Most SSC in SRHR is documented through case studies and success narratives rather than rigorous evaluation. Whether knowledge transfers actually change outcomes in recipient countries is rarely assessed systematically.

**Power asymmetries**: SSC's rhetoric of "mutual benefit" and "horizontal partnership" can obscure real power differentials. When China builds a hospital in an African country, the dynamics may not differ substantially from traditional donor-funded infrastructure.

**Context transferability**: SRHR programmes are deeply embedded in cultural, political, and health system contexts. What works in Bangladesh (strong NGO sector, government commitment, specific cultural factors) may not work in Niger or DRC. SSC can oversimplify the transferability of complex social interventions.

**Rights concerns**: Some SSC providers have domestic SRHR policies that conflict with UNFPA's rights-based approach. China's history of coercive population policies, India's past sterilisation campaigns, and restrictions on reproductive rights in some SSC provider countries create tensions when these countries present themselves as SRHR models.

**Reporting and accountability**: SSC flows are not reported through the OECD DAC system, making it difficult to track volumes, assess quality, or hold providers accountable. UNFPA's own reporting on SSC facilitation is primarily narrative rather than results-based.

## CURRENT STATUS

- UNFPA continues to facilitate SSC/TrC as part of its partnership strategy, with dedicated focal points in regional offices
- The BAPA+40 outcome document (2019) reaffirmed SSC principles and encouraged UN entities to expand facilitation; UNFPA has aligned its approach accordingly
- Growing interest from Gulf states and Asian middle-income countries (Malaysia, Thailand, Vietnam) in providing SSC in SRHR
- Digital platforms for SSC knowledge exchange have expanded post-COVID-19, reducing the cost of peer learning
- The evidence base for SSC effectiveness in SRHR remains weak; UNFPA has not conducted a comprehensive evaluation of its SSC portfolio
- China's Belt and Road health investments create both opportunities (infrastructure, commodity supply) and concerns (debt, governance, rights) for SRHR programming
