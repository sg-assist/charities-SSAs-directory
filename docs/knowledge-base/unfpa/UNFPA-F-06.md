## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

UNFPA's strategic plans have evolved significantly since the organisation's founding, reflecting shifts in the global development landscape, the ICPD paradigm, donor expectations, and the organisation's own learning. The current planning architecture follows a four-year cycle aligned with the Quadrennial Comprehensive Policy Review (QCPR), with each strategic plan setting the results framework, resource allocation principles, and programmatic priorities that guide UNFPA's global operations.

The most significant conceptual evolution has been the progressive sharpening of UNFPA's focus around the "three transformative results" — ending preventable maternal deaths, ending unmet need for family planning, and ending gender-based violence and harmful practices — first articulated in the 2014–2017 plan and refined in subsequent cycles. This focus has improved strategic clarity and external communication but has also narrowed the frame through which UNFPA's work is understood, potentially marginalising important areas like population data, ageing, migration, and adolescent development that do not fit neatly within the three results.

This document traces the evolution of UNFPA's strategic plans from the post-ICPD era through the current 2022–2025 plan and the emerging 2026–2029 framework, examining how strategy has responded to crises, evidence, and political shifts.

---

## KEY FACTS

- **Strategic plan cycle**: Four years, aligned with the QCPR cycle
- **Current plan**: UNFPA Strategic Plan 2022–2025, endorsed by the Executive Board in 2021
- **Next plan**: 2026–2029, under development with expected Board endorsement in 2025
- **Three transformative results (first articulated 2014)**: (1) End preventable maternal deaths; (2) End unmet need for family planning; (3) End gender-based violence and harmful practices
- **ICPD alignment**: All strategic plans since 1994 reference the ICPD Programme of Action as the foundational mandate
- **SDG alignment**: The 2018–2021 and 2022–2025 plans explicitly align with SDGs 3, 5, and other goals
- **ICPD25 Nairobi commitments (2019)**: Over 1,300 commitments from 170+ countries; UNFPA committed to specific acceleration targets incorporated into the 2022–2025 plan
- **Midterm reviews**: Conducted at the two-year mark of each plan cycle; the 2024 midterm review of the 2022–2025 plan assessed progress and identified areas for acceleration
- **Budget integration**: Each strategic plan includes an Integrated Budget linking programme resources, institutional budget, and the IRRF
- **Humanitarian integration**: The 2018–2021 plan was the first to explicitly integrate humanitarian response as a core function rather than an add-on

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### Pre-ICPD Era: Population and Development

Before 1994, UNFPA's strategic direction was shaped by the population and development paradigm — a framework that emphasised demographic targets (reducing fertility rates, slowing population growth) as a means to economic development. This approach was reflected in UNFPA's support for family planning programmes that, in some countries, prioritised demographic outcomes over individual choice. The language of "population control," while never UNFPA's official terminology, was common in the broader field.

### Post-ICPD Paradigm Shift (1994–2007)

The 1994 International Conference on Population and Development fundamentally reoriented UNFPA's strategic direction. The ICPD Programme of Action established that:
- Population policies must respect individual rights and choices
- Reproductive health and rights are the organising framework, not demographic targets
- Gender equality and women's empowerment are prerequisites for sustainable development
- Adolescent health and education require specific attention

UNFPA's strategic frameworks in this period reflected the ICPD principles but were relatively broad — covering reproductive health, gender equality, population and development, and advocacy. The organisation had not yet developed the sharp programmatic focus that would come later.

### The Multi-Year Funding Framework and First Strategic Plans (2008–2013)

The 2008–2011 Strategic Plan represented a more structured approach, introducing a results-based framework with defined outcomes and outputs. The 2012–2013 extension bridged to the next full plan cycle. During this period, UNFPA began to sharpen its focus areas and improve its results architecture.

## DETAIL

### Strategic Plan 2014–2017: The Three Transformative Results

This plan was transformative for UNFPA's identity. It introduced the "three transformative results" — ending preventable maternal deaths, ending unmet need for family planning, and ending gender-based violence and harmful practices — as the organising framework for all UNFPA programming. This was a deliberate strategic choice: rather than being "the population agency" or "the reproductive health agency," UNFPA positioned itself around three specific, measurable, time-bound goals that aligned with the emerging SDG framework.

The plan also:
- Introduced the concept of "modes of engagement" (advocacy, service delivery, capacity building, knowledge management)
- Emphasised the demographic dividend as a strategic opportunity for investment
- Strengthened humanitarian response as a core function
- Aligned with the QCPR and Delivering as One frameworks

**Assessment**: The 2014–2017 plan was widely credited with improving UNFPA's strategic clarity and external positioning. Critics noted that some important areas (population data, ageing, migration) were marginalised by the three-results framework.

### Strategic Plan 2018–2021: Acceleration and Leave No One Behind

Building on the three transformative results, the 2018–2021 plan introduced the concept of "acceleration" — recognising that achieving the SDGs by 2030 required not just continued progress but a faster pace of change. The plan:

- Set explicit acceleration targets for each transformative result
- Integrated the SDG principle of "leaving no one behind" with attention to marginalised populations
- Strengthened the humanitarian-development nexus approach
- Introduced innovation and data as cross-cutting enablers
- Expanded attention to adolescents and youth as a strategic priority

The plan period was dramatically disrupted by COVID-19 (2020–2021), which diverted health system resources away from SRHR, disrupted supply chains, increased GBV, and closed schools — reversing progress on multiple indicators. UNFPA's response included rapid adaptation (telemedicine for ANC, dignity kit distribution, GBV hotlines) but the pandemic exposed the fragility of SRHR gains.

**Assessment**: The plan's strategic direction was sound, but COVID-19 made the acceleration targets unreachable. The pandemic also revealed weaknesses in UNFPA's digital infrastructure, supply chain resilience, and capacity to maintain SRHR services during health system shocks.

### Strategic Plan 2022–2025: Recovery and the Unfinished Agenda

Developed in the shadow of COVID-19, the 2022–2025 plan retained the three transformative results but added emphasis on:

- **Recovery from COVID-19 setbacks**: Rebuilding SRHR service delivery disrupted by the pandemic
- **Bodily autonomy**: Elevated as a cross-cutting principle, drawing on the 2021 State of World Population report
- **Data and evidence**: Strengthened commitment to population data, disaggregated data, and evidence-based programming
- **Climate and humanitarian response**: Recognition of climate change as a driver of SRHR vulnerability
- **Partnerships**: Greater emphasis on South-South cooperation, private sector engagement, and multi-stakeholder platforms

The plan's IRRF includes approximately 30 outcome and 40+ output indicators, with targets set against 2021 baselines (adjusted for COVID-19 impacts).

**Midterm review (2024)**: Found mixed progress — family planning indicators showing recovery, maternal mortality gains stalling, GBV and harmful practices showing uneven progress, population data work on track. Humanitarian demands (Sudan, Gaza, Ukraine, Myanmar) absorbed growing resources.

### Emerging Strategic Plan 2026–2029

The next strategic plan is under development as of 2025–2026, with several anticipated directions:

- Continued focus on the three transformative results, potentially with refined indicators
- Stronger integration of climate change as a cross-cutting driver
- Greater attention to technology, digital health, and AI as enablers
- Revenue diversification and innovative financing
- Enhanced humanitarian-development-peace nexus programming
- Deeper attention to adolescent and youth agency
- Potential expansion of the mandate frame to accommodate ageing populations and migration

The plan will be the last full cycle before the 2030 SDG deadline, making its targets politically significant — UNFPA will need to assess honestly which SDG targets are achievable and which represent an unfinished agenda for the post-2030 framework.

## CURRENT STATUS

- The 2022–2025 Strategic Plan is in its final implementation year
- Consultations for the 2026–2029 plan are underway, including regional consultations, youth consultations, and thematic expert meetings
- The Executive Board is expected to endorse the new plan in its 2025 annual session or early 2026
- Key debates include: whether to retain or modify the three transformative results framework; how to integrate climate change; how to address the growing humanitarian portfolio; and how to position UNFPA for the post-2030 development framework
- The Nairobi Summit (ICPD25) commitments are being reviewed for incorporation into the new plan, with accountability for 2019 pledges
