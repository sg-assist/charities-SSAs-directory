## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

HIV/AIDS and sexual and reproductive health have been historically separated in global health programming — funded through different channels, delivered through different facilities, and governed by different institutional structures. This separation is both artificial and harmful: HIV is sexually transmitted, SRHR services provide the primary platform for HIV prevention, and people living with HIV have specific reproductive health needs (safe conception, PMTCT, contraception, cervical cancer screening). The case for integration is well-established in evidence and endorsed by WHO, UNAIDS, and UNFPA, yet implementation remains incomplete due to funding silos, institutional inertia, and political sensitivities.

UNFPA's role in the HIV response is defined by the UNAIDS Division of Labour, which assigns UNFPA lead responsibility for: condom programming; linking SRHR and HIV; HIV prevention among young people and key populations; and addressing gender inequality in the HIV response. UNFPA is one of 11 UNAIDS Cosponsors but is not the dominant HIV agency (UNAIDS Secretariat, WHO, and UNICEF have larger HIV portfolios), nor does it receive the largest HIV-specific funding (the Global Fund and PEPFAR dwarf UNFPA's HIV-related budget).

The integration agenda has progressed but remains unfinished. Countries that have integrated HIV testing into ANC (Botswana, Rwanda, Thailand) have achieved dramatic reductions in mother-to-child transmission. Countries that offer family planning at HIV treatment sites have improved dual protection. But in many countries, SRHR and HIV services remain parallel: a woman visiting an ANC clinic must go to a separate HIV testing site, then to a separate FP provider, then to a separate STI clinic — each visit requiring separate travel, waiting, and fees.

## KEY FACTS

- **Global HIV burden (2023)**: Approximately 39.9 million people living with HIV; 1.3 million new infections; 630,000 AIDS-related deaths (UNAIDS 2024)
- **Sub-Saharan Africa**: Approximately 25.6 million PLHIV — 65% of the global total; young women aged 15–24 in SSA are 3 times more likely than young men to acquire HIV
- **Key populations**: MSM, sex workers, people who inject drugs, transgender persons, and prisoners account for approximately 55% of new infections globally despite being a small share of the population
- **PMTCT (prevention of mother-to-child transmission)**: Global coverage approximately 82%; when integrated into ANC, HIV transmission from mother to child drops from ~25% to under 2%
- **Dual protection**: Condoms prevent both pregnancy and HIV/STIs — the only method offering dual protection; yet condom programming is often separate from FP programmes
- **Cervical cancer and HIV**: Women living with HIV are 6 times more likely to develop cervical cancer; integration of HPV screening into HIV care is critical
- **UNAIDS Division of Labour**: UNFPA is co-convener on condom programming and SRHR-HIV linkages; one of 11 Cosponsors
- **Global Fund**: The largest source of HIV/AIDS, TB, and malaria funding (~USD 4B annually); funds are channelled to countries, not to UNFPA directly
- **PEPFAR**: US President's Emergency Plan for AIDS Relief; approximately USD 6–7B annually; the largest bilateral HIV programme
- **Integration evidence**: Cochrane review (2018) found that integrating FP into HIV services increases contraceptive uptake without compromising HIV care quality

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### The Separation Problem

The HIV/AIDS response developed as a vertical programme — with dedicated funding, institutions, and delivery mechanisms — separate from the broader SRHR agenda. This separation had pragmatic origins: the HIV epidemic required urgent, focused action that the general health system was not providing. The creation of UNAIDS (1996), the Global Fund (2002), and PEPFAR (2003) established massive HIV-specific funding channels that dwarfed SRHR budgets.

But the separation created problems:
- SRHR services (ANC, FP, STI treatment) were the natural platform for HIV prevention and care, yet HIV funding flowed to separate facilities and programmes
- Women living with HIV had unmet family planning needs but were not offered contraception at HIV clinics
- HIV prevention for young people was separated from CSE
- Condom programming was divided between FP (under UNFPA/SRHR) and HIV prevention (under UNAIDS/Global Fund)

### The Integration Agenda

The evidence for integration has been building since the early 2000s:
- **2004 Glion Consultation**: Called for linking SRHR and HIV
- **2010 UNFPA-WHO-UNAIDS framework**: Published "Sexual and Reproductive Health and HIV: Linkages" with specific recommendations
- **2018 Lancet Commission on SRHR**: Explicitly called for integration as essential for both SRHR and HIV outcomes
- **WHO guidelines**: Multiple guidelines recommend integrating FP into HIV services, HIV testing into ANC, and cervical cancer screening into HIV care

## DETAIL

### Where Integration Works

**ANC-HIV integration**: Countries that have integrated HIV testing into routine ANC visits have achieved near-universal testing of pregnant women and dramatic PMTCT coverage. Botswana, Rwanda, and Thailand have virtually eliminated MTCT through this approach. The key: testing is opt-out (default) rather than opt-in, provided at the same visit as other ANC services, with immediate ART initiation for HIV-positive women.

**FP-HIV integration**: Offering family planning services at ART (antiretroviral therapy) clinics increases contraceptive uptake among women living with HIV. Studies in Kenya, Uganda, and Malawi found 30–60% increases in modern FP use when services were co-located. This prevents unintended pregnancies among PLHIV and reduces MTCT.

**Cervical cancer-HIV integration**: Women living with HIV face 6x risk of cervical cancer due to HPV co-infection. Integrating HPV screening into HIV care visits (rather than requiring a separate screening visit) dramatically increases screening coverage. PEPFAR has invested significantly in cervical cancer screening in SSA, and UNFPA's cervical cancer programming aligns with this approach.

### UNFPA's Specific Role

UNFPA's HIV-related work focuses on:
1. **Condom programming**: UNFPA is the world's largest multilateral procurer of male and female condoms; distributes approximately 1 billion condoms annually to programme countries
2. **SRHR-HIV linkages**: Technical support to countries for integrating FP, ANC, and HIV services
3. **Youth HIV prevention**: Working with young people on SRHR education that includes HIV prevention
4. **Key population services**: SRHR services for MSM, sex workers, and transgender persons
5. **Gender and HIV**: Addressing gender inequality as a driver of women's HIV vulnerability

### Barriers to Integration

**Funding silos**: The Global Fund and PEPFAR have specific HIV mandates and reporting requirements that may not accommodate integrated SRHR-HIV programming. A health facility funded by the Global Fund to provide ART may not have a budget line for contraceptive supplies or FP counselling.

**Institutional separation**: In many countries, the National AIDS Control Programme and the Reproductive Health Division operate separately within the Ministry of Health — with different budgets, staff, and reporting lines. Integration at the policy level does not automatically translate to integration at the facility level.

**Political sensitivities**: HIV prevention for key populations (MSM, sex workers) is politically sensitive in many countries. Linking this work to "mainstream" SRHR programming can either normalise key population services (positive) or politicise SRHR programming (negative), depending on context.

---

## CURRENT STATUS

- SRHR-HIV integration has progressed significantly but remains incomplete in most LMIC health systems
- PMTCT integration into ANC is a major success story with near-universal coverage in many high-burden countries
- FP-HIV integration is advancing but unevenly; facility-level integration depends on training, commodity supply, and management commitment
- The Global Fund's 2023–2028 strategy includes sexual and reproductive health as a cross-cutting theme, potentially reducing funding silos
- PEPFAR's future under the current US administration is uncertain, with implications for global HIV financing
- UNFPA's condom procurement and SRHR-HIV technical assistance continue as core functions
