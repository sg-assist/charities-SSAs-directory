## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Small Island Developing States (SIDS) are among the most climate-vulnerable nations on earth, and their SRHR challenges are shaped by this vulnerability alongside geographic isolation, small populations, limited health workforces, and economies dependent on external support. UNFPA works in SIDS across three oceanic regions: the Pacific (Fiji, Vanuatu, Samoa, Tonga, Solomon Islands, Kiribati, Tuvalu, Marshall Islands, Micronesia, Palau, Nauru, Cook Islands, PNG), the Caribbean (Jamaica, Trinidad and Tobago, Barbados, Guyana, Suriname, Haiti, and others), and the Atlantic/Indian Ocean (Cabo Verde, Comoros, Maldives, Mauritius, São Tomé and Príncipe, Seychelles).

SIDS share common characteristics: small populations (some under 100,000), limited health infrastructure, high per-capita costs of service delivery due to geographic dispersion, vulnerability to cyclones and sea-level rise that can destroy health facilities and displace populations, and economic dependence on tourism, fisheries, and remittances. SRHR indicators vary widely — from Haiti (MMR approximately 350/100,000, the highest in the Western Hemisphere) to Barbados and Mauritius (MMR under 50/100,000).

UNFPA operates primarily through multi-country offices (MCOs) in SIDS regions, with one office covering multiple countries — a model that achieves efficiency but limits country-specific depth. The climate-SRHR nexus is particularly acute in SIDS: when cyclones destroy health facilities, maternal and reproductive health services are among the first disrupted and last restored. Climate migration and displacement create additional SRHR vulnerabilities. SIDS are thus a critical testing ground for UNFPA's emerging climate-resilience programming.

## KEY FACTS

- **SIDS count**: 39 UN-recognised SIDS across three oceanic regions
- **Combined SIDS population**: Approximately 65 million, but highly concentrated — PNG alone (approximately 10 million) and Haiti (approximately 11 million) account for roughly one-third
- **MMR range in SIDS**: From under 30/100,000 (Barbados, Mauritius) to approximately 350/100,000 (Haiti) and approximately 145/100,000 (PNG)
- **Adolescent birth rate**: High in several Pacific SIDS — Solomon Islands approximately 78/1,000, PNG approximately 52/1,000, Vanuatu approximately 47/1,000; also high in Caribbean SIDS
- **Climate vulnerability**: 8 of the 10 most climate-vulnerable countries globally are SIDS (Germanwatch Global Climate Risk Index)
- **Cyclone impacts**: Cyclone Pam (Vanuatu, 2015) destroyed 90% of crops and severely damaged health infrastructure; Cyclone Winston (Fiji, 2016) was the strongest recorded in the Southern Hemisphere
- **Sea-level rise**: Kiribati, Tuvalu, and Marshall Islands face existential threats from sea-level rise; potential for entire population displacement within decades
- **Health workforce**: Many SIDS have fewer than 1 physician per 1,000 population; specialist obstetricians may be absent entirely on outer islands
- **UNFPA MCOs**: Pacific Multi-Country Office (Suva, Fiji); Caribbean Sub-Regional Office (Jamaica); other SIDS covered through regional arrangements
- **Contraceptive access**: Supply chain challenges due to distance, small order volumes, and limited storage infrastructure; stockouts are common on outer islands
- **GBV in SIDS**: Extremely high prevalence in some Pacific SIDS — lifetime intimate partner violence exceeds 60% in Solomon Islands, Kiribati, and Vanuatu
- **SAMOA Pathway (2014)**: The framework for SIDS sustainable development, recognising their unique vulnerabilities

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### Why SIDS Are Different

SIDS face a unique combination of development challenges that distinguish them from other developing country categories:

**Remoteness and dispersion**: Pacific SIDS span millions of square kilometres of ocean. Vanuatu comprises 83 islands; the Marshall Islands stretches across nearly 2 million km² of ocean. Delivering SRHR services to outer island communities requires boats, small aircraft, or long sea journeys — dramatically increasing per-capita costs.

**Small scale**: Countries with populations under 100,000 (Tuvalu, Nauru, Palau) cannot sustain the specialised health workforce needed for comprehensive SRHR services. A single obstetrician departure can leave a country without specialist maternal care.

**Climate vulnerability**: SIDS contribute negligibly to greenhouse gas emissions but bear disproportionate climate impacts. Sea-level rise threatens the physical existence of atoll nations; cyclones regularly destroy health facilities; drought affects water supply for health facilities; and coral reef degradation undermines food security and economic livelihoods.

**Economic vulnerability**: Tourism-dependent economies are highly sensitive to external shocks (COVID-19 devastated Pacific and Caribbean tourism). Limited fiscal space constrains health spending.

## DETAIL

### Climate-SRHR Nexus in SIDS

The intersection of climate change and SRHR is more immediate in SIDS than anywhere else:

**Cyclone impacts on maternal health**: When cyclones destroy health facilities, pregnant women lose access to antenatal care, skilled birth attendance, and emergency obstetric care. UNFPA's experience responding to Cyclone Pam (2015), Cyclone Winston (2016), Cyclone Harold (2020), and other events has demonstrated that: reproductive health kits must be pre-positioned before cyclone season; temporary maternity shelters need rapid deployment capacity; and communication systems for referral must be climate-resilient.

**Climate migration and SRHR**: As sea-level rise, drought, and repeated disasters make some islands uninhabitable, migration — internal and international — creates SRHR challenges. Displaced women and girls face disrupted maternal care continuity, increased GBV risk, and reduced contraceptive access in destination settings.

**Heat and reproductive health**: Emerging evidence suggests that extreme heat affects pregnancy outcomes (preterm birth, low birth weight). SIDS with limited air conditioning and climate-resilient infrastructure face direct heat impacts on maternal health.

### GBV in the Pacific

The Pacific has some of the world's highest rates of intimate partner violence. The Solomon Islands Family Health and Safety Study found that 64% of ever-partnered women aged 15–49 had experienced physical and/or sexual partner violence — among the highest rates documented globally. Vanuatu, Kiribati, Samoa, and Tonga also show elevated rates.

UNFPA's Pacific GBV programming includes safe spaces, helplines, community engagement, and support for legislative reform. Progress has been made — Vanuatu enacted the Family Protection Act (2008), Solomon Islands the Family Protection Act (2014) — but implementation and enforcement remain weak.

### UNFPA's Multi-Country Office Model

UNFPA's Pacific MCO (Suva) covers 14 Pacific Island countries and territories from a single office, with some sub-offices. This model:
- Achieves administrative efficiency (one office instead of 14)
- Allows pooling of technical expertise
- But limits country-specific depth and responsiveness
- Creates challenges for countries that feel underserved by a distant regional office

The model is pragmatic given small populations and limited resources, but UNFPA staff acknowledge the trade-offs.

## CURRENT STATUS

- Climate change is increasingly central to UNFPA's SIDS programming, with climate-resilient SRHR service delivery becoming a programme priority
- GBV remains a critical challenge in Pacific SIDS with high prevalence and limited services
- COVID-19 severely impacted SIDS economies and disrupted SRHR service delivery; recovery is ongoing
- The SIDS4 conference (Antigua and Barbuda, 2024) reaffirmed commitments to SIDS-specific development needs including health and gender equality
- UNFPA continues to advocate for SIDS-specific approaches to SRHR within the broader UN system
- Pre-positioning of reproductive health supplies and climate-resilient health facility design are emerging priorities
