## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The International Conference on Population and Development (ICPD), held in Cairo from 5–13 September 1994, produced a 20-year Programme of Action (PoA) that fundamentally reoriented global population policy. Before Cairo, the dominant paradigm was demographic: governments set fertility targets, and population programmes were designed to reduce birth rates for macroeconomic purposes. The ICPD replaced this with a rights-based paradigm: reproductive health and reproductive rights became the organising concepts, individual choice replaced demographic targets, and gender equality and women's empowerment were recognised as both ends in themselves and prerequisites for sustainable development.

The PoA was adopted by 179 governments — a near-universal consensus — though with notable reservations from the Holy See, several Latin American countries aligned with the Vatican, and some Organisation of Islamic Cooperation (OIC) members on issues relating to abortion, adolescent sexuality, and family definitions. Despite these reservations, the core paradigm shift was endorsed and has shaped UNFPA's mandate, global health policy, and reproductive rights advocacy for three decades.

The PoA's legacy is substantial but incomplete. It catalysed the creation of the "reproductive health" concept as a field, transformed UNFPA's programming, influenced national legislation and policies in dozens of countries, and provided the normative foundation for the SDGs' SRHR targets. But many of its commitments remain unfulfilled: maternal mortality has declined but not enough; unmet need for family planning persists for over 200 million women; gender-based violence remains pervasive; and the promise of universal access to reproductive health services by 2015 was not achieved.

The ICPD+5 (1999), ICPD+10 (2004), ICPD+20 (2014) reviews, and the ICPD25 Nairobi Summit (2019) have reaffirmed and updated the Cairo commitments. The Nairobi Summit generated over 1,300 commitments from more than 170 countries and stakeholders, representing the most significant recommitment to the ICPD agenda since 1994.

## KEY FACTS

- **Conference**: International Conference on Population and Development, Cairo, Egypt, 5–13 September 1994
- **Attendance**: 179 governments, plus NGOs, civil society, and international organisations
- **Programme of Action**: 16 chapters, covering population, reproductive health, gender equality, family, migration, education, technology, and international cooperation
- **Duration**: Originally a 20-year PoA (1994–2014); extended "beyond 2014" by General Assembly resolution and the Nairobi Summit commitments
- **Paradigm shift**: From demographic targets to reproductive rights; from population control to individual choice
- **Key concepts introduced/formalised**: Reproductive health, reproductive rights, sexual health, unmet need for family planning, bodily autonomy (concept, though term came later)
- **Reservations**: Holy See, several Catholic-majority Latin American countries, and some OIC members registered reservations on abortion, adolescent SRHR, and family definitions
- **ICPD+5 (1999)**: Five-year review at UNGASS; strengthened language on adolescent SRHR and unsafe abortion
- **ICPD+20 (2014)**: UN General Assembly framework for review; 176 countries participated in regional reviews; reaffirmed Cairo agenda
- **Nairobi Summit (ICPD25, 2019)**: Co-convened by UNFPA, Kenya, and Denmark; over 1,300 commitments from 170+ countries and stakeholders
- **Nairobi commitment categories**: Zero preventable maternal deaths, zero unmet need for FP, zero GBV and harmful practices (aligned with UNFPA's three transformative results)
- **Financing commitment (original)**: The PoA estimated that implementing its provisions would cost approximately USD 17 billion annually by 2000 and USD 21.7 billion by 2015 — targets that were never fully met

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### Before Cairo: The Population Control Era

The ICPD's significance can only be understood against the backdrop of what came before. From the 1950s through the early 1990s, international population policy was dominated by demographic concern — anxiety that rapid population growth in developing countries would overwhelm economic development, exhaust natural resources, and cause political instability.

This concern drove significant investment in family planning programmes, much of it from the United States (through USAID), the World Bank, and UNFPA. Some of these programmes achieved impressive results (Bangladesh, Thailand, Indonesia); others involved coercion or semi-coercion (India's Emergency-era sterilisation campaign in 1975–76, China's one-child policy from 1980, Peru's sterilisation campaign in the 1990s).

The women's health movement, feminist organisations, and reproductive rights advocates increasingly challenged the demographic paradigm:
- Family planning programmes were designed to reduce fertility, not to serve women's health needs or autonomy
- Methods were promoted based on demographic effectiveness (long-acting, provider-controlled) rather than women's preferences
- Quality of care was often poor — side effects were dismissed, informed consent was incomplete, method choice was limited
- Men's roles in reproductive health were largely ignored
- Adolescents' needs were invisible

### The Cairo Negotiation

The ICPD negotiation brought together these critiques with a broader rethinking of population policy. Key elements of the negotiation:

**The women's caucus**: A coalition of women's rights organisations (International Women's Health Coalition, DAWN, WEDO, and others) played an unprecedented role in shaping the PoA text, working directly with government delegations.

**The South-South dimension**: Developing country governments — particularly from Latin America, sub-Saharan Africa, and South and Southeast Asia — negotiated as active participants, not just recipients of donor-designed programmes.

**The Vatican opposition**: The Holy See mounted a vigorous campaign against the PoA's references to "reproductive rights," abortion, adolescent sexual health, and non-traditional family forms. The Vatican built alliances with conservative Catholic governments (Honduras, El Salvador, Argentina under Menem) and some OIC states. The final text represents a compromise: reproductive rights are affirmed but abortion is treated cautiously.

## DETAIL

### What the Programme of Action Contains

The PoA's 16 chapters cover a comprehensive agenda:

**Chapter 7: Reproductive Rights and Reproductive Health** — The heart of the document. Defines reproductive health as "a state of complete physical, mental and social well-being in all matters relating to the reproductive system"; defines reproductive rights as based on "the basic right of all couples and individuals to decide freely and responsibly the number, spacing and timing of their children and to have the information and means to do so." Paragraph 7.2 is the foundational definition that has shaped global SRHR policy ever since.

**Chapter 8: Health, Morbidity and Mortality** — Addresses maternal health, HIV/AIDS (reflecting the early 1990s epidemic), and other health issues.

**Chapter 4: Gender Equality, Equity and Empowerment of Women** — Establishes gender equality as a prerequisite for population and development goals. Calls for elimination of discrimination, violence against women, and harmful practices.

**Chapter 7 (continued) on Abortion**: Paragraph 8.25 states that "in no case should abortion be promoted as a method of family planning" but that "where abortion is not against the law, such abortion should be safe." This formulation was a political compromise that has been cited by both sides of the abortion debate.

**Chapter 6: Population Growth and Structure** — Addresses ageing, migration, urbanisation, and indigenous peoples.

**Chapter 11: Population, Development and Education** — Calls for universal education, including "population and health education."

### The ICPD Review Processes

**ICPD+5 (1999)**: The five-year review at a UN General Assembly Special Session (UNGASS) produced "Key Actions" that strengthened language on adolescent SRHR, unsafe abortion, and HIV prevention. This was significant because it moved the international consensus incrementally forward from the carefully negotiated 1994 text.

**ICPD+10 (2004) and ICPD+15 (2009)**: Marked by expert reviews rather than intergovernmental negotiation, these reviews assessed progress and identified gaps. A consistent finding was that the ICPD agenda had been "adopted in principle but underfunded in practice."

**ICPD Beyond 2014 (2012–2014)**: A major review process including a global survey of 176 countries, regional conferences, and expert analysis. The resulting report documented both achievements and shortfalls:
- Contraceptive use had increased globally but unmet need persisted, particularly in SSA
- Maternal mortality had declined by approximately 45% since 1990 but was far from the MDG target
- GBV remained widespread with inadequate legal and programmatic responses
- Adolescent SRHR remained neglected in many countries

**Nairobi Summit (ICPD25, 2019)**: Co-convened by UNFPA, Kenya, and Denmark in November 2019 — 25 years after Cairo. The summit generated over 1,300 specific commitments from governments, civil society, private sector, and international organisations. The commitments were framed around UNFPA's three zeros. UNFPA committed to specific acceleration targets.

### What Has Been Achieved

1. **Conceptual transformation**: "Reproductive health" and "reproductive rights" are now universally used terms in global health policy. The paradigm shift from population targets to individual rights is entrenched in UN system programming.
2. **Fertility decline without coercion**: Global TFR declined from approximately 3.0 (1994) to 2.3 (2024) through voluntary family planning, education, and economic development — not through demographic targets.
3. **Maternal health investment**: The ICPD catalysed global investment in maternal health that contributed to significant mortality reductions.
4. **Legal reform**: Dozens of countries have reformed reproductive health laws, established GBV legislation, raised marriage ages, and enacted FGM prohibitions since 1994.
5. **Institutional change**: UNFPA's transformation from a "population fund" to a "sexual and reproductive health and rights" agency reflects the ICPD's conceptual impact.

### What Remains Unfulfilled

1. **Universal access**: The PoA's goal of universal access to reproductive health by 2015 was not achieved; over 200 million women still have unmet need for modern contraception.
2. **Maternal mortality**: The 2015 target (75% reduction) was not met; the 2030 SDG target (MMR below 70/100,000) is not on track.
3. **Financing**: The original financial estimates (USD 17–22 billion annually) were never fully mobilised; actual investment has been higher in nominal terms but insufficient relative to need.
4. **Abortion**: The PoA's cautious language on abortion has not prevented the issue from remaining the most contested area of SRHR policy globally.
5. **Adolescent SRHR**: Despite 30 years of commitments, adolescent access to SRHR services and CSE remains limited in many countries.

## CURRENT STATUS

- The ICPD Programme of Action remains the foundational normative document for UNFPA's mandate and global SRHR policy
- Nairobi Summit commitments are being tracked through UNFPA's commitment monitoring platform; compliance is uneven
- The ICPD paradigm is periodically challenged by conservative political movements that seek to revert to family-centred or pro-natalist frameworks
- UNFPA's 2026–2029 Strategic Plan will continue to reference the ICPD as its mandate foundation
- The post-2030 development framework discussions will need to address whether and how the ICPD agenda is incorporated beyond the SDGs
