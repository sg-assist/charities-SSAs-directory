## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Menstrual health has emerged as a recognised component of sexual and reproductive health, moving from a taboo subject to a policy priority over the past decade. Approximately 1.8 billion people menstruate globally, yet millions lack access to affordable menstrual products, adequate water and sanitation, accurate information, and supportive social environments. The consequences span health (infections from unhygienic practices), education (absenteeism), economic participation (lost work days), and dignity.

UNFPA has integrated menstrual health into its SRHR programming, particularly through: provision of menstrual products in humanitarian settings (dignity kits), menstrual health education within CSE, advocacy against menstrual stigma and harmful practices (menstrual seclusion/chhaupadi in Nepal), and support for policy reform (product taxation, school WASH facilities). The organisation recognises menstrual health as a gender equality, health, and human rights issue that intersects with nearly every aspect of its mandate.

The evidence base is growing but still has significant gaps. The impact of menstrual product provision on school attendance has been widely claimed but inconsistently demonstrated in rigorous studies. Product innovation (menstrual cups, reusable pads) shows promise for affordability and environmental sustainability, but uptake depends on cultural acceptability and product availability. Policy changes (removing taxes on menstrual products, mandating WASH facilities in schools) are expanding in many countries.

## KEY FACTS

- **Menstruating population**: Approximately 1.8 billion people menstruate at any given time globally
- **Period poverty**: An estimated 500 million people lack access to adequate menstrual products and/or facilities
- **School attendance**: Studies report 5–20% of school days missed due to menstruation in some SSA and South Asian contexts; rigorous evidence is mixed
- **Chhaupadi (Nepal)**: Practice of menstrual seclusion (banishing menstruating women to huts); illegal since 2017 but persists
- **Product taxation**: As of 2024, approximately 30+ countries have reduced or eliminated tax on menstrual products ("tampon tax"); many countries retain it
- **Product innovation**: Menstrual cups (reusable for up to 10 years; cost-effective at approximately USD 1–3 per year vs. USD 10–30 for disposable products); reusable cloth pads
- **Environmental impact**: Estimated 12 billion disposable pads/tampons discarded annually; environmental concern driving interest in reusable products
- **WHO/UNICEF JMP**: Joint Monitoring Programme now includes menstrual health indicators in WASH monitoring
- **UNFPA dignity kits**: Distributed in humanitarian settings; contain menstrual products, soap, underwear, and other hygiene items
- **Workplace policies**: Menstrual leave policies exist in Japan, South Korea, Indonesia, Spain, and a few other countries; debated elsewhere
- **Menstrual health education**: Should be included in CSE and integrated into school health programmes; coverage is uneven

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### From Taboo to Policy Priority

Menstrual health was virtually absent from international health and development policy until the 2010s. The topic was considered too intimate for public discourse, and the silence reinforced stigma. Several developments brought menstrual health into the mainstream:

- **WASH sector recognition**: The water, sanitation and hygiene community recognised that inadequate menstrual hygiene management was a significant barrier to girls' education and women's economic participation
- **Civil society advocacy**: Organisations like WASH United, Days for Girls, and local movements in India, Kenya, and elsewhere mobilised around menstrual equity
- **Media attention**: Documentaries, social media campaigns (#MenstruationMatters), and the 2018 Academy Award for "Period. End of Sentence." brought global attention
- **Policy responses**: India eliminated its tax on menstrual products (2018); Scotland became the first country to provide free products in all public places (2022); Kenya pioneered free products in schools

### Menstrual Health as SRHR

Menstrual health is intrinsically linked to SRHR:
- Menarche (first menstruation) marks the beginning of reproductive capacity and is often when girls first need SRHR information
- Menstrual disorders (dysmenorrhoea, endometriosis, PCOS) affect reproductive health and quality of life
- Contraceptive methods affect menstruation (hormonal methods may cause irregular bleeding, amenorrhoea, or heavy bleeding — all of which influence method choice and continuation)
- Menopause marks the end of menstruation and brings its own health needs
- Menstrual stigma is a manifestation of gender inequality

## DETAIL

### UNFPA's Menstrual Health Work

**Humanitarian response**: UNFPA distributes dignity kits containing menstrual products (disposable pads, reusable pads, underwear, soap) to women and girls in humanitarian settings. This is one of UNFPA's most visible and immediate contributions to displaced women's dignity and health. However, dignity kits are a temporary measure — sustainable menstrual health requires functioning markets, affordable products, and adequate WASH facilities.

**Education**: UNFPA integrates menstrual health into comprehensive sexuality education, ensuring that girls (and boys) receive accurate information about menstruation. This includes: what menstruation is (biological process), how to manage it hygienically, what is normal vs. when to seek health care, and challenging stigma and myths.

**Advocacy**: UNFPA advocates for: elimination of menstrual product taxes; school WASH standards that include menstrual-friendly toilets (private, with water, disposal facilities); workplace policies accommodating menstruation; and ending harmful practices like menstrual seclusion.

**Product innovation support**: UNFPA has engaged with menstrual cup programmes and reusable pad initiatives, though this is not a primary procurement focus in the way contraceptives are.

### Evidence Gaps

**School attendance**: The claim that providing menstrual products increases girls' school attendance has been widely made but inconsistently supported by rigorous evidence. Several RCTs (Kenya, Nepal, Uganda) have found modest or no significant effects of product provision alone on attendance. The barriers to attendance during menstruation appear to be multi-faceted — inadequate school toilets, pain, stigma, and teacher attitudes — rather than solely product access.

**Health outcomes**: The health effects of different menstrual management practices (disposable pads vs. cloth vs. cups vs. nothing) are not well-established. A 2019 Lancet Public Health systematic review of menstrual cups found them safe and effective but noted limited evidence from low-income settings.

**Economic impact**: Claims about the economic cost of menstruation (lost work days, reduced productivity) are common in advocacy but poorly quantified. Rigorous economic analyses are needed.

## CURRENT STATUS

- Menstrual health is now a recognised component of SRHR and WASH policy, though programming and financing remain limited
- Product innovation (cups, reusable pads) is expanding but market access and cultural acceptability vary
- Policy reform on taxation, school WASH, and product provision is advancing in many countries
- UNFPA continues to integrate menstrual health into humanitarian response and CSE
- Evidence gaps on the impact of menstrual health interventions on education and economic outcomes need addressing
