## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Singapore's philanthropic landscape for maternal, reproductive, and children's health is dominated by a small number of large family foundations and government-linked entities, supplemented by a broader ecosystem of intermediaries, civil society organisations, and an emerging family office layer. As of FY2024, total tracked philanthropic disbursements across 126 organisations reached S$419 million, with health as the single largest recipient sector.

This document identifies and categorises the Singapore-based foundations most relevant to UNFPA's mandate, profiles their funding priorities, and provides a four-tier directory structured by alignment with maternal, reproductive, and children's health (MRCH) programming. It is intended as a standing reference for LKYSPP programme teams engaged in partnership and resource mobilisation for UNFPA.

Four foundations — Temasek Foundation, Tanoto Foundation, Quantedge Advancement Initiative, and DBS Foundation — have documented, active commitments to MRCH at scale and are classified as Tier 1 direct funders. A further five foundations give substantially to health but without exclusive MRCH focus and form Tier 2. Tier 3 covers ecosystem enablers and philanthropic intermediaries through which indirect engagement is possible. Tier 4 covers civil society and operational partners relevant to UNFPA programming.

## REGULATORY ENVIRONMENT

### Charities Act and Commissioner of Charities (COC)

All registered charities in Singapore must satisfy the Commissioner of Charities that they operate for exclusively charitable purposes and for public benefit. The COC, located within the Ministry of Culture, Community and Youth (MCCY), registers and regulates all charities. Foundations giving to health-sector charities in Singapore are typically dealing with COC-registered counterparties.

### Institutions of a Public Character (IPCs)

IPC status is granted by the COC to charities that meet additional governance and accountability standards. Donations to IPCs by individuals and corporations attract a 250% tax deduction under the Income Tax Act. The IPC system is the primary mechanism through which major foundations structure their tax-efficient giving in Singapore. Health IPCs are governed as a sub-category under the MOH sector.

### MOH as Sector Administrator

The Ministry of Health acts as the Sector Administrator for health charities in Singapore. Health charities seeking IPC status must obtain MOH's endorsement before the COC will grant or renew IPC status. This arrangement makes MOH a gatekeeper for donor relationships with health-sector IPCs, including KKH Charity Fund, Singapore Children's Society, and others in Tier 4 of this directory.

### Overseas Humanitarian Donations (2025–2028 Pilot)

Singapore Budget 2025 introduced a 100% tax deduction (not the usual 250% IPC rate, but still deductible) for qualifying overseas humanitarian donations made through approved channels. This incentive, active from 2025 to 2028, is relevant for foundations with regional programming interests in maternal and child health — including UNFPA's Southeast Asian programme countries. The pilot creates a narrow window for engaging Singapore funders on cross-border MRCH initiatives.

### Family Offices and the Variable Capital Company (VCC) Framework

Singapore's approximately 1,400 single-family offices manage wealth under the 13O and 13U fund tax incentive schemes. Many operate philanthropic vehicles alongside investment mandates. The Variable Capital Company (VCC) framework, introduced in 2020, enables SFOs to structure pooled philanthropic giving efficiently. UNFPA's engagement with this layer is primarily through intermediary networks (AVPN, PAA) rather than direct bilateral outreach.

**Notes on Tier 1 engagement:**
- Temasek Foundation is the anchor funder in this tier. Its Richard Magnus Endowment is the single largest announced commitment to maternal and child health in Singapore's philanthropic sector. The Foundation's Public Health Innovations stream offers a formal entry point for UNFPA-linked proposals.
- Tanoto Foundation's MRF was specifically designed to fill research gaps in maternal fertility and paediatric health. With KKH as an institutional partner, co-engagement alongside KKH is the recommended approach.
- Quantedge operates with a lower public profile but participates in PAA's Health for Human Potential Community, which is the primary convening vehicle for pooled MRCH giving in Southeast Asia.
- DBS Foundation's KidSTART programme marks a direct entry into prenatal nutrition, an area of UNFPA mandate. DBS's scale and CSR infrastructure make it a viable partner for structured programmes rather than pure grants.

### Tier 2 — Significant Health Givers

These foundations make substantial philanthropic disbursements to health in Singapore but do not have explicit MRCH mandates. Their health giving tends to be directed at hospitals, medical research broadly, or disease-specific programmes. Engagement is viable through health-system partnerships and co-funding of specific MRCH components.

| Foundation | 2024 Disbursement | Health Focus | MRCH Relevance |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Lee Foundation** | S$33.2M | S$50M to SingHealth Duke-NUS; medical bursaries | Indirect — SingHealth partnership could include MRCH components |
| **Moh Family Foundation** | S$22.4M | 75.1% of disbursements to health sector | High concentration in health; no confirmed MRCH specificity |
| **Low Tuck Kwong Foundation** | S$23.2M (S$127.6M in 2023) | Hospitals in Singapore and regional (Indonesia, others) | Regional scope creates alignment with UNFPA SE Asia priorities |
| **Shaw Foundation** | ~S$5M | National Kidney Foundation (largest recipient); paediatric research | Paediatric research component is relevant |
| **Ngee Ann Kongsi** | S$40.6M | S$40M to SingHealth; S$12.5M TCM at NTU | SingHealth scale creates indirect MRCH pathway |

**Notes on Tier 2 engagement:**
- The Lee Foundation's S$13.6B asset base makes it the largest private foundation in Singapore by total assets. Its giving tends to be anchor or endowment-style rather than programme grants, and it does not maintain a public grant application process.
- Moh Family Foundation's 75.1% concentration in health is unusually high for a generalist foundation and signals a deep institutional commitment; however, it operates with limited public disclosure. Engagement is best pursued through existing hospital relationships.
- Ngee Ann Kongsi's S$40M SingHealth donation mirrors the Lee Foundation's approach of large, infrequent anchor gifts. Its TCM investment at NTU is relevant to reproductive and maternal health from a traditional medicine integration angle.

### Tier 3 — Ecosystem Enablers and Philanthropic Intermediaries

These entities do not primarily give direct grants to MRCH programmes but serve critical infrastructure roles: convening funders, providing donor-advised fund (DAF) infrastructure, facilitating regional co-investment, or coordinating pooled mechanisms through which MRCH funding flows.

| Entity | Role | Scale | UNFPA Relevance |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Community Foundation of Singapore (CFS)** | Cause-neutral DAF sponsor; gateway for international organisations receiving Singapore philanthropic capital | S$28.4M disbursed (2024); S$353M raised lifetime | Enables Singapore funders to give to UNFPA or UNFPA-linked charities with tax efficiency |
| **Lien Foundation** | Eldercare and palliative care (exiting early childhood); systems-change funder | S$24.4M disbursed (2024) | Relevant for sexual and reproductive health in ageing demographics; diminishing early childhood relevance |
| **AVPN** | Network of 700+ social investors; operates MNCHN pooled fund for SE Asia | US$1M pooled fund, maternal/newborn/child/adolescent health | Direct — AVPN MNCHN fund is active MRCH vehicle in UNFPA geographies |
| **Philanthropy Asia Alliance (PAA)** | Temasek-linked convener of Asia philanthropists; Health for Human Potential Community | US$100M target by 2030 (launched May 2025) | High — PAA's Health Community is the region's most significant pooled MRCH vehicle |

**Notes on Tier 3 engagement:**
- PAA's Health for Human Potential Community, launched May 2025 with a US$100M target, is the highest-priority Tier 3 engagement. The four co-anchoring foundations (Temasek Foundation, Tanoto Foundation, Quantedge, and the Gates Foundation) overlap with UNFPA's Tier 1 targets. UNFPA's institutional presence in Singapore creates a natural participation case.
- AVPN's MNCHN pooled fund is smaller but immediately operational. AVPN's 700-member network includes family offices, corporate foundations, and development finance institutions across Asia, making it a leverage point for UNFPA's SE Asia programming.
- CFS engagement is largely administrative (structuring tax-efficient giving channels) but is important for any major Singapore-based donor who wishes to support UNFPA's international work through a local entity.

## IMPLICATIONS FOR LKYSPP TEAM

1. **Prioritise PAA engagement immediately.** The Health for Human Potential Community, launched May 2025 with a US$100M target, is the largest single MRCH-aligned pooled fund in Asia. UNFPA's participation as a technical or programme partner would position it alongside Gates, Temasek, Tanoto, and Quantedge in a convening structure purpose-built for the mandate.

2. **Use KKH as a bridge institution.** KKH receives funding from Temasek Foundation, Tanoto Foundation, and the Shaw Foundation, and operates international programmes (WAH Foundation) directly aligned with UNFPA's midwifery and maternal health priorities. An institutional partnership with KKH provides a credible entry point to three Tier 1/2 funders simultaneously.

3. **Leverage AVPN's MNCHN infrastructure.** AVPN's existing pooled fund for maternal, newborn, child, and adolescent health in SE Asia is operationally active and does not require the creation of new mechanisms. UNFPA can participate as a technical partner or programmatic co-implementer with immediate effect.

4. **Monitor the overseas humanitarian donation pilot (2025–2028).** The 100% tax deduction for qualifying overseas humanitarian donations creates a time-limited window to attract Singapore foundations and family offices to UNFPA's international programming. This incentive expires in 2028 and should inform the partnership-building timeline.

5. **Build a CFS pathway for family office capital.** Singapore's ~1,400 family offices represent a large, diffuse pool of philanthropic capital. CFS is the most accessible gateway for structuring Singapore-family-office donations to international causes with tax efficiency. Establishing a CFS relationship allows UNFPA to receive Singapore philanthropic capital without requiring each donor to set up independent giving structures.

6. **Do not under-invest in civil society relationships.** AWARE, KidSTART, and the WAH Foundation represent operational and advocacy partners whose existing presence in MRCH creates implementation leverage. These are not primarily funding relationships but are essential for programme credibility and government relations in Singapore.

## LANDSCAPE CONTEXT

### Singapore's Philanthropic Scale in Regional Perspective

With a population of approximately 5.9 million, Singapore generates philanthropic disbursements that are exceptional relative to its size. The S$419 million recorded by Soristic in FY2024 represents roughly S$71 per capita in organised philanthropic giving — a figure that significantly exceeds comparable per-capita measures for most of Southeast Asia and rivals developed-country benchmarks.

This density of philanthropic capital is driven by a combination of exceptional wealth concentration (Singapore has one of the highest numbers of millionaires and billionaires per capita globally), a favourable tax environment for charitable giving (250% IPC deduction), and a civic culture that associates philanthropy with social responsibility among business elites. The Singapore government actively cultivates this culture through public recognition mechanisms including the President's Volunteerism and Philanthropy Award and the National Volunteer and Philanthropy Centre (NVPC).

The ~1,400 single-family offices registered in Singapore as of 2023 represent a further structural dimension: Singapore serves not only as the domicile for Singapore-based family wealth but as the preferred wealth management hub for families from Indonesia, China, India, Malaysia, and other parts of Asia. This means that Singapore's philanthropic capital is substantially more than Singapore-origin wealth; it includes the growing philanthropic programmes of Asian families who have chosen Singapore as their financial base.

### Singapore's Total Fertility Rate and MRCH Urgency

A critical contextual factor for MRCH philanthropy in Singapore is the city-state's demographic trajectory. Singapore's total fertility rate (TFR) reached a historic low of approximately 0.97 in 2023, well below the replacement level of 2.1 and among the lowest nationally recorded TFRs in the world. The government has responded with a suite of Baby Bonus incentives, parental leave enhancements, and funded fertility treatment — but philanthropic foundations have also aligned with this national priority.

The Richard Magnus Endowment (Temasek Foundation, 2023), the Tanoto Foundation MRF (2023), and the PAA Health for Human Potential Community (2025) all reflect, in part, a philanthropic response to Singapore's fertility and maternal health concerns. For UNFPA, this demographic context provides a powerful engagement frame: Singapore's MRCH philanthropy surge is not incidental but is driven by a nationally recognised crisis that creates sustained institutional commitment to MRCH outcomes.

UNFPA's global evidence base on fertility trends, reproductive health system design, and the relationship between women's empowerment and fertility rates positions it as a uniquely relevant technical partner in this context.

### Health Philanthropy's Structural Advantages in Singapore

Several structural features of Singapore's regulatory environment make health philanthropy particularly efficient:

The IPC system ensures that major health charities — including KKH Charity Fund, Singapore Children's Society, and AWARE — can issue 250% tax-deductible receipts, making health the most tax-efficient sector for Singapore philanthropists. This structural tax advantage contributes to health's position as the leading recipient sector in Singapore philanthropy.

MOH's role as Sector Administrator creates a government validation function: MOH's endorsement of health IPC applications is a quality signal that foundations and donors rely on when directing capital to health charities. This means that UNFPA's engagement with MOH-endorsed health charities (KKH, AWARE, Singapore Children's Society) carries implicit MOH credibility.

The 2025–2028 overseas humanitarian donation pilot creates a time-limited but significant window for international organisations to attract Singapore philanthropic capital to cross-border programmes. UNFPA should treat the 2025–2028 period as a priority resource mobilisation window.
