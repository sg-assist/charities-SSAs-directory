## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Disaster risk reduction (DRR) — the systematic practice of analysing and reducing the causal factors of disasters — intersects directly with SRHR. Natural disasters (earthquakes, floods, cyclones, droughts) and slow-onset climate impacts disrupt health systems, displace populations, and create conditions that increase maternal mortality, GBV, and unmet need for family planning. The Sendai Framework for Disaster Risk Reduction (2015–2030) provides the international policy architecture for DRR, and UNFPA's role is ensuring that SRHR is integrated into DRR planning, preparedness, and response.

UNFPA's DRR-SRHR work includes: pre-positioning reproductive health kits in disaster-prone countries; integrating SRHR into national disaster preparedness plans; training emergency health workers in the MISP; building climate-resilient health facility infrastructure; and supporting community-based disaster preparedness that includes SRHR components. The evidence shows that SRHR services are among the first disrupted and last restored during disasters — a pattern that can be broken through systematic preparedness.

---

## KEY FACTS

- **Sendai Framework (2015–2030)**: Global DRR framework; 7 targets including reduced disaster mortality and economic loss; health is a cross-cutting theme
- **Disaster-affected populations**: Over 200 million people affected by natural disasters annually (EM-DAT)
- **SRHR disruption pattern**: Maternal health, FP, and GBV services are consistently among the first disrupted and last restored during disasters
- **Pre-positioning**: UNFPA pre-positions reproductive health kits in approximately 50 high-risk countries
- **Climate-related disasters**: Increasing in frequency and intensity; 80% of disaster-related displacement is climate-related
- **Women's vulnerability**: Women face heightened risks during disasters — GBV increases, maternal care is disrupted, and recovery resources are allocated inequitably
- **Contingency planning**: UNFPA participates in country-level inter-agency contingency planning processes; SRHR should be included but often is not
- **Build back better**: Post-disaster recovery is an opportunity to build more resilient health systems including SRHR capacity

---

## DETAIL

UNFPA's DRR-SRHR approach spans the full disaster cycle: **preparedness** (pre-positioning, training, contingency planning, community awareness), **response** (MISP implementation, GBV services, RH kit distribution), and **recovery** (health system rebuilding, "build back better" including improved SRHR facilities).

Experience from major disasters demonstrates the pattern: after Cyclone Nargis (Myanmar, 2008), maternal mortality spiked due to destroyed facilities and disrupted referral systems. After the Nepal earthquake (2015), UNFPA rapidly deployed mobile SRHR clinics but many remote areas went without care for weeks. After Cyclone Idai (Mozambique, 2019), an estimated 250,000 women and girls of reproductive age needed SRHR services in affected areas.

Integration of SRHR into national DRR plans remains inconsistent — many countries' disaster preparedness plans do not specifically address reproductive health, GBV, or maternal care.
