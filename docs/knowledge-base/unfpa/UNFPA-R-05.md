## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The funding landscape for sexual and reproductive health and rights (SRHR) is undergoing its most severe disruption in decades. The US withdrawal from UNFPA funding — approximately $335 million in terminated grants plus the permanent Kemp-Kasten invocation — coincides with broader contractions in official development assistance (ODA) and a global humanitarian system that received only 25% of what it requested in 2025. This document maps the current funding crisis, identifies where new capital is emerging, and equips UNFPA staff with the data needed for conversations with potential alternative funding partners.

The core message for partnership conversations: UNFPA's funding diversification is not optional — it is existential. The agencies and instruments that can fill the gap are identifiable, and the urgency creates a window for innovative partnership structures that would have been harder to catalyse in stable funding environments.

---

## KEY FACTS: UNFPA FINANCIAL POSITION

- **Total UNFPA revenue (2024)**: $1,635 million (a historic high before the 2025 disruption)
  - Core (unrestricted): $397 million (24% of total — below the UN Funding Compact's 30% target)
  - Non-core (earmarked): $1,238 million (76%)
- **Top core donors (2024)**: Norway ($56.4M), Germany ($46.4M), Sweden ($45.4M), Netherlands ($37.5M), Denmark ($32.7M), US ($30.5M core only)
- **US total contribution (2023)**: $194.4 million ($30.6M core + $163.8M project funding) — the single largest government contributor
- **Number of core donors**: Declined from 95 (2023) to 90 (2024) to 47 (as of May 2025)
- **UNFPA 2026 humanitarian appeal**: $1 billion across 41 countries; targeting 34 million women, girls, and young people

## THE US FUNDING CUT: TIMELINE AND IMPACT

### What Happened

| Date | Event |
|---|---|
| January 2025 | Trump executive order directing halt to funding for organisations supporting coercive practices; 90-day foreign aid freeze |
| February 2025 | 48 UNFPA grants terminated — approximately $377 million covering humanitarian programmes in 25+ countries |
| March 2025 | 7 projects temporarily reinstated ($89 million — Afghanistan, Haiti, Madagascar, Nigeria, Sudan, Syria, Ukraine) |
| April 2025 | Afghanistan and Syria projects re-terminated; 43 projects ($330 million) remain cut |
| May 2025 | Secretary Rubio invokes Kemp-Kasten Amendment — permanent ban on all future US funding to UNFPA |
| July 2025 | Congress approves $500 million rescission of global health/family planning funds |
| January 2026 | US announces intention to formally withdraw from UNFPA; FY2026 budget includes zero UNFPA funding |

### Human Impact

- **1,000+ UNFPA-supported health facilities and mobile health teams** shut down or near closure
- **250+ safe spaces** for GBV survivors forced to close
- UNFPA can fund only **47% of the 3,521 midwives** intended for crisis settings
- **1,700 of 3,100 female health workers** in Afghanistan lost employment
- Projected consequences: 47.6 million women/couples denied contraceptive care; 17.1 million unintended pregnancies; 34,000 preventable maternal deaths annually

### What This Means for Partnership Conversations

The US withdrawal is the third Kemp-Kasten invocation against UNFPA (also 2002–2008, 2017–2020). After the first Trump-term cuts, the EU, Germany, Sweden, Norway, and the Netherlands filled much of the gap. However, the current environment is materially worse:
- UK and Netherlands are also cutting development budgets
- The overall UN humanitarian system is critically underfunded
- The UN Secretary-General proposed merging UNFPA and UN Women (September 2025)
- UNFPA's 2025 humanitarian appeal was only **30% funded**

**Implication for funders**: UNFPA's track record of delivering results with limited resources is well-documented. The current crisis creates urgency for non-traditional funding sources — family offices, DFIs, philanthropic foundations, and blended finance vehicles — to fill gaps that traditional bilateral donors cannot.

## GLOBAL ODA TRENDS

### Overall Trajectory

- OECD projects a **9–17% drop in ODA** in 2025 across major donor countries
- Global SRHR aid dropped **27% in 2023 vs. 2022** (DSW, Donors Delivering for SRHR 2025)
- Donor government funding for family planning: **$1.36 billion in 2024**, down 8% from $1.47 billion in 2023 — one of the lowest levels since the 2012 London Family Planning Summit
- Five major donors decreased family planning funding in 2024: Canada, Germany, Netherlands, Sweden, UK
- USAID's effective dissolution in early 2025 affected programmes reaching an estimated 47.6 million people

### The SDG Financing Gap

- Annual SDG financing gap for developing countries: **$3.8–4.3 trillion** (UN FSDR 2024; UNDP cites $4.2 trillion)
- This is a significant upward revision from the previously cited $2.5 trillion (UNCTAD, 2015)
- Only **16% of SDG targets** are on track globally; a third have regressed below 2015 levels
- Asia faces an **$815 billion annual climate finance gap** (Philanthropy Asia Alliance estimate)

## MULTILATERAL HEALTH FUNDING: COMPARATIVE BENCHMARKS

For partnership conversations, UNFPA staff should understand where UNFPA sits relative to comparable multilateral health mechanisms:

### GAVI (the Vaccine Alliance)

- **1.2 billion children** immunised since 2000; 20.6 million deaths prevented
- **72 million children** vaccinated in 2024 alone — a single-year record
- Gavi 5.0 (2021–2025): $21.3 billion in donor contributions and pledges
- Gavi 6.0 (2026–2030) target: $11.9 billion; **$9 billion+ secured** at June 2025 pledging summit
- Economic returns: **$21 saved for every $1 spent** on immunisation
- US has withdrawn future contributions; UK reduced pledge from £1.6B to £1.25B

### Global Fund to Fight AIDS, TB, and Malaria

- **$69.9 billion** disbursed since 2002 (up from the previously cited $50 billion)
- **70 million lives saved**; disease mortality reduced by 61%
- 8th Replenishment (2026–2028): **$12.64 billion** pledged vs. $18 billion target — significant shortfall
- US funding cuts of **$1.4 billion** affecting 100+ countries in current cycle
- Combined HIV/TB/malaria mortality rate reduced by **61%** since 2002

### Global Financing Facility (GFF)

- SRHR investment cases now cover 97% of partner countries
- 187 million unintended pregnancies averted cumulatively
- Explicitly targets RMNCAH (reproductive, maternal, newborn, child, adolescent health)

### Talking Point for Funders

UNFPA operates at a fraction of the cost of GAVI and the Global Fund but delivers comparable impact density per dollar in its mandate areas. UNFPA's $1.6 billion annual budget reaches communities in 150+ countries; by comparison, GAVI has mobilised $21.3 billion for a single intervention class (vaccines). The cost-effectiveness argument for UNFPA is powerful — each dollar invested prevents more unintended pregnancies, maternal deaths, and GBV incidents than virtually any other global health mechanism.

## WHERE NEW CAPITAL IS EMERGING

### 1. Asian Philanthropy and Impact Investing

- Global impact investing market: **$1.57 trillion** (2024), growing rapidly
- Asia became the **world's largest recipient of blended finance** in 2024: $6.3 billion
- Each $1 of philanthropic capital can unlock **$4–30** in SDG-focused investments
- 88–89% of Asia-focused impact investors report financial returns meeting or exceeding expectations
- Singapore family offices grew from 400 (2020) to **2,000+** (end-2024) — see UNFPA-R-06

### 2. Climate Finance

- Total blended finance cumulative: **$213 billion** across 1,123 deals (Convergence 2025)
- Climate blended finance in 2024: **$15.5 billion across 84 deals**
- East Asia & Pacific climate blended finance surged **77%** ($1.3B to $5B) in 2023–2024
- Green Climate Fund portfolio: **$19.3 billion** across 336 projects — but SRHR is almost entirely absent (see UNFPA-R-07)

### 3. Gender Lens Investing

- Global GLI assets: **$67.5–122 billion** (depending on measurement)
- Asia is the **second-largest GLI region**: $1.4 billion across 47 vehicles
- Gender-diverse funds in emerging markets show **20% higher net IRR**
- Closing gender gaps could add **$4.5 trillion** to Asia-Pacific annual GDP
- Women-owned businesses face a **$1.7 trillion global finance gap**

## HUMANITARIAN LOCALISATION: THE UNFULFILLED PROMISE

- Grand Bargain (2016) target: 25% of humanitarian funding to local/national actors
- Actual direct funding to local actors (2023): **4.5%** (up from 2.3% in 2022, but skewed by two non-GB donors)
- Grand Bargain signatory donors' direct funding to LNAs: **0.6%**
- Country-Based Pooled Funds (CBPFs) to local actors: **30–43%** — a bright spot
- Grand Bargain 3.0 (2023–2026): 71 signatories; only 4 donors reported reaching 25% target
- **Implication for UNFPA**: The localisation gap is both a problem (funding doesn't reach communities) and an opportunity (UNFPA's community-based distribution model is precisely the kind of localised delivery that the Grand Bargain envisions)

## IMPLICATIONS FOR PARTNERSHIP CONVERSATIONS

### The Pitch

UNFPA is in a critical funding moment. The US withdrawal creates a $335 million gap that traditional ODA cannot fully replace. This is an inflection point: organisations that step in now become founding partners of UNFPA's diversified funding model, not just donors filling a hole.

### What Different Funders Care About

| Funder Type | Key Interests | UNFPA Alignment |
|---|---|---|
| Family offices | Impact + returns; ESG alignment; legacy | Gender lens investing; community resilience; measurable outcomes |
| DFIs (ADB, IFC, Temasek) | Near-commercial returns; system strengthening | Health system resilience; blended finance vehicles |
| Philanthropic foundations | Measurable social impact; evidence base | Three transformative results; community-based delivery |
| Climate funds (GCF, AF) | Adaptation; resilience; NDC alignment | Climate-SRHR nexus; health system climate-proofing |
| Corporate foundations | Gender equity; women's empowerment; brand | GBV prevention; maternal health; adolescent programmes |
| Government ODA | SDGs; bilateral relationships; value for money | Country programme model; proven cost-effectiveness |

### Three Conversation Starters

1. **"UNFPA prevented 34,000 maternal deaths annually with US funding. That capacity is now unfunded. What would it take for your organisation to become part of the solution?"**

2. **"SRHR is almost entirely absent from climate finance — despite clear evidence linking climate change to maternal mortality, GBV, and contraceptive disruption. This is the biggest untapped funding opportunity in climate-health."**

3. **"Singapore's family office ecosystem has doubled to 2,000+ in three years. MAS now recognises blended finance at 2x value toward capital deployment requirements. The infrastructure for innovative SRHR financing has never been stronger."**
