## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Guttmacher-Lancet Commission on Sexual and Reproductive Health and Rights, published in May 2018, is the most comprehensive and influential evidence synthesis on SRHR of the past decade. Titled "Accelerate Progress — Sexual and Reproductive Health and Rights for All," the Commission brought together 16 commissioners from diverse backgrounds (researchers, clinicians, advocates, policymakers from multiple regions) to define what comprehensive SRHR means, what it costs, and how to achieve it. Its most significant contribution is the definition of an essential SRHR package comprising nine service categories that the Commission argues should be universally available as part of UHC.

The Commission was convened by the Guttmacher Institute (a US-based research and policy organisation focused on SRHR) and published in The Lancet. It was explicitly designed to influence post-2015 development policy, national health planning, and SRHR financing. UNFPA endorsed the Commission's framework and has used it as a reference point for programme design, advocacy, and the development of strategic plans.

The Commission's essential package goes beyond what many governments currently include in their SRHR programmes. By including safe abortion care, comprehensive sexuality education, services for sexual function and satisfaction, and counselling for infertility — alongside the less contested services of contraception, maternal care, and STI treatment — the Commission deliberately pushed the boundaries of the SRHR definition. This has been both its strength (providing the most complete evidence-based SRHR framework) and its limitation (creating political opposition from governments and movements that reject some components).

## KEY FACTS

- **Publication**: May 2018, The Lancet
- **Commissioners**: 16, from 10+ countries, chaired by Ann Starrs (Guttmacher) and co-chaired by Alex Ezeh, Alaka Basu, and others
- **Essential SRHR package — 9 service categories**:
  1. Contraceptive counselling and a full range of modern methods
  2. Comprehensive maternal and newborn care
  3. Safe abortion care (to the full extent of the law)
  4. Post-abortion care (regardless of legal status)
  5. Prevention, detection, and treatment of STIs including HIV
  6. Comprehensive sexuality education
  7. Prevention, detection, and management of reproductive cancers (cervical, breast)
  8. Counselling and services for sexual function and satisfaction
  9. Prevention, detection, and management of GBV

- **Costing**: Full essential SRHR package in developing countries: approximately USD 68.8 billion annually; contraception alone: USD 12.6 billion; maternal and newborn care: USD 33.4 billion
- **Current gap**: Approximately USD 30 billion annually between current spending and full package cost in developing countries
- **Impact if fully funded**: Estimated to prevent approximately 76,000 maternal deaths, 1.1 million newborn deaths, 25 million unsafe abortions, and 600,000 unintended pregnancies annually
- **UNFPA endorsement**: UNFPA adopted the Commission's framework as a reference for comprehensive SRHR programming
- **Political controversy**: Inclusion of safe abortion care and CSE provoked opposition from some governments, the Holy See, and conservative advocacy groups

## DETAIL

### The Nine Service Categories in Detail

**1. Contraceptive counselling and methods**: Full method mix (pills, injectables, implants, IUDs, condoms, sterilisation, emergency contraception, fertility awareness); client-centred counselling; removal of barriers for adolescents and unmarried persons; inclusion of new methods as they become available.

**2. Maternal and newborn care**: Comprehensive package from pre-conception through ANC, labour and delivery (skilled attendance, EmONC), and postnatal care; including respectful maternity care, companion of choice, and birth in the position of choice.

**3. Safe abortion care**: "To the full extent of the law" — meaning that whatever abortion services are legally available in a country should be accessible, safe, and high-quality. The Commission also advocated for liberalisation of restrictive abortion laws on public health grounds.

**4. Post-abortion care**: Treatment of complications from all abortions (safe and unsafe), regardless of legal status; FP counselling and provision; compassionate care without judgement.

**5. STI services including HIV**: Screening, diagnosis, treatment, and partner notification for gonorrhoea, chlamydia, syphilis, HPV, and HIV; PrEP for HIV; hepatitis B vaccination.

**6. CSE**: Comprehensive, age-appropriate, evidence-based sexuality education in and out of schools; covering relationships, consent, gender equality, body autonomy, contraception, and STI prevention.

**7. Reproductive cancers**: Cervical cancer screening (HPV testing, VIA) and vaccination (HPV vaccine); breast cancer awareness and screening where feasible; endometrial and ovarian cancer awareness.

**8. Sexual function and satisfaction**: Counselling for sexual dysfunction, education on sexual health as a positive aspect of wellbeing (not only disease prevention), and addressing harmful norms around sexuality. This is the most novel of the nine categories and the most underdeveloped in terms of service delivery infrastructure.

**9. GBV prevention and response**: Clinical management of rape, psychosocial support, referral to legal and social services, primary prevention through gender-transformative programming.

### How the Commission Has Been Used

**UNFPA**: The Commission's framework informs UNFPA's strategic plan, country programme design, and advocacy for SRHR-inclusive UHC.

**WHO**: WHO's essential packages and guidelines reference the Commission's evidence; WHO's self-care guidelines align with the Commission's emphasis on expanded access.

**National policy**: Several countries have used the Commission's framework to review and expand their essential health benefits packages to include comprehensive SRHR.

**Advocacy**: Civil society organisations cite the Commission extensively in national and international advocacy for SRHR rights and financing.

### Critiques and Limitations

**Political backlash**: The inclusion of safe abortion care as an essential service has been used by conservative governments and movements to reject the entire Commission framework.

**Implementation gap**: The package is aspirational — no country currently provides all nine service categories at universal coverage and quality. The gap between what the Commission recommends and what health systems deliver is vast, particularly in LDCs and fragile states.

**Costing assumptions**: The cost estimates rely on modelling assumptions (unit costs, coverage scenarios, health system capacity) that may not hold in the most challenging contexts.

---

## CURRENT STATUS

- The Commission remains the definitive reference for comprehensive SRHR
- Its framework is widely used in global health policy and UNFPA programming
- Updated costing and coverage estimates may be needed to reflect post-COVID realities
- Debates about what constitutes "essential" SRHR continue in national and international forums
