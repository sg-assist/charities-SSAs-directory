## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women (CEDAW), adopted by the UN General Assembly in 1979 and entering into force in 1981, is the most comprehensive international legal instrument for women's rights and provides the binding treaty framework within which reproductive rights are grounded. Unlike the ICPD Programme of Action (a political consensus document), CEDAW creates legally binding obligations for its 189 states parties. CEDAW's provisions on health (Article 12), marriage and family (Article 16), and rural women (Article 14) directly address reproductive health and rights, while the CEDAW Committee's General Recommendations — particularly General Recommendation 24 on women and health (1999) and General Recommendation 35 on gender-based violence (2017) — have significantly expanded the treaty's application to SRHR.

For UNFPA, CEDAW is both a normative foundation and a practical tool. UNFPA's programming is guided by the rights articulated in CEDAW, and the organisation supports states in implementing their CEDAW obligations on reproductive health, GBV, and harmful practices. UNFPA also supports civil society organisations in engaging with the CEDAW Committee's periodic review process — including through "shadow reports" that document gaps between state commitments and practice.

The limitations of CEDAW as a tool for advancing reproductive rights are significant: the United States has signed but not ratified the Convention; several states parties maintain reservations on key articles; enforcement mechanisms are weak (the Committee can issue Concluding Observations but cannot compel compliance); and the Optional Protocol allowing individual complaints has been ratified by only 115 states. Nevertheless, CEDAW remains the most authoritative international legal instrument affirming reproductive rights.

## KEY FACTS

- **Adoption**: 18 December 1979 (GA resolution 34/180); entered into force 3 September 1981
- **States parties**: 189 — the second most widely ratified human rights treaty (after the Convention on the Rights of the Child)
- **Notable non-ratification**: United States (signed 1980, never ratified); Iran, Palau, Somalia, Sudan, Tonga, and the Holy See have neither signed nor ratified
- **Reservations**: Multiple states maintain reservations on Articles 2, 9, 15, 16, and 29 — some reservations are considered incompatible with the object and purpose of the Convention
- **CEDAW Committee**: 23 independent experts elected by states parties; reviews country reports on a periodic cycle (every 4 years in theory, often longer in practice)
- **General Recommendation 24 (1999)**: On women and health — affirms state obligations on reproductive health including access to family planning, safe motherhood, and addressing gender-based barriers to health
- **General Recommendation 35 (2017)**: Updated GR 19 on violence against women — recognises GBV as a form of discrimination and details state obligations on prevention, protection, and prosecution
- **Optional Protocol (1999)**: Allows individual complaints and inquiry procedures; ratified by 115 states parties
- **Reporting cycle**: States parties submit periodic reports; the Committee issues Concluding Observations with specific recommendations; UNFPA supports both state reporting and shadow report preparation
- **Link to SDGs**: CEDAW obligations underpin SDG 5 (Gender Equality) targets; CEDAW Committee Concluding Observations frequently reference SDG indicators

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### CEDAW's Place in the Human Rights Framework

CEDAW is one of nine core international human rights treaties. It is distinctive in focusing specifically on discrimination against women in all fields — political, economic, social, cultural, and civil. The Convention:
- Defines discrimination against women (Article 1)
- Requires states to take all appropriate measures to eliminate discrimination (Article 2)
- Addresses specific areas: political participation, nationality, education, employment, health, economic and social benefits, rural women, legal capacity, marriage and family

### How CEDAW Addresses Reproductive Rights

**Article 12 (Health)**: Requires states parties to "take all appropriate measures to eliminate discrimination against women in the field of health care in order to ensure, on a basis of equality of men and women, access to health care services, including those related to family planning."

**Article 12(2)**: Specifically requires states to ensure "appropriate services in connection with pregnancy, confinement and the post-natal period, granting free services where necessary, as well as adequate nutrition during pregnancy and lactation."

**Article 16 (Marriage and Family)**: Guarantees women the same rights as men in marriage and family relations, including "the same rights to decide freely and responsibly on the number and spacing of their children and to have access to the information, education and means to enable them to exercise these rights."

**Article 14 (Rural Women)**: Requires states to address the particular problems of rural women, including ensuring access to "adequate health care facilities, including information, counselling and services in family planning."

### General Recommendations: Expanding the Framework

The CEDAW Committee's General Recommendations have progressively expanded the Convention's application to reproductive rights:

**GR 24 (1999) — Women and Health**: The most detailed elaboration of CEDAW's reproductive health obligations. Key provisions:
- States must ensure access to a full range of health services, including family planning
- Refusal to provide reproductive health services that only women need is discrimination
- States must address barriers to health access, including cost, distance, lack of informed consent, and harmful traditional practices
- Criminalisation of medical procedures that only women undergo (implicitly, abortion) can violate women's right to health

**GR 35 (2017) — Gender-Based Violence**: Updated the foundational GR 19 (1992). Establishes that GBV is a form of discrimination; details state obligations on due diligence, prevention, protection, prosecution, and reparation. Includes reproductive coercion and forced sterilisation as forms of GBV.

## DETAIL

### CEDAW in Practice: The Reporting and Review Process

The CEDAW Committee reviews states parties' periodic reports (ideally every four years) and issues Concluding Observations with specific recommendations. This process:

1. **State report**: The government submits a report on legislative, judicial, administrative, and other measures taken to implement CEDAW
2. **Shadow reports**: NGOs, women's organisations, and national human rights institutions submit alternative reports documenting gaps
3. **List of Issues**: The Committee sends questions to the state based on its preliminary review
4. **Constructive dialogue**: A two-day session where the Committee questions the state delegation
5. **Concluding Observations**: The Committee's formal recommendations, which may include specific SRHR recommendations

UNFPA supports this process by:
- Providing technical assistance to governments on CEDAW reporting related to SRHR
- Supporting civil society organisations in preparing shadow reports
- Using Concluding Observations to advocate for national policy reform

### Limitations of the CEDAW Framework

**Enforcement weakness**: The Committee's Concluding Observations are not legally binding in the sense of court orders. States can and do ignore them. The Optional Protocol's individual complaint mechanism has produced important jurisprudence but covers only 115 states.

**Reservations**: Some states parties maintain broad reservations that effectively hollow out their obligations. Saudi Arabia, for example, ratified CEDAW with reservations on any provisions inconsistent with Islamic Sharia. The Committee has stated that some reservations are incompatible with the Convention's object and purpose, but there is no mechanism to compel withdrawal.

**US non-ratification**: The United States' failure to ratify CEDAW limits the Convention's universality and reduces US accountability to international reproductive rights norms.

**Political backlash**: In recent years, some states have resisted CEDAW Committee recommendations on SRHR, arguing that reproductive health decisions should be left to national sovereignty and cultural/religious values.

### CEDAW and Abortion

The CEDAW Committee has increasingly addressed abortion in its Concluding Observations and General Recommendations, though the Convention text does not mention abortion explicitly. The Committee has:
- Called on states to decriminalise abortion (in Concluding Observations to Ireland, El Salvador, Honduras, and others)
- Recommended that states ensure access to safe abortion where it is legal
- Identified criminalisation of abortion as a barrier to women's right to health
- Recommended that states at minimum allow abortion in cases of rape, incest, and threat to health

This interpretation is contested by states that maintain restrictive abortion laws and argue that CEDAW does not create a right to abortion.

## CURRENT STATUS

- CEDAW remains the primary international legal instrument for women's rights and reproductive rights
- The CEDAW Committee continues to issue progressive interpretations on SRHR, but enforcement capacity is limited
- The interaction between CEDAW and national constitutional courts has produced important domestic jurisprudence on reproductive rights (Colombia, India, Kenya)
- UNFPA continues to use CEDAW as a normative framework for advocacy, programming, and technical support
- The Optional Protocol's individual complaint mechanism is generating a body of jurisprudence on reproductive rights
- Proposals for a new General Recommendation on the rights of indigenous women (with SRHR implications) are under discussion
