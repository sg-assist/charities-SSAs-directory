## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Arab States region presents UNFPA with a distinctive combination of challenges: protracted armed conflicts (Syria, Yemen, Sudan, Libya, Iraq, Gaza/Palestine), refugee crises of unprecedented scale (Jordan and Lebanon hosting millions of Syrian refugees), deeply conservative social norms around sexuality and gender, and significant economic inequality between resource-rich Gulf states and conflict-affected or low-income Arab countries. UNFPA's Arab States Regional Office (ASRO), based in Cairo, oversees programming in approximately 18 countries spanning North Africa and the Middle East.

The region's SRHR profile is highly heterogeneous. Gulf states and North African countries like Tunisia and Morocco have relatively low maternal mortality and moderate contraceptive prevalence. But Yemen (MMR approximately 183/100,000 and collapsing health system), Sudan (conflict since April 2023 devastating health infrastructure), and conflict-affected populations in Syria, Iraq, and Palestine face acute reproductive health emergencies. Child marriage remains prevalent in Yemen, Sudan, and among refugee populations. GBV — including conflict-related sexual violence — is pervasive but severely underreported due to stigma, legal barriers, and lack of services.

UNFPA's approach in the Arab States requires navigating cultural and political sensitivities around SRHR terminology and programming. The concept of "reproductive rights" is contested by some governments that frame SRHR within family and marriage contexts. Comprehensive sexuality education, abortion, and LGBTQ+ rights are particularly contentious. UNFPA has developed approaches that work within these constraints — emphasising maternal health, family protection, and community-accepted frameworks — while gradually expanding the space for rights-based programming.

## KEY FACTS

- **Regional population**: Approximately 440 million across the Arab States
- **MMR range**: From under 10/100,000 (UAE, Qatar, Bahrain) to 183/100,000 (Yemen) and higher in conflict-affected areas
- **Modern CPR**: Ranges from over 55% (Morocco, Algeria, Tunisia) to under 30% (Yemen, Sudan, Somalia)
- **Refugee populations**: Jordan hosts approximately 670,000 registered Syrian refugees; Lebanon approximately 1.5 million; Turkey approximately 3.6 million — all requiring SRHR services
- **Yemen crisis**: 80% of population requires humanitarian assistance; approximately 5 million women and girls of reproductive age need SRHR services; only 50% of health facilities functional
- **Sudan crisis (2023–)**: Conflict displaced over 8 million internally and 1.5+ million as refugees; health system collapse in conflict areas; massive GBV reports
- **Gaza/Palestine**: Conflict since October 2023 has devastated health infrastructure; estimated 50,000+ pregnant women in Gaza with extremely limited access to maternity care
- **Child marriage**: Yemen has no minimum marriage age; Sudan, Iraq, and Palestine have elevated rates, particularly among displaced populations
- **GBV**: Extremely high underreporting; UNFPA operates safe spaces and GBV case management in humanitarian settings across the region
- **Adolescent birth rate**: Regional average approximately 40/1,000, but higher in conflict zones and among displaced populations
- **CSE**: Comprehensive sexuality education is not implemented in most Arab States; UNFPA works through "life skills" and "family education" frameworks

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### The Arab Spring and Its Aftermath

The 2011 Arab Spring uprisings and their aftermath transformed the region's political and humanitarian landscape. The Syrian civil war (2011–), Libyan instability, Yemeni conflict (2014–), and political transitions in Tunisia, Egypt, and Sudan created both opportunities and crises for SRHR programming.

**Opportunities**: Post-revolution constitutions in Tunisia and Egypt initially expanded women's rights language. Youth demands for dignity, opportunity, and agency aligned with UNFPA's mandate on adolescent development.

**Crises**: Protracted conflicts in Syria, Yemen, Libya, and subsequently Sudan created massive humanitarian needs. UNFPA became a major humanitarian responder in the region, operating under the MISP and providing reproductive health services to millions of displaced persons.

### Cultural and Political Context

SRHR programming in the Arab States operates within specific constraints:
- **Islamic jurisprudence**: Interpretations of Islamic law inform government policies on marriage, family planning, abortion, and sexuality. These interpretations vary widely — from Tunisia's relatively liberal personal status code to Yemen's and Saudi Arabia's more restrictive frameworks.
- **Family-centred framing**: Many Arab governments frame reproductive health within the context of married couples and family welfare rather than individual rights. UNFPA has adapted its messaging to work within this frame while maintaining its mandate commitments.
- **SRHR terminology**: Terms like "sexual rights," "reproductive rights," and "bodily autonomy" are contested. UNFPA uses alternative framing (maternal health, family protection, women's health) to achieve programmatic objectives while avoiding political confrontation.

## DETAIL

### Humanitarian Response

The Arab States is UNFPA's second-largest humanitarian operational region (after sub-Saharan Africa). Major operations include:

**Syria and neighbouring countries**: Since 2011, UNFPA has provided reproductive health and GBV services in Syria and to Syrian refugees in Jordan, Lebanon, Turkey, Iraq, and Egypt. This is one of UNFPA's longest-running L3 humanitarian responses, with annual appeals exceeding USD 100 million.

**Yemen**: Often described as the world's worst humanitarian crisis. UNFPA supports reproductive health services in approximately 150 health facilities, provides midwifery training and deployment, manages GBV safe spaces, and distributes reproductive health kits. The challenge is immense: approximately 5 million women and girls need SRHR services, but access is constrained by conflict, fuel shortages, and health worker absence.

**Sudan**: The April 2023 conflict between the Sudanese Armed Forces and the Rapid Support Forces has created catastrophic humanitarian conditions. UNFPA has reported widespread conflict-related sexual violence, particularly in Darfur, and is providing GBV response services and reproductive health support to displaced populations.

**Gaza/Palestine**: The October 2023 escalation and subsequent military operations in Gaza have created extreme reproductive health needs among an estimated 50,000+ pregnant women with limited or no access to maternity care, blood supplies, caesarean section capacity, or neonatal care.

### Family Planning and Maternal Health

In non-conflict settings, UNFPA's Arab States programming addresses:
- Expanding family planning access in countries with moderate-to-low CPR (Egypt, Iraq, Sudan before the conflict)
- Improving quality of maternal health care (Egypt's focus on reducing C-section rates which exceed 50% in some areas; Morocco's maternal health programme)
- Addressing maternal mortality in low-income Arab states and conflict zones
- Youth-friendly SRHR services — a particularly challenging area given conservative norms around premarital sexuality

### GBV in the Arab States

GBV is pervasive but severely underreported across the region. Specific challenges include:
- Honour-based violence and killings
- Child marriage (particularly in Yemen, Iraq, and among displaced populations)
- Domestic violence (legal frameworks vary; some countries have enacted domestic violence laws — Jordan 2017, Lebanon 2014 — while others have not)
- Conflict-related sexual violence (Syria, Yemen, Sudan, Iraq)
- Limited safe reporting mechanisms and survivor services

UNFPA operates GBV safe spaces (Women and Girls Safe Spaces) across humanitarian operations in the region, providing psychosocial support, case management, referral, and economic empowerment. These spaces also serve as platforms for community engagement on GBV prevention.

## CURRENT STATUS

- The humanitarian burden in the Arab States continues to grow with the Sudan crisis, ongoing conflicts in Syria and Yemen, and the Gaza situation
- UNFPA's humanitarian spending in the region represents a large and growing share of its Arab States portfolio
- Development programming continues in stable countries (Morocco, Tunisia, Jordan, Egypt, Gulf states) but receives less attention and funding than humanitarian response
- Gulf state engagement as UNFPA donors is growing modestly; UAE, Saudi Arabia, and Qatar have contributed to UNFPA humanitarian operations
- The region remains the most challenging for rights-based SRHR programming due to political and cultural constraints
- Youth population growth (approximately 60% of the Arab population is under 30) creates both demographic pressure and potential for positive change
