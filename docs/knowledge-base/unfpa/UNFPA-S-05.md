## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Male engagement in sexual and reproductive health has evolved from a marginal concern to a recognised programme priority. Evidence consistently shows that men's attitudes, behaviours, and power within relationships significantly influence women's SRHR outcomes: partner opposition is a leading barrier to contraceptive use in many countries; male violence is the most direct threat to women's bodily autonomy; and men's involvement in maternal health (accompanying partners to ANC, birth preparedness) improves outcomes. Yet SRHR programming has historically been directed almost exclusively at women and girls, treating men as obstacles to be worked around rather than potential allies to be engaged.

UNFPA has adopted male engagement as a cross-cutting programme approach, drawing on evidence from the MenEngage Alliance, Promundo, and other organisations that have developed gender-transformative approaches working with men and boys. The evidence is strongest for: community-based group education programmes that address gender norms and masculinities (Promundo's Programme H and its adaptations); male involvement in ANC and birth preparedness; male engagement in GBV prevention; and youth-focused CSE that includes boys. The evidence is weaker for male contraceptive uptake (vasectomy and condom use remain underutilised) and for sustained norm change beyond programme participants.

A persistent critique is that male engagement programming risks re-centring men in spaces designed for women's empowerment. Well-designed programmes address this by focusing on shifting harmful masculinity norms rather than simply adding men to women's programmes, and by ensuring that women's voices, needs, and leadership remain central.

## KEY FACTS

- **Partner opposition to FP**: In many SSA and South Asian countries, 15–30% of women cite partner opposition as a reason for non-use of contraception (DHS data)
- **Male contraceptive methods**: Condoms and vasectomy account for approximately 21% of global contraceptive use; vasectomy is highly underutilised in LMICs (prevalence below 1% in most SSA and South Asian countries)
- **ANC accompaniment**: Studies show that male accompaniment to ANC visits is associated with higher facility delivery, better birth preparedness, and improved maternal nutrition
- **GBV perpetration**: Studies across multiple countries find that 25–50% of men report having perpetrated intimate partner violence; norms around masculinity are a key driver
- **Promundo's Programme H**: Evidence-based group education approach for young men; evaluated in multiple countries; shown to reduce reported violence perpetration and increase contraceptive use and equitable attitudes
- **MenEngage Alliance**: Global alliance of over 700 organisations working on engaging men and boys in gender equality; UNFPA is a partner
- **Male circumcision**: VMMC (voluntary medical male circumcision) for HIV prevention is one of the most successful male-targeted health interventions; UNFPA supports VMMC in high-HIV-prevalence countries
- **Fatherhood programmes**: Emerging evidence that active fatherhood engagement improves child health, maternal well-being, and relationship quality
- **Boys in CSE**: Including boys in comprehensive sexuality education is critical for changing gender norms; boys-only and mixed-gender CSE formats both have evidence
- **Emerging male contraception**: Research on hormonal male contraceptives (injectable, gel) continues; no new male method has been approved since the condom

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### From "Missing Men" to Male Engagement

The 1994 ICPD Programme of Action called for "shared responsibility of men" in reproductive health — a landmark recognition that SRHR is not exclusively a women's issue. However, progress has been slow. The 2012 UNFPA publication "Engaging Men and Boys in Gender Equality and Health" synthesised evidence and proposed a framework, but implementation at country level remained limited.

The field has evolved through several phases:
1. **Adding men** (1990s–2000s): Simply including men in FP counselling and maternal health services
2. **Gender-sensitive** (2000s–2010s): Recognising that masculinity norms affect SRHR outcomes
3. **Gender-transformative** (2010s–present): Actively working to transform harmful gender norms, power relations, and masculinities — not just including men but changing what masculinity means

### The Evidence Base

The strongest evidence comes from structured group education programmes (Promundo's Programme H/M/D series, Stepping Stones, One Man Can) that:
- Engage small groups of men in facilitated discussions about masculinity, power, violence, and relationships
- Run for 10–16 sessions over several months
- Use participatory methods (role-play, storytelling, critical reflection)
- Are complemented by community campaigns and institutional change

Evaluations (including RCTs in Brazil, India, Rwanda, and DRC) have found:
- Reduced reported IPV perpetration (20–50% reduction in some studies)
- Increased contraceptive use
- More equitable gender attitudes
- Improved relationship communication
- Effects that persist 12–24 months post-intervention (longer-term follow-up is limited)

## DETAIL

### UNFPA's Male Engagement Work

**Family planning**: UNFPA supports male engagement in FP through community dialogue, couples counselling, and promotion of male methods (condoms, vasectomy). Vasectomy promotion faces cultural resistance in many contexts (association with emasculation), but successful programmes in Rwanda and Brazil have increased uptake.

**Maternal health**: UNFPA promotes male involvement in ANC, birth preparedness, and postnatal care. Evidence from Sub-Saharan Africa shows that when men accompany partners to ANC, women are more likely to deliver in a facility and receive postnatal care. However, some facilities restrict male entry, and male accompaniment programmes must be designed to empower women rather than reinforce male control.

**GBV prevention**: UNFPA supports gender-transformative GBV prevention programmes targeting men and boys. These are among the most evaluated interventions in UNFPA's portfolio. The SASA! model (developed by Raising Voices in Uganda) engages community members including men in changing norms around violence — an RCT found 52% reduction in physical IPV in intervention communities.

**CSE for boys**: UNFPA's support for comprehensive sexuality education includes programming specifically for boys on consent, healthy relationships, gender equality, and sexual health. Evidence suggests that CSE that includes boys reduces sexual risk-taking and improves gender attitudes.

### Critiques and Tensions

**Re-centring men**: Feminist scholars and women's rights organisations have raised valid concerns that male engagement programming can divert attention and resources from women's empowerment. When "engaging men" becomes the priority, women's spaces for healing, organising, and leadership can be diminished.

**Instrumental framing**: Some male engagement programmes frame men's involvement instrumentally — "engage men to improve women's health outcomes" — rather than addressing men's own health needs and genuine interest in equitable relationships. This instrumental approach may be less sustainable.

**Male health needs**: Men face their own SRHR needs (STI screening, prostate health, mental health, sexual dysfunction) that are largely neglected in LMIC health systems. A genuine SRHR-for-all approach should address male health needs alongside women's health needs.

---

## CURRENT STATUS

- Male engagement is an established programme approach in UNFPA's portfolio, though implementation depth varies by country
- Evidence for gender-transformative approaches is growing but long-term impact data remains limited
- Vasectomy promotion is increasing but remains marginal in most LMIC contraceptive method mixes
- Research on new male contraceptive methods continues but no new products are near market
- The tension between male engagement and women's empowerment continues to shape programme design
