## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Fragile and conflict-affected states represent the most challenging operational environments for UNFPA and are where SRHR needs are greatest relative to available services. Approximately 25 per cent of UNFPA's total expenditure now goes to humanitarian response — a share that has grown substantially over the past decade as protracted crises in Syria, Yemen, DRC, South Sudan, Myanmar, Afghanistan, Sudan, Ukraine, and Gaza have expanded the humanitarian caseload. In these settings, UNFPA operates at the intersection of the humanitarian-development-peace nexus, providing both immediate life-saving SRHR and GBV services and longer-term health system strengthening.

UNFPA's humanitarian mandate is anchored in two roles: implementing the Minimum Initial Service Package (MISP) for reproductive health in crises, and leading the GBV Area of Responsibility (AoR) within the IASC Protection Cluster. These roles give UNFPA a defined and recognised position in the humanitarian architecture, though the organisation remains significantly smaller than humanitarian heavyweights like UNHCR, WFP, and UNICEF.

The fundamental challenge in fragile states is that humanitarian and development needs co-exist and compound each other. A country like DRC experiences acute conflict alongside chronic underdevelopment; Yemen's humanitarian crisis is layered on top of pre-existing SRHR deficits; Sudan's conflict has destroyed health infrastructure that was already inadequate. The nexus approach — programming that bridges emergency response and long-term system building — is conceptually sound but operationally difficult, requiring different funding streams, planning horizons, and partnerships that the aid system has not fully integrated.

## KEY FACTS

- **Humanitarian expenditure share**: Approximately 25% of UNFPA total expenditure — up from approximately 15% a decade ago
- **L3 emergencies (UNFPA's highest classification)**: Typically 5–8 concurrent L3 declarations, including Syria, Yemen, Ukraine, Sudan, Afghanistan, DRC, Myanmar, and others
- **Affected population**: Over 300 million people require humanitarian assistance globally (OCHA 2024); UNFPA targets women and girls of reproductive age — approximately 25% of crisis-affected populations
- **GBV in conflict**: An estimated 1 in 5 women in conflict and displacement settings has experienced sexual violence; actual rates likely higher due to severe underreporting
- **Maternal deaths in fragile states**: Countries classified as fragile by the OECD account for approximately 60% of global maternal deaths
- **MISP implementation**: UNFPA leads MISP implementation in humanitarian settings; coverage is improving but remains incomplete — many crises still lack full MISP deployment in early emergency phases
- **GBV sub-cluster leadership**: UNFPA leads or co-leads the GBV sub-cluster/AoR in the vast majority of humanitarian emergencies
- **Reproductive health kits**: UNFPA distributes pre-packaged RH kits (13 different kit types for different settings and needs) in emergencies; annual distribution exceeds 1 million kits in major crisis years
- **Women and Girls Safe Spaces**: UNFPA operates hundreds of safe spaces in humanitarian settings providing GBV services, psychosocial support, and SRHR information
- **CERF funding**: UNFPA receives allocations from the Central Emergency Response Fund — typically USD 30–50 million annually
- **Humanitarian staff**: UNFPA has scaled up humanitarian surge capacity including a standby partner roster and emergency response teams
- **Protracted crises**: Most of UNFPA's major humanitarian operations have lasted over 5 years, blurring the line between emergency response and long-term programming

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### UNFPA's Humanitarian Evolution

UNFPA was not originally designed as a humanitarian agency. Its mandate — population and development, reproductive health, gender equality — was oriented toward development programming. The humanitarian role grew incrementally:

**1990s**: UNFPA responded to the Rwandan genocide and Balkan wars, providing reproductive health supplies and GBV response. These experiences demonstrated the acute SRHR needs in emergencies and UNFPA's unique capacity to address them.

**2000s**: The MISP was standardised (originally developed in 1996 by the Inter-agency Working Group on Reproductive Health in Crises) and UNFPA took on an increasingly formal role. The 2005 Humanitarian Reform established the cluster system; UNFPA was designated to lead the GBV sub-cluster under the Protection Cluster.

**2010s**: The Syrian crisis (2011–), South Sudan civil war (2013–), Ebola (2014–2016), and Yemen conflict (2014–) dramatically expanded UNFPA's humanitarian portfolio. The 2018–2021 Strategic Plan explicitly integrated humanitarian response as a core function.

**2020s**: COVID-19, the Afghanistan Taliban takeover (2021), Ukraine invasion (2022), Sudan conflict (2023), and Gaza crisis (2023–) have further expanded humanitarian demands. UNFPA now operates in more concurrent emergencies than at any point in its history.

### The Nexus Approach

The humanitarian-development-peace (HDP) nexus, endorsed by the OECD DAC (2019) and the UN system, recognises that:
- Humanitarian and development needs are not sequential but simultaneous
- Short-term emergency response and long-term system building must be pursued concurrently
- Programming should address root causes of vulnerability, not just symptoms
- Different funding instruments (humanitarian appeals, development budgets, peacebuilding funds) need to be coordinated

For UNFPA, the nexus means: providing MISP services while simultaneously building midwifery capacity; operating GBV safe spaces while supporting national GBV legislation; distributing RH kits while strengthening pharmaceutical supply chains.

## DETAIL

### UNFPA's Humanitarian Operations

**Syria and the wider crisis (2011–present)**: UNFPA's longest-running L3 emergency. Operations span Syria, Turkey, Jordan, Lebanon, Iraq, and Egypt. Programming includes reproductive health services, GBV response (safe spaces, case management, psychosocial support), and support to health systems in host countries serving refugees.

**Yemen (2014–present)**: UNFPA supports approximately 150 health facilities, deploys midwives, manages GBV safe spaces, and distributes reproductive health kits. Operating under active conflict with limited access and a collapsing health system.

**DRC (ongoing)**: Eastern DRC faces chronic conflict with mass displacement and extreme levels of conflict-related sexual violence. UNFPA provides clinical management of rape, GBV case management, and reproductive health services in displacement camps and affected communities.

**Ukraine (2022–present)**: Europe's largest humanitarian crisis. UNFPA focuses on maternity care for displaced women, GBV response (including documentation of conflict-related sexual violence as potential war crimes), and mobile health services.

**Sudan (2023–present)**: The rapid onset of conflict between the SAF and RSF has created catastrophic conditions. UNFPA has reported widespread conflict-related sexual violence, particularly by RSF forces in Darfur.

**Afghanistan (2021–present)**: Under Taliban restrictions on women, UNFPA is one of the few international organisations maintaining SRHR service delivery through female health workers. Operating constraints are severe.

### Challenges

**Funding**: Humanitarian SRHR is underfunded relative to need. UNFPA's humanitarian appeals are rarely fully funded (typically 50–70% funded). Within broader humanitarian appeals, SRHR and GBV compete with food, shelter, and water for limited resources.

**Access**: In active conflict, reaching affected populations is dangerous and sometimes impossible. UNFPA staff and partners have been killed, injured, and detained in conflict zones.

**Coordination**: The cluster system requires extensive coordination with other UN agencies, NGOs, and government counterparts — time-intensive in contexts where rapid response is essential.

**Nexus in practice**: Despite the conceptual appeal, nexus programming remains difficult. Humanitarian and development budgets operate on different timelines, with different accountability requirements and donor expectations. A country office cannot easily merge a 12-month humanitarian appeal with a four-year development programme.

**Accountability to affected populations**: Ensuring that crisis-affected women and girls have voice in programme design and delivery remains a challenge, despite commitments to accountability.

## CURRENT STATUS

- UNFPA's humanitarian portfolio continues to grow as the number of concurrent crises remains historically high
- The organisation has invested in surge capacity, emergency preparedness, and partnership with standby partners (NRC, DRC, RedR)
- GBV sub-cluster leadership is well-established but increasingly demanding as crisis complexity grows
- Nexus programming is an aspiration being tested in several contexts (Syria, DRC, Afghanistan) with mixed results
- Humanitarian SRHR funding remains insufficient — UNFPA advocates for SRHR to be recognised as life-saving within humanitarian appeals
- Conflict-related sexual violence documentation and response is an area of growing UNFPA engagement, particularly in Sudan, Ukraine, and DRC
