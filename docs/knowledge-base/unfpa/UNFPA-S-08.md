## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The intersection of mental health and sexual and reproductive health is one of the most underprogrammed areas in UNFPA's mandate. The evidence linking the two is extensive: gender-based violence causes PTSD, depression, and anxiety; perinatal mental disorders (postpartum depression, anxiety, psychosis) affect 10–20 per cent of pregnant and postpartum women globally; child marriage, FGM, and denial of reproductive autonomy have documented mental health consequences; and adolescent SRHR challenges (unintended pregnancy, sexual violence, identity-related stigma) intersect with the adolescent mental health crisis.

Despite this evidence, SRHR and mental health services remain separated in most health systems. A woman who experiences intimate partner violence may receive medical care for injuries and be counselled on a safety plan, but is unlikely to be assessed for depression or offered psychotherapy. A postpartum woman struggling with depression is unlikely to have her contraceptive needs addressed at a mental health appointment. The separation reflects historical siloing of health disciplines and chronic underfunding of mental health in LMICs — where the ratio of mental health professionals per capita can be 100 times lower than in high-income countries.

UNFPA's engagement with mental health is primarily through its GBV response programming, where psychosocial support (PSS) is a core service component. In humanitarian settings, UNFPA provides mental health and psychosocial support (MHPSS) through safe spaces, case management, and referral. Beyond GBV, UNFPA's engagement with mental health — particularly perinatal mental health, adolescent mental health, and the mental health impacts of harmful practices — is limited.

## KEY FACTS

- **Perinatal depression**: Affects approximately 10–20% of pregnant and postpartum women globally; higher in LMICs (up to 25% in some studies); associated with preterm birth, low birth weight, impaired bonding, and infant developmental delays
- **GBV and mental health**: Women who experience IPV are approximately 3 times more likely to have depressive disorders and 4 times more likely to experience PTSD (WHO 2013)
- **Child marriage**: Documented associations with depression, anxiety, and lower psychological well-being; linked to social isolation, loss of education and peer networks, and early childbearing stress
- **FGM**: Mental health consequences include PTSD (up to 30% in some studies), anxiety, depression, and sexual dysfunction
- **Adolescent mental health**: Approximately 1 in 7 adolescents globally experiences a mental disorder (WHO 2021); intersection with SRHR through unintended pregnancy, sexual violence, and identity-related distress
- **Mental health treatment gap**: In LMICs, 75–85% of people with mental disorders receive no treatment (WHO Mental Health Atlas 2021)
- **Mental health workforce**: LMICs have on average 1.6 mental health workers per 100,000 population; high-income countries average over 60
- **WHO mhGAP**: Mental Health Gap Action Programme (2010, updated 2016) — framework for integrating mental health into primary care; includes perinatal depression and GBV-related disorders
- **MHPSS in humanitarian settings**: IASC Guidelines on MHPSS (2007) establish a multi-layered framework; UNFPA's GBV programming includes PSS as a core service
- **Suicide and SRHR**: Suicide is a leading cause of death among young women aged 15–19 in some contexts; links to unwanted pregnancy, forced marriage, and sexual violence

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### Why the Separation Exists

Mental health and SRHR have developed as separate fields with different institutional homes, funding streams, and professional identities. In most LMICs:
- Mental health is managed by a small psychiatric/psychological workforce, often concentrated in urban tertiary facilities
- SRHR is delivered through primary health care, maternal health services, and community-based programmes
- The two rarely interact at the service delivery level

The global mental health movement (spearheaded by the Lancet Commission on Global Mental Health, 2007 and 2018) has called for integration of mental health into primary care and task-shifting to non-specialist providers. This has direct relevance for SRHR: midwives, community health workers, and FP providers could be trained to identify and manage common mental disorders if given appropriate tools and support.

### UNFPA's Current Engagement

UNFPA's mental health work is concentrated in two areas:

**GBV response**: MHPSS is a core component of UNFPA's GBV case management. Women and Girls Safe Spaces provide psychosocial support (structured activities, peer support, individual counselling). UNFPA-supported GBV case managers are trained in psychological first aid and referral for more severe mental health needs. This is the most developed area of UNFPA-mental health intersection.

**Humanitarian response**: Under the IASC MHPSS framework, UNFPA contributes to the multi-layered response — particularly through safe spaces (Layer 3: focused non-specialised support) and community-based psychosocial activities (Layer 2: community and family support). UNFPA does not provide specialised mental health care (Layer 4), which is the domain of WHO and specialised mental health agencies.

## DETAIL

### Perinatal Mental Health

Perinatal depression and anxiety are the most common complications of pregnancy, yet they are routinely undetected and untreated in LMICs. The consequences are severe:
- For the mother: prolonged suffering, impaired functioning, risk of self-harm, difficulty bonding with the infant
- For the infant: preterm birth and low birth weight (associated with prenatal stress), impaired cognitive and emotional development, insecure attachment
- For the family: relationship strain, economic consequences, multigenerational effects

**Screening**: Simple screening tools (Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale, PHQ-9) can be administered by midwives during routine ANC and postnatal visits. Yet screening for perinatal depression is not standard practice in most LMIC SRHR programmes, including UNFPA-supported services.

**Treatment**: Evidence-based treatments include structured psychological interventions (cognitive behavioural therapy, interpersonal therapy) that can be delivered by trained non-specialist providers (task-shifting). The WHO Thinking Healthy Programme, designed for delivery by community health workers in LMICs, has been evaluated in RCTs in Pakistan and found effective.

**UNFPA's gap**: Perinatal mental health is not systematically addressed in UNFPA's maternal health programming. This represents a significant gap given UNFPA's reach in ANC and postnatal care.

### Adolescent Mental Health and SRHR

The convergence of adolescent SRHR and mental health is increasingly recognised:
- Unintended pregnancy in adolescence is associated with depression, anxiety, and school dropout
- Sexual violence against adolescents causes PTSD, depression, and suicidality
- LGBTQ+ adolescents face elevated mental health risks due to stigma and discrimination
- Child marriage and early pregnancy create social isolation and psychological distress
- CSE that includes emotional well-being and healthy relationships has mental health benefits

UNFPA's adolescent programming could integrate mental health screening, referral, and psychosocial support more systematically, but this requires training and tools that most country offices do not yet have.

### Harmful Practices and Mental Health

FGM and child marriage both have documented mental health consequences:
- **FGM**: Studies in the Gambia, Somalia, and Europe have found elevated PTSD (15–30%), depression, and anxiety among women who have undergone FGM. The experience of the procedure itself and its ongoing health consequences (pain, sexual dysfunction, obstetric complications) contribute to psychological distress.
- **Child marriage**: Loss of childhood, removal from education and peer networks, premature adult responsibilities, and often IPV create conditions for depression and anxiety. Studies in Bangladesh, Ethiopia, and India document these associations.

---

## CURRENT STATUS

- Mental health-SRHR integration is gaining policy attention but implementation remains minimal
- UNFPA's strongest engagement is through GBV psychosocial support in humanitarian settings
- Perinatal mental health screening and treatment is not yet integrated into UNFPA's maternal health programming at scale
- The WHO mhGAP framework provides tools for task-shifting that could be adapted for SRHR settings
- The 2026–2029 Strategic Plan offers an opportunity to strengthen mental health integration across UNFPA's programme areas
- Mental health workforce shortages in LMICs mean that integration must rely on task-shifting to existing SRHR providers
