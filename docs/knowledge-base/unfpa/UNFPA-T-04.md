## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

UNFPA is the world's largest multilateral procurer of contraceptives and reproductive health commodities, purchasing over USD 300 million worth of products annually for distribution in over 140 countries. The "last mile" — getting commodities from national warehouses to health facilities and ultimately to users — remains the most persistent challenge in reproductive health supply chains. UNFPA can deliver contraceptives to a country's capital efficiently, but whether those commodities reach a rural health post in northern Nigeria, a displacement camp in eastern DRC, or an outer island clinic in Vanuatu depends on national logistics systems that are frequently underfunded, understaffed, and poorly managed.

Supply chain innovations address this challenge through multiple approaches: digital logistics management information systems (LMIS) that provide real-time visibility on stock levels; drone delivery of health commodities to remote areas; improved forecasting using machine learning; temperature-controlled supply chain improvements for heat-sensitive products; social marketing and pharmacy-based distribution models that leverage private sector channels; and regional manufacturing partnerships that reduce dependence on long-distance international shipping.

This document examines the current state of reproductive health supply chains, specific innovations being deployed or piloted, evidence on their effectiveness, and the structural barriers that technology alone cannot overcome.

---

## KEY FACTS

- **UNFPA procurement volume**: Over USD 300 million annually in contraceptives and other reproductive health commodities; supplies reach over 140 countries
- **Products procured**: Male and female condoms, oral contraceptive pills, injectable contraceptives, implants, IUDs, emergency contraception, maternal health medicines (oxytocin, magnesium sulfate, misoprostol), and reproductive health kits for emergencies
- **Stockout rates**: Studies across SSA find that 30–50% of health facilities experience stockouts of at least one contraceptive method at any given time (PMA surveys)
- **CHANNEL system**: UNFPA's supply chain management platform tracking commodity shipments from procurement to country-level receipt
- **LMIS adoption**: Digital logistics management systems implemented in approximately 30 UNFPA-supported countries; transition from paper-based to digital inventory management
- **Drone delivery**: Pilot programmes in Ghana (Zipline), Rwanda (Zipline), and other countries deliver health commodities including contraceptives and blood products by drone
- **Forecasting accuracy**: Typical forecast accuracy for contraceptive demand is 60–80%; machine learning approaches have improved this in pilot settings
- **Cold chain challenge**: Oxytocin (essential for preventing postpartum haemorrhage) requires refrigeration; many rural facilities in LMICs lack reliable cold chain
- **Social marketing**: Organisations like DKT International, PSI, and Marie Stopes distribute contraceptives through commercial channels (pharmacies, shops) in over 60 countries
- **Regional manufacturing**: India produces approximately 40% of global generic pharmaceutical output; local manufacturing in Africa is growing but still represents a small share of total supply
- **RHSC (Reproductive Health Supplies Coalition)**: Multi-stakeholder partnership that UNFPA co-leads; coordinates supply chain actors across the reproductive health commodity space

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### The Supply Chain Architecture

Reproductive health commodity supply chains typically follow a multi-tier structure:

**International procurement** → UNFPA (and other buyers like USAID, DFID/FCDO, Global Fund) procure from manufacturers (primarily in India, Europe, and the US) through competitive bidding. UNFPA's procurement volume gives it negotiating leverage — contraceptive unit costs through UNFPA procurement are typically 20–40% below private market rates.

**International shipping** → Commodities are shipped to country-level central warehouses (usually government medical stores or dedicated logistics units).

**National distribution** → From central warehouses to regional/district warehouses. This step is usually government-managed and is where inefficiencies begin to accumulate.

**Last-mile delivery** → From district warehouses to health facilities. This is the weakest link. Distribution depends on government-owned vehicles (often insufficient), fuel budgets (often cut), and logistics staff (often undertrained).

**User access** → From health facility to user. Even when commodities are physically present, barriers include: provider bias (refusal to provide certain methods to young or unmarried women), user fees, limited counselling time, and social stigma.

## DETAIL

### Digital LMIS and Real-Time Visibility

The transition from paper-based to digital logistics management is one of the most impactful supply chain innovations. When health facility staff record stock levels digitally (via mobile phone, tablet, or computer), district and national managers can see in near-real-time which facilities are running low and need resupply.

Examples:
- **eLMIS in Tanzania**: Digitalised the pharmaceutical supply chain; UNFPA supported integration of contraceptive tracking. Reduced stockout rates at health facilities from approximately 45% to approximately 20% over three years.
- **DHIS2 Supply Chain module**: Integrates commodity data with health information systems, enabling facilities to report both service delivery and stock levels in one platform.
- **mSupply**: Open-source medical supply chain software used in several Pacific Island countries, adapted for reproductive health commodity tracking.

Challenges: Digital LMIS requires reliable internet connectivity, electricity for device charging, trained staff, and ongoing system maintenance — all of which are constraints in the most underserved areas.

### Drone Delivery

The most prominent drone delivery programmes in health commodities are operated by Zipline:
- **Rwanda (2016–)**: Zipline delivers blood products and some essential medicines by drone from distribution centres to health facilities. Delivery time reduced from hours by road to approximately 30 minutes by drone.
- **Ghana (2019–)**: Expanded to include contraceptives and other reproductive health commodities. UNFPA has partnered with Zipline to include RH commodities in drone delivery payloads.

Evidence: Drone delivery demonstrably reduces delivery time and can reach remote areas inaccessible by road (particularly during rainy seasons). However, cost per delivery is higher than traditional logistics for high-volume, non-urgent commodities. Drones are most cost-effective for emergency resupply, low-volume high-value products, and genuinely remote locations.

### Heat-Stable Formulations

Oxytocin — the gold standard treatment for postpartum haemorrhage (the leading cause of maternal death globally) — requires refrigeration. This is a critical problem because the health facilities most likely to manage deliveries in rural LMICs are those least likely to have functioning cold chains.

Innovations include:
- **Heat-stable carbetocin**: A room-temperature-stable alternative to oxytocin, WHO pre-qualified in 2019. UNFPA has begun procurement and distribution, though scale-up is still in progress.
- **Improved cold chain**: Solar-powered refrigerators and passive cooling devices designed for health facilities without reliable electricity.
- **Misoprostol as alternative**: Oral misoprostol does not require refrigeration and can be administered by lower-level health workers. It is less effective than injectable oxytocin but is critical where cold chain is unavailable.

### Social Marketing and Private Sector Distribution

In many LMICs, the private sector (pharmacies, drug shops, market vendors) is the primary source of contraceptives for a significant proportion of users. Social marketing organisations (DKT International, PSI, Marie Stopes) purchase contraceptives (often at subsidised prices through UNFPA or donor procurement) and distribute them through commercial channels at affordable prices.

This model reaches users who do not access public health facilities, expands method choice (condoms and pills are widely available through pharmacies; increasingly implants and injectables through trained pharmacists), and creates sustainable distribution channels that do not depend on government logistics.

## CURRENT STATUS

- Supply chain innovation is one of the most dynamic areas in UNFPA's operational work
- Digital LMIS adoption is expanding, with evidence of stockout reduction in implementing countries
- Drone delivery is scaling in a small number of countries but not yet transformative at global level
- Heat-stable carbetocin is a significant innovation for maternal health; procurement scale-up is underway
- African continental manufacturing capacity is a growing priority (African Medicines Agency, Partnerships for African Vaccine Manufacturing) though impact on RH commodities is not yet significant
- The RHSC continues to coordinate supply chain actors; the coalition's Visibility and Analytics Network (VAN) provides data on global commodity flows
