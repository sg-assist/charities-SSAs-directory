## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

An estimated 1.3 billion people globally — approximately 16 per cent of the world's population — live with some form of significant disability (WHO 2023). Persons with disabilities face systematic exclusion from sexual and reproductive health information and services, compounded by harmful stereotypes that deny their sexuality, autonomy, and reproductive capacity. The Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD, 2006) affirms the rights of persons with disabilities to the highest attainable standard of health including SRHR (Article 25), to found a family (Article 23), and to live free from exploitation, violence, and abuse (Article 16). Yet these rights remain largely unrealised.

Evidence consistently shows that persons with disabilities experience: 2–3 times higher rates of sexual violence than the general population; lower access to contraception and maternal health services; exclusion from comprehensive sexuality education; higher rates of forced sterilisation and forced contraception (particularly women with intellectual disabilities); and poorer maternal outcomes when pregnant. Health facilities are often physically inaccessible; health information is not available in accessible formats; health workers lack training on disability-inclusive care; and social stigma compounds all other barriers.

UNFPA has adopted disability inclusion as a cross-cutting priority, publishing guidelines on disability-inclusive SRHR programming and partnering with the International Disability Alliance (IDA) and other disability-led organisations. However, implementation lags behind policy: most UNFPA country programmes do not yet systematically include disability-specific SRHR interventions, data disaggregated by disability status is rarely collected, and the IRRF does not include disability-specific indicators for most outputs.

## KEY FACTS

- **Global disability prevalence**: Approximately 1.3 billion people (16% of global population) live with significant disability (WHO Global Report on Health Equity for Persons with Disabilities, 2023)
- **CRPD ratification**: 190 states parties as of 2025 — the most rapidly ratified human rights treaty in history
- **Sexual violence**: Persons with disabilities face 2–3 times higher risk of sexual violence than the general population; women with intellectual disabilities face up to 10 times higher risk
- **Forced sterilisation**: Documented in dozens of countries; particularly affects women and girls with intellectual and psychosocial disabilities; CRPD Committee has called for absolute prohibition
- **Contraceptive access**: Studies in LMICs find that women with disabilities are 2–3 times less likely to use modern contraception than women without disabilities
- **Maternal health**: Women with disabilities who become pregnant face higher rates of complications, partly due to later presentation for antenatal care, physical access barriers, and provider bias
- **CSE exclusion**: Most comprehensive sexuality education programmes are not accessible to learners with visual, hearing, intellectual, or physical disabilities; separate disability-specific CSE is rare
- **Health facility accessibility**: WHO estimates that less than 50% of health facilities in LMICs meet basic physical accessibility standards; SRHR-specific services may be even less accessible
- **Data gap**: Fewer than 20% of countries disaggregate SRHR indicators by disability status in national surveys (DHS, MICS)
- **Washington Group questions**: A standardised set of questions for identifying persons with disabilities in surveys; increasingly adopted in DHS and MICS but not yet universal
- **UNFPA disability strategy**: UNFPA adopted a disability inclusion strategy and guidelines for SRHR programming; implementation varies by country
- **IDA partnership**: UNFPA has a partnership with the International Disability Alliance for joint advocacy and programme development

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### The CRPD Framework

The Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (adopted 2006, entered into force 2008) represented a paradigm shift from a medical/charity model of disability to a human rights/social model. Under the social model, disability results from the interaction between persons with impairments and environmental/attitudinal barriers — it is society's responsibility to remove barriers, not disabled persons' responsibility to "overcome" their impairments.

Key CRPD provisions for SRHR:
- **Article 23 (Respect for home and family)**: States parties shall eliminate discrimination in marriage, family, parenthood, and relationships; persons with disabilities retain fertility on an equal basis with others (prohibiting forced sterilisation)
- **Article 25 (Health)**: Equal access to the same range, quality, and standard of health care as provided to other persons, including SRHR; health services shall be provided as close as possible to communities, including in rural areas
- **Article 16 (Freedom from exploitation, violence and abuse)**: Protection from all forms of violence, including sexual violence, with age- and gender-responsive measures

### Historical Context

The history of disability and reproductive rights includes profound abuses:
- **Eugenics era**: Forced sterilisation of persons with disabilities was legal in many countries (US, Sweden, Germany, Japan) through much of the 20th century. An estimated 60,000+ people were forcibly sterilised in the US alone under eugenics laws.
- **Institutionalisation**: Persons with disabilities in institutions (psychiatric hospitals, residential facilities) were routinely denied sexual autonomy, subjected to forced sterilisation, and exposed to sexual abuse with inadequate oversight.
- **Continuing violations**: Forced sterilisation of women and girls with intellectual disabilities continues to be documented in countries across all income levels. The CRPD Committee has issued numerous recommendations calling for its prohibition.

## DETAIL

### Barriers to SRHR for Persons with Disabilities

**Physical barriers**: Steps, narrow doorways, examination tables without adjustable height, delivery rooms not designed for wheelchair users, inaccessible toilets in health facilities. These barriers prevent persons with physical disabilities from accessing SRHR services.

**Communication barriers**: Health information in text format only (excluding persons with visual impairments); no sign language interpretation for deaf patients; complex medical language without plain language alternatives for persons with intellectual disabilities; lack of Braille or audio materials on contraceptive options.

**Attitudinal barriers**: Health workers who assume persons with disabilities are asexual, should not have children, cannot make informed decisions about contraception, or are not at risk of STIs. These attitudes lead to denial of services, provision of inappropriate care (e.g., automatic recommendation of long-acting or permanent contraception without discussing preferences), and failure to screen for GBV.

**Legal barriers**: Guardianship laws that allow others to make medical decisions for persons with intellectual or psychosocial disabilities, including decisions about contraception and sterilisation. The CRPD Committee's General Comment 1 on legal capacity asserts that all persons with disabilities have the right to make their own health decisions, with supported decision-making rather than substitute decision-making.

**Information barriers**: CSE programmes that are not adapted for learners with different disabilities. Sex education in mainstream schools is often inaccessible to students with disabilities who may be in separate classrooms or who require adapted materials.

### UNFPA's Disability-Inclusive SRHR Work

UNFPA has taken several steps toward disability inclusion:

1. **Guidelines**: Published "Women and Young Persons with Disabilities: Guidelines for Providing Rights-Based and Gender-Responsive Services to Address Gender-Based Violence and Sexual and Reproductive Health and Rights" (2018)
2. **IDA partnership**: Joint advocacy, programme development, and inclusion of disability-led organisations in UNFPA consultations
3. **Data improvement**: Supporting the inclusion of Washington Group questions in national surveys to enable disability-disaggregated SRHR data
4. **Accessible GBV services**: Ensuring that Women and Girls Safe Spaces in humanitarian settings are physically accessible and include staff trained in communicating with persons with disabilities
5. **CSE**: Working on accessible CSE materials, though coverage remains extremely limited

### Gaps in Implementation

Despite policy commitments, UNFPA's disability inclusion faces significant implementation gaps:
- Most country programmes do not have dedicated disability-inclusion budgets or staff
- The IRRF does not require disability-disaggregated reporting for most output indicators
- Humanitarian operations (where UNFPA operates at scale) rarely achieve full accessibility in safe spaces and health facilities
- Partnerships with disability-led organisations are growing but remain ad hoc in many countries
- Evidence on what works for disability-inclusive SRHR in LMIC settings is thin

## CURRENT STATUS

- UNFPA's disability inclusion work is growing but remains at an early stage of systematic implementation
- The Washington Group questions are increasingly adopted in national surveys, improving the data foundation
- The 2026–2029 Strategic Plan is expected to strengthen disability-inclusion commitments
- The CRPD Committee continues to issue recommendations on SRHR that reinforce the normative framework
- The humanitarian disability inclusion agenda (through the IASC Guidelines on Inclusion of Persons with Disabilities in Humanitarian Action, 2019) is pushing UNFPA to improve accessibility in emergency response
