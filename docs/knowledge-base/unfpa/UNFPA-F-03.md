## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

UNFPA is governed by an Executive Board that it shares with the United Nations Development Programme (UNDP) and the United Nations Office for Project Services (UNOPS). This shared board — formally the "Executive Board of UNDP, UNFPA and UNOPS" — comprises 36 member states elected by the Economic and Social Council (ECOSOC) on a rotating basis. The Board provides intergovernmental oversight of UNFPA's strategic direction, approves country programmes, endorses the strategic plan, reviews financial and programmatic performance, and holds the Executive Director accountable.

The governance arrangement is unusual in the multilateral system. Unlike the World Bank or IMF (where voting power is weighted by financial contribution), or the WHO (where all member states sit in the World Health Assembly), UNFPA's board is a small, rotating body that must balance the interests of donor and programme countries, navigate politically sensitive issues (reproductive rights, sexuality education, abortion), and oversee three distinct organisations in the same sessions. The Board meets three times per year in formal sessions, supplemented by informal consultations.

Key governance challenges include: the inherent tension between donors who fund UNFPA and programme countries where it operates; the political sensitivity of UNFPA's mandate (which periodically leads to contested board decisions); the limitations of a shared board that must divide attention among three entities; and the broader question of Executive Board effectiveness in an era when the real power increasingly lies in bilateral donor relationships and earmarked funding decisions made outside the boardroom.

---

## KEY FACTS

- **Board composition**: 36 members elected by ECOSOC — 8 from African states, 7 from Asia-Pacific, 4 from Eastern European, 5 from Latin American and Caribbean, 12 from Western European and Others Group (WEOG)
- **Term length**: Three years, with one-third rotating annually
- **Sessions**: Three formal sessions per year (January/February, May/June, August/September), plus informal and joint meetings
- **Shared governance**: Same 36 members oversee UNDP, UNFPA, and UNOPS in the same sessions — the only such shared arrangement in the UN system
- **Decision-making**: By consensus where possible; formal votes are rare and politically sensitive
- **Key board actions**: Approves UNFPA strategic plan (four-year cycle), approves country programme documents, adopts the integrated budget, reviews the annual report of the Executive Director
- **Executive Director**: Appointed by the UN Secretary-General in consultation with the Board; currently Dr. Natalia Kanem (since 2017, reappointed 2022)
- **Relationship to ECOSOC**: The Board reports to ECOSOC, which reports to the General Assembly — creating a three-tier governance chain
- **Observer participation**: Non-member states, NGOs, and other UN entities can attend as observers but cannot vote
- **Bureau**: Five officers (President and four Vice-Presidents) elected annually from different regional groups; the Bureau manages board proceedings and intersessional work

## BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

### Origins of the Governance Structure

When UNFPA was established in 1967 as the United Nations Fund for Population Activities, it operated under the authority of the UNDP Governing Council (UNFPA's original administrative home was within UNDP). In 1972, the General Assembly placed UNFPA under its authority, and in 1993, the General Assembly renamed the fund and established its current mandate. The shared Executive Board was created in 1994 as part of broader UN governance reforms (General Assembly resolution 48/162), which established separate executive boards for UNDP/UNFPA/UNOPS, UNICEF, and WFP.

The decision to keep UNDP and UNFPA under the same board reflected their historical administrative connection and a desire to limit the proliferation of governing bodies. However, the two organisations have very different mandates, operational models, and political dynamics. UNOPS was added later, further stretching the board's attention.

### How the Board Functions in Practice

**Formal sessions**: Three times per year, the Board convenes for one to two weeks. The January/February session typically addresses financial and budgetary matters. The May/June (annual) session is the most substantive, reviewing the Executive Director's annual report, programmatic performance, and strategic issues. The August/September (second regular) session focuses on country programmes, evaluation, and thematic issues.

**Country programme approval**: Each UNFPA country programme document (CPD) must be approved by the Executive Board. CPDs are presented in batches, with brief introductions and limited discussion time. In practice, most CPDs are approved with minimal scrutiny; substantive board engagement on individual country programmes is rare.

**Strategic plan**: The Board endorses UNFPA's four-year strategic plan after extensive informal consultations. The plan sets the results framework, resource allocation principles, and strategic priorities. Midterm reviews allow for adjustments.

**Informal consultations**: Much of the Board's substantive work occurs in informal settings — briefings, retreats, and bilateral meetings between the UNFPA secretariat and delegations. These are not formally recorded and lack the transparency of formal sessions.

## DETAIL

### Power Dynamics on the Board

The Board's 36 seats are distributed across regional groups, but power does not flow equally from each seat. In practice:

**Donor influence**: The 12 WEOG seats plus Japan (counted in Asia-Pacific) and occasionally South Korea represent UNFPA's primary funding sources. These delegations tend to drive discussions on results, accountability, efficiency, and strategic direction. Their leverage is financial — the implicit message is that funding depends on satisfactory performance and governance.

**Programme country voice**: African, Asia-Pacific, and Latin American delegations represent UNFPA's operational geography. These countries have a strong interest in maintaining and expanding UNFPA's country presence, ensuring needs-based resource allocation, and protecting UNFPA's programmatic mandate from political interference. Programme countries sometimes form coalitions to resist donor-driven reforms perceived as reducing country-level flexibility.

**Political blocs on SRHR**: The most politically charged board dynamics concern UNFPA's core mandate — sexual and reproductive health and rights. A loose coalition of conservative member states (often including the Holy See as observer, some OIC members, and the US under Republican administrations) periodically challenges language on reproductive rights, comprehensive sexuality education, and sexual orientation/gender identity. Progressive donor countries (Nordic states, Netherlands, Canada, New Zealand) defend and advance this language. Board decisions and strategic plan language often reflect carefully negotiated compromises.

**The US factor**: The United States, when it sits on the Board (WEOG rotation), exercises significant influence due to its overall UN funding leverage, even when it withholds UNFPA-specific contributions. Under Trump-era delegations, the US has introduced contentious amendments and opposed consensus on SRHR language.

### Accountability Mechanisms

The Board exercises accountability through several channels:

1. **Annual report review**: The Executive Director presents an annual report with performance data against the strategic plan's results framework. Board members can question performance, request additional information, and issue decision language requiring corrective action.

2. **Audit and oversight**: The UN Board of Auditors audits UNFPA annually. The Office of Internal Oversight Services (OIOS) and UNFPA's own internal audit function provide additional oversight. The Joint Inspection Unit (JIU) conducts system-wide reviews that cover UNFPA. Audit findings are presented to the Board.

3. **Evaluation**: UNFPA's independent Evaluation Office reports directly to the Board, not to UNFPA management. The Board reviews and discusses evaluation findings, management responses, and follow-up.

4. **MOPAN assessments**: The Multilateral Organisation Performance Assessment Network (comprising donor governments) periodically assesses UNFPA's institutional effectiveness, strategic management, and development results. MOPAN findings influence donor confidence and funding decisions.

5. **Financial reporting**: Detailed financial statements, including revenue, expenditure, and reserve levels, are reviewed by the Board's Advisory Committee on Administrative and Budgetary Questions (ACABQ).

### Limitations of the Current Governance Model

**Shared board attention deficit**: With three organisations (UNDP, UNFPA, UNOPS) competing for board time, UNFPA's issues receive limited attention. UNDP's larger budget and broader mandate typically dominates the agenda. UNFPA-specific strategic discussions may be compressed into brief segments.

**Limited technical depth**: Board members are diplomats in permanent missions, not SRHR specialists. Their engagement with UNFPA's technical work is necessarily superficial. Deep programmatic discussions occur more in bilateral donor consultations than in the boardroom.

**Consensus pressure**: The Board's preference for consensus means that decisions often reflect the lowest common denominator. Controversial issues (CSE, reproductive rights, LGBTQ+ inclusion) are resolved through ambiguous language that defers rather than resolves disagreements.

**Disconnect between governance and funding**: The Board approves strategic plans and country programmes, but the real resource allocation power lies with individual donor capitals that decide earmarking. A Board-approved strategic plan may be undermined by donor earmarking patterns that direct resources away from Board-endorsed priorities.

## CURRENT STATUS

- The Executive Board continues to meet three times annually, with sessions increasingly addressing humanitarian response alongside development programming
- Board reform discussions are ongoing within the broader UN reform agenda, including proposals for more effective oversight, reduced transaction costs, and better alignment between governance and funding
- The 2026–2029 Strategic Plan is expected to be presented for Board endorsement in 2025–2026, with significant informal consultations preceding formal approval
- Political tensions on SRHR language have intensified with shifts in US policy under the current administration
- Digital participation options introduced during COVID-19 have been partially maintained, improving accessibility for capital-based delegates
